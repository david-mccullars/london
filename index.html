Dogget

From ancient British fishermen to London watermen, this family bears the distinctive mark of webbed hands and white hair. Their line traces from Celtic fishermen through armourers and watermen to a museum curator in the late 20th century.

Ducket

The aristocratic branch of the Dogget family, from city alderman and powerful merchants to the Earls of St James.

Bull

Proud merchants and brewers, the Bulls built their fortune on the Thames. From Saxon warriors to aldermen to Victorian industrialists, this family's power lay in commerce and trade.

Silversleeves

Norman merchants and masters of debt and manipulation. This calculating family pursued power through cunning schemes - from wine trading to fraudulent alchemy to constructing the Tower of London.

Barnikel

Vikings who became fishmongers, the Barnikels dominated London's markets and waterways for generations. Their name echoes through guild halls and along the wharves of old London.

Carpenter

Master craftsmen and eventually devout Puritans, the Carpenters shaped both London's buildings and its soul. From the Globe Theatre to St. Paul's Cathedral, their hands carved wood while their hearts fought for religious reform.

Fleming

Merchants and bankers with Flemish roots, this family navigated London's commercial world with shrewd business sense, their fortunes tied to trade and finance.

Meredith

Welsh gentry who came to London seeking fortune and position, the Merediths moved between court, church, and commerce, their ambitions as varied as the city itself.

Penny

Huguenot refugees who became prosperous financiers, the Penny family represents London's evolving relationship with money, morality, and social change.
