Overview

Mary Anne is the youngest and prettiest of the four Dogget daughters, who marries Edward Bull of the Bull Brewery family. Vivacious and spirited, she briefly experiences a moment of profound romantic longing when she meets the young Colonel Meredith at her father's dinner party in 1851, but settles into a conventional Victorian marriage. Decades later, she re-encounters Meredith, now a distinguished colonel, sparking complex emotions before her daughter Violet ultimately marries him instead.

Physical Description

Mary Anne is described as pretty and slim with vivacious hazel eyes and a distinctive flash of white in her curly brown hair that gives her a distinguished appearance. She possesses an elegance and style that her plainer sisters lack. By middle age (1889-1890), she remains attractive enough to turn heads, though she feels the passage of time keenly when she re-encounters Meredith.

Character and Personality

Mary Anne is spirited, adventurous, and willing to challenge convention within the boundaries permitted to a woman of her class. Unlike her more conservative sisters, she possesses intellectual curiosity and sensitivity to refinement. At the 1851 dinner party, she immediately recognizes young Meredith's superior breeding and intelligence, feeling contempt for her husband and family's dismissive treatment of him. She has enough self-awareness to realize that, in marrying the prosperous Bull Brewery, she has chosen security and comfort over intellectual companionship.

She is capable of both defiance (insisting on her balloon ride, defending Meredith at dinner) and self-control (suppressing her romantic longings, accepting the limitations of her marriage). Though she experiences moments of boredom and frustration with her husband's world, she understands that "marriage was about not screaming" and finds consolation in its material rewards. Her relationship with her daughter Violet is complicated; she discourages Violet's educational ambitions to protect the family's social position, even while she herself once craved wider horizons.

Life and Events

1851: The Great Exhibition Year

Mary Anne's twenty-fourth year marks a pivotal moment in her life. Already married to Edward Bull, heir to the Bull Brewery, she makes a daring balloon ascent on her birthday, departing from Vauxhall Gardens and landing on Blackheath. The adventure, which terrifies and exhilarates her in equal measure, is expensive but affordable given Edward's prospects.

At her father Silas's birthday dinner that evening, she serves as hostess and is seated next to young Meredith, just out of Eton and about to travel to India. Their conversation reveals her intellectual curiosity – she is fascinated by his plans to study Hindu religion and temples. When her husband and family dismiss Meredith's interests as nonsense, she defends him, recognizing his superior breeding and intelligence. She realizes with some bitterness that she has married "the Bull Brewery, with all its virtues, strengths, and limitations."

At the meal's conclusion, she squeezes Meredith's hand – a tiny gesture of connection and rebellion that her sister Charlotte notices. Though she wishes she "could come with you" to India, she knows she will never see him again.

1851-1889: Marriage and Family

Mary Anne's marriage to Edward proves prosperous beyond expectations. Edward makes his fortune supplying drink to the British army during the Crimean War in the 1850s, becoming very rich. The family eventually moves into her father's big house on Blackheath, and Edward later purchases the Bocton estate in Kent, establishing himself as a country gentleman.

She bears six children: her eldest son Richard (who attends Oxford), two other sons, and three daughters including Violet. While she finds comfort in her children and the material blessings of her marriage, there are moments "when she felt like screaming with boredom and frustration." She develops a love of reading, building collections of Dickens and Thackeray, and discovering Tennyson's poetry at Hatchards bookshop in Piccadilly, which becomes her sanctuary.

When Violet wants to attend university, Mary Anne discourages her, not because she disapproves of education – she herself would have welcomed it – but because it might make the rising family "look too middle class." She tells Violet that "men don't like women to be too intelligent" and advises her to conceal it if she is. This hypocrisy later angers Violet, who tells her mother: "You've never wanted anything else in your life" – a charge that, while unfair, Mary Anne cannot entirely refute.

1889-1890: Re-encountering Meredith

At age sixty-two, Mary Anne's life is disrupted when she encounters Meredith at Hatchards. Now a distinguished colonel recently returned from thirty years abroad, he is devastatingly handsome with graying temples, a long silky moustache, and the bearing of a man who has "seen much of the world in all kinds of weather." To her horror, she finds herself blushing like a girl.

He invites her to tea at his exotic house in Holland Park, and she brings Violet as chaperone. The visit overwhelms her – surrounded by his collections from India and Arabia, served by his turbaned Sikh servant, she experiences "that same giddying sensation that she had once felt when the balloon rushed her up into the sky." By the time he serves walnut cake, she knows "she wanted to leave her house, her difficult daughter and her husband and to come to rest, for as long as he would have her, in Meredith's arms."

But this romantic fantasy is shattered when Violet announces she is going to marry Meredith. The girl has been seeing him secretly for weeks, attending his poetry reading and meeting him at "lectures." Mary Anne is devastated – "But you should be marrying a young man!" She protests about his age, about the impropriety, but Violet counters: "We are passionately in love." Most cruelly, Violet adds: "One thing is certain anyway. You can't have him."

The marriage proceeds despite Mary Anne's protests. Edward, pragmatic as always, is "quite glad to have me off his hands," as Violet correctly predicts.

1907-1908: Death and Aftermath

Mary Anne dies in 1907, having lived to see her daughter married to the man she herself had loved. By the time of the 1908 Suffragette chapter, Violet (now widowed after Meredith's death in 1905) visits her father at Bocton and reflects that "Mary Anne had died the previous year."

Relationships

Edward Bull

Mary Anne's relationship with her husband is characterized by affection, respect, and quiet resignation. Edward is prosperous, hearty, and competent in his world of brewing and business, but intellectually limited. She recognizes early that he is "made of coarse cloth" compared to someone like Meredith, yet she finds ways to appreciate him. He gives her the balloon ride she requested, provides handsomely for her, and fathers her children. If "sometimes she might have wished for something more – that his friends' senses of humour were a little lighter, that at least one of them had read a book – she kept it to herself." She considers her thirty years of marriage "not so bad," understanding that "the rewards of marriage – the comfort, the children – had been blessings indeed."

Colonel Meredith

Meredith represents everything Mary Anne's life lacks: intellectual companionship, cultural sophistication, adventure, and romance. Their initial meeting in 1851 plants a seed of unfulfilled longing that lies dormant for nearly forty years. When they meet again, she is immediately overwhelmed by attraction so powerful it leaves her feeling faint. She knows the fantasy is impossible – she is married, he is a gentleman – but for a brief moment she allows herself to imagine abandoning everything for him. The cruelest irony is that her daughter, recognizing her mother's attraction, claims Meredith for herself.

Violet Bull

The relationship between mother and daughter is complex and marked by mutual disappointment. Mary Anne loves Violet but cannot understand or support her desire for university education, seeing it as a threat to the family's social position. She tells Violet that life "isn't the way you think it should be" and that "the sooner you realize it the better" – advice reflecting her own resignation. Violet, in turn, sees her mother as complicit in patriarchal limitations and holds her in contempt for being "perfectly happy doing whatever papa says." Their only neutral ground is their weekly visits to Hatchards bookshop. The final betrayal comes when Violet marries Meredith, with full awareness of her mother's feelings – though whether this is cruelty or simply youth's ruthless claim on life remains ambiguous.

Silas Dogget (The Guv'nor)

As the youngest and prettiest daughter, Mary Anne occupies a special place in her father's affections. He indulges her balloon ride, finding it amusing rather than scandalous, and as Harriet observes: "He always did let you get away with anything, Mary Anne." This favoritism likely contributed to her sense that she might have some agency in her life, even as she ultimately conformed to expectations.

Historical Context

Mary Anne's life spans a remarkable period of British prosperity and social change. Born during the reign of George IV, she comes of age in the optimistic early years of Victoria's reign, marries during the Great Exhibition of 1851 – the apex of British imperial confidence – and lives through the entire Victorian era into the Edwardian period. Her father's transformation from dredger to wealthy gentleman, and her own marriage into brewing wealth, exemplifies the social mobility possible in Victorian commercial Britain.

She belongs to that particular generation of Victorian women who received some education, developed cultural interests (her love of Dickens, Thackeray, and Tennyson), yet remained firmly constrained by convention. She can ride in a balloon, attend bookshops, and hold opinions, but she cannot study at university, pursue a career, or leave her marriage. The tension between her intelligence and her limitations shapes her life and, problematically, her relationship with her daughter.

Themes and Significance

Mary Anne embodies the conflict between individual desire and social expectation that characterized women's lives in Victorian Britain. Her story explores the price of security and respectability: material comfort purchased with the sacrifice of intellectual companionship and romantic fulfillment. Unlike the Suffragettes of her daughter's generation, Mary Anne belongs to a cohort that accepted these limitations as inevitable.

Her physical characteristic – the white flash in her hair – links her to the Dogget family line while also marking her as distinctive. This hereditary trait, passed to her daughter Violet, symbolizes both family continuity and individual distinction.

The balloon ride that opens her story is symbolically rich: a moment of exhilarating freedom and terror, floating above the constraints of ordinary life, offering a panoramic view unavailable to those on the ground. Yet the balloon must come down, and Mary Anne spends the rest of her life on solid ground, looking up at possibilities that recede into the distance.

Most poignantly, Mary Anne represents the generational transmission of limitation and longing. She discourages Violet's ambitions because she has internalized her own constraints, yet her thwarted desires have shaped her daughter's rebellion. When Violet marries Meredith, it is simultaneously a betrayal and a fulfillment – the daughter claiming the life the mother could only imagine.
