Overview

Herbert Fleming is the older brother of Percy Fleming, a cheerful and theatrical clerk who works in the City of London. Known for his clowning and music-hall performances, Herbert provides comic relief and emotional support to his more serious brother while building a respectable suburban life in Crystal Palace with his wife Maisie. During World War II, he serves as an Auxiliary Fire Service volunteer alongside Percy.

Physical Description

No detailed physical description is provided in the text.

Character and Personality

Herbert is characterized by his theatrical, outgoing personality. He enjoys clowning and performing, striking melodramatic attitudes reminiscent of music hall entertainment. Unlike his quieter brother Percy, Herbert is naturally sociable and can easily strike up conversations with strangers. He loves the raffish, theatrical atmosphere of Soho where he grew up, drawn to its entertainment and lively character.

Despite his theatrical nature, Herbert is fundamentally caring and devoted to family. He is deeply concerned about his younger brother's wellbeing, encouraging Percy to move to Crystal Palace for better air quality and persistently trying to arrange his marriage prospects. He demonstrates genuine affection for Percy, telling him "I don't see enough of you, Percy, and you're all that's left of the family now" after their parents' deaths.

Herbert has a gift for entertaining. His music-hall performances at the piano are described as popular and morale-boosting, particularly during the war years. He can bash out songs like "Nellie Dean" and recreate entire music-hall performances for his wife Maisie's enjoyment. Admiral Barnikel himself singles out Herbert's piano playing as "the best I've heard in any station."

Life and Events

Early Life and Family (c. 1875-1900)

Herbert was born in 1875, the son of a tobacconist in Soho, just east of Regent Street. He grew up in the area during a period of transformation when the Metropolitan Board of Works built Charing Cross Road and Shaftesbury Avenue, the latter becoming lined with theatres. Herbert always loved the raffish, theatrical atmosphere of Soho, in contrast to his brother Percy who was drawn to the more sedate world of Mayfair tailoring.

After a "brief flirtation with the theatre," Herbert settled into work as a clerk. By the old century's end, both his parents had died, leaving him and Percy as the only remaining members of their immediate family.

Marriage and Suburban Life (1908)

By 1908, Herbert was married to Maisie and living in Crystal Palace, a desirable suburban area on the ridge south of London with excellent air and magnificent views. He commuted daily into the City, taking the train from the nearby station at Cannon Street. The couple lived in a nice little villa with two storeys over a half-basement, featuring a small lawn with a privet hedge.

Herbert and Maisie prided themselves on being "very jolly." They went to a music hall once a month without fail, and Maisie would get "the whole performance back from Herbert the next evening." Maisie participated in a drama society and had a "lovely singing voice." Their favourite summer activity was cycling together on Sunday afternoons, with Maisie recommending the pastime enthusiastically to others.

Tower Bridge Incident (1908)

Herbert's theatrical personality was on full display when he helped convince Percy to move to Crystal Palace. After meeting Percy on a Saturday in the City, Herbert playfully stood in the middle of Tower Bridge striking melodramatic attitudes and calling out: "Decide, Percy! I shall stand here even if the bridge opens up until you do!" and "Oh, Percy, you will kill me!"

His music-hall style performance attracted a small crowd, including Jenny Ducket, whom he immediately enlisted as a witness to Percy's commitment. Herbert approached her directly, announcing that Percy was "very respectable you know, and in need of a wife" before insisting she shake Percy's hand to seal the agreement. His bold intervention inadvertently introduced Percy to his future wife.

Percy's Courtship (1908-1912)

Herbert strongly encouraged Percy's relationship with Jenny. When Percy hesitated about proposing, Herbert advised him. He and Maisie hosted the crucial Sunday lunch where Percy brought Jenny to meet the family, though the meeting did not go as smoothly as Herbert might have hoped due to Maisie's social anxieties about Jenny's servant background.

World War I Era (1914-1918)

Herbert and Maisie maintained their suburban life through the Great War years. After the war ended, they continued their involvement with Maisie's drama club, working on Christmas pantomimes together.

World War II and the Blitz (1940)

At age 65, Herbert volunteered for the Auxiliary Fire Service alongside Percy, working nights at a small Fire Brigade substation near Crystal Palace. Though really belonging to the Home Guard given their age, Herbert's piano performances were considered so good for morale that the station officer kept both brothers on.

Herbert's musical talents proved valuable during the war. He set up an old upright piano at the schoolhouse substation and played "all the favourite music-hall songs" for the crews. When Admiral Sir William Barnikel made an unannounced inspection, he heard Herbert play "Nellie Dean" and declared it "the best I've heard in any station," ordering Herbert to tune the piano properly.

The night of a major raid on the City, Herbert was at the substation when the station officer asked Percy to fill in for a sick crew member at the Bull Brewery fire. It was the last time Herbert saw his wife Maisie alive.

Maisie's Death (1940)

During the same night that Percy fought the brewery fire, Maisie left their house after one in the morning, unable to sleep, intending to walk to Jenny's house nearby. She paused at the crest of the ridge to watch dogfights between British fighters and German Messerschmitts over Crystal Palace. Caught up in the excitement of watching the aerial combat, she waved her arms and shouted encouragement to the British pilots. She never noticed the hail of shrapnel from anti-aircraft fire that struck the back of her head, killing her instantly.

When Percy returned exhausted from the brewery fire at nine o'clock the following morning, Herbert and Jenny decided not to tell him immediately about Maisie's death. "He's been out all night and he'll feel he's got to do something. Let him sleep," Herbert insisted. The brothers only shared their grief that evening.

Relationships

Percy Fleming

Herbert's relationship with his younger brother is characterized by affection, concern, and persistent encouragement. After their parents' death, Herbert actively works to remain close to Percy, urging him to move to Crystal Palace where they can see each other more often and where the better air will help Percy's chronic cough.

Herbert demonstrates a protective, almost parental care for Percy. He lobbies persistently for Percy to leave Tom Brown's employment and set up independently as a tailor, pressing his case during their Saturday walk from the City to Tower Bridge. His theatrical performance on the bridge, while embarrassing Percy, ultimately succeeds in extracting the commitment Herbert seeks.

The brothers remain close throughout their lives, working together during the Blitz as AFS volunteers in their sixties. After Maisie's death, Herbert shows concern for Percy's wellbeing by insisting he rest before hearing the tragic news.

Maisie Fleming

Herbert and Maisie shared a marriage built on middle-class respectability and entertainment. They enjoyed music halls together, with Herbert recreating the performances for her afterward. They participated in Maisie's drama society, and Herbert appreciated her "lovely singing voice." They had no children but maintained a comfortable, sociable suburban existence for over thirty years until her death in the Blitz.

Jenny Fleming

Herbert's relationship with his sister-in-law began with his impulsive introduction of her to Percy on Tower Bridge. While Maisie and Jenny never became close, Herbert and Jenny maintained a friendly connection. After Maisie's death, Jenny was present with Herbert when Percy returned from firefighting, and together they decided how to break the news to Percy.

Historical Context

Herbert represents the emergence of the London suburban clerk class in the late Victorian and Edwardian periods. The expansion of railways and the growth of banking, insurance companies, and imperial administration created an army of clerks who commuted from suburbs like Crystal Palace into the City for work. These men, whose parents or grandparents had been shopkeepers or craftsmen, put on suits and took trains to offices, establishing a new middle-class identity.

His life in Crystal Palace exemplifies the suburban ideal of the period: clean air away from London smog, respectable housing with small gardens, and daily rail commutes. The area represented social mobility and a step up from the cramped quarters of central London.

During World War II, Herbert's service in the Auxiliary Fire Service at age 65 reflects the desperate need for volunteers during the Blitz. His musical talents, which had provided entertainment in peacetime, became a tool for maintaining morale during the nightly terror of German bombing raids. His loss of Maisie to shrapnel represents one of the random tragedies that struck countless London families during the war.

Themes and Significance

Herbert embodies the importance of joy and entertainment in sustaining people through difficult times. His theatrical nature and music-hall performances provide comic relief and emotional support both in peace and war. His piano playing at the AFS station serves a vital function in maintaining morale during the Blitz, recognized by Admiral Barnikel himself.

His character illustrates the strong bonds of family loyalty that persist across generations of the Fleming line. Herbert's determination to keep his family close and support his brother's happiness drives much of his action in the narrative. Even after both parents are dead, Herbert works actively to prevent Percy from becoming isolated.

The contrast between Herbert's outgoing theatrical personality and Percy's quieter, more serious nature demonstrates how siblings can complement each other. Herbert provides the energy and initiative that helps Percy overcome his natural reticence, particularly in matters of romance.

Herbert's suburban life represents the aspirations of London's emerging clerical middle class: respectability, homeownership, cultural participation through drama clubs and music halls, and escape from the crowded, polluted center of London. His marriage to Maisie, while childless, represents a stable middle-class partnership built on shared social activities and mutual support.

The tragedy of Maisie's death from anti-aircraft shrapnel, rather than from German bombs, illustrates the random and indiscriminate nature of war's destruction. Her death while watching dogfights in fascination reminds us that civilians on the home front faced dangers from both enemy action and friendly fire, and that death could come at any moment from any direction during the Blitz.
