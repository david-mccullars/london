Overview

Dionysius Silversleeves is a twenty-nine-year-old clerk at the royal Mint in the Tower of London during the reign of Edward I. A descendant of the once-prominent Silversleeves family, he is a peculiar and somewhat grotesque figure whose aggressive good humor masks a lifestyle devoted entirely to pursuing prostitutes. Disfigured by severe acne and shunned by respectable women, Dionysius frequents every brothel in London with relentless enthusiasm. His minor but pivotal role in the story of Joan Fleming and Martin Fleming demonstrates both his reckless exuberance and his complete lack of social awareness.

Physical Description

Dionysius is twenty-nine years old with a distinctive and unfortunate appearance. He has dark hair, a long nose, and a thin body. His most notable feature is his severe acne – fiery red pimples that cover his neck, forehead, shoulders, chin, and especially his long nose, which glistens with them after drinking. His cheeks are red, and his eyes are oddly bright. He cheerfully attributes his condition to the "humours" in his body being "hot and dry, like fire." Despite his parents' assurances in his youth, the condition has never improved, persisting through the decades.

Character and Personality

Dionysius possesses an aggressive, relentless good humor that serves as both his defining trait and his defense mechanism. He is remarkably cheerful despite his physical disfigurement and social rejection, approaching life with a combination of confidence and obliviousness. His personality is characterized by:

Exuberant Energy: He throws himself into life with gusto, whether teasing lions at the Tower menagerie or pursuing women in brothels. His cheerfulness seems genuine rather than compensatory.

Social Obliviousness: Dionysius displays a remarkable lack of awareness about how his actions affect others. When he dramatically announces at the hanging that he had slept with Joan, he does so thinking he will be "talked about all over London," completely failing to grasp the harm he is causing.

Relentless Pursuit: In his quest for sexual gratification, he is tireless and systematic, visiting every brothel in London regularly. The prostitutes remark that he turns up "like a bad penny."

Contentment: Unlike many characters driven by ambition or burdened by responsibility, Dionysius is genuinely happy with his lot. He has no family obligations (his two elder brothers carry on the family line), a job he loves, and the freedom to pursue his pleasures.

Playful Aggression: His nightly ritual of teasing the lions at the Tower reveals a peculiar mix of bravado and childish delight. He enjoys provoking the dangerous animals, squealing with pleasure when they become enraged.

Life and Events

1295: The Dog's Head Incident

On a November evening in 1295, Dionysius receives word of a virgin at the Dog's Head brothel on Bankside – a rare opportunity in his habitual rounds of London's stew-houses. After finishing his work at the Mint and performing his usual ritual of taunting the lions in the Tower menagerie, he heads to Bankside with eager anticipation.

At the Dog's Head, he encounters Joan, the young woman who has entered the brothel as part of an elaborate plan to save her condemned lover Martin Fleming from hanging. The Dogget sisters have orchestrated an evening of food and wine to delay the proceedings. Dionysius, never one to refuse hospitality, settles in for a night of eating, drinking, and waiting.

Joan herself eventually appears and joins the party, sitting in his lap and encouraging him to drink more. The evening becomes increasingly convivial, with Dionysius growing happier than he has ever been in his life – not just because he will have a "fresh, clean woman," but because she seems actually to like him, an entirely novel experience.

When they finally ascend to Joan's attic room in the darkness of the cold November night, Dionysius is extremely drunk. The east wind has picked up, the lantern has gone out, and in the pitch-black staircase, the Dogget sisters execute their plan. In the darkness and confusion, one of the sisters pulls him into her room instead, while Joan hides in a corner. The drunken Dionysius, unable to see in the darkness, believes he is with the virgin and completes the act before falling asleep.

The Newgate Hanging

The following morning, Dionysius happens to be walking to Westminster to watch the gathering for Parliament when he notices a crowd heading to Newgate for a hanging. Curious, he follows and arrives just in time to witness an argument at the gallows.

Joan's father is protesting that his daughter is respectable, that she only left home the day before, and that she is a virgin. The crowd laughs derisively. When Joan claims to be a whore so that she can legally claim Martin Fleming and save him from hanging, her father desperately tries to maintain that she is a virgin.

Seeing his opportunity for drama and attention, Dionysius bursts forward from the crowd. "It's true, sirs," he calls out to the justice and sheriff. "I'm Dionysius Silversleeves, of the Mint." He announces that Joan is indeed a whore, that he had her the previous night, and points out William Bull, cheerfully adding "And so did he!"

He beams at the crowd, delighted with his dramatic intervention, thinking "They'll be talking about me all over London." He is completely oblivious to the horror and distress on Joan's face, or the pain his announcement causes Martin Fleming who must watch from the tumbrel. His testimony, though unintentionally, actually helps Joan's plan succeed by confirming her status as a prostitute, allowing her to claim Martin as her husband and save him from execution.

Consequences

After Christmas of that year, Dionysius begins experiencing a burning sensation in his private parts. It turns out that the woman he slept with in the darkness was Margery Dogget, not Joan, and Margery had been infected with the "burning sickness" – a form of syphilis. The disease, which Margery had been trying to conceal from the brothel's regular inspections, is now Dionysius's problem.

This bitter irony completes his story: in seeking a virgin, he contracts venereal disease; in seeking glory and attention at the gallows, he unwittingly helps Joan's scheme succeed while humiliating himself.

Relationships

The Prostitutes of London

Dionysius is well known in every brothel in London – on Bankside, in Cock's Lane near St Bartholomew's, and even in the unlicensed establishments in Gropeleg Lane off West Cheap. His relationship with these women is purely transactional, yet he approaches them with genuine enthusiasm rather than shame or guilt. The prostitutes tolerate him as a regular customer, though his frequent visits earn him the sardonic nickname of appearing "like a bad penny."

Margery Dogget

Though he never realizes it, the woman he actually sleeps with during the virgin incident is Margery Dogget. Their encounter in the darkness while he is drunk and she is desperately trying to protect her sister's secret leads to him contracting her venereal disease. The irony is complete: in seeking fresh, clean companionship, he encounters infection.

Joan Fleming and Martin Fleming

Dionysius's relationship with Joan is entirely one-sided. He believes he has bedded her and thinks she liked him (a rare and wonderful experience for him). In reality, Joan has never touched him and views him only as an obstacle to overcome in her plan. His testimony at the gallows, meant to bring himself attention and glory, inadvertently aids her scheme while deeply wounding both her and Martin, who must believe (at least temporarily) that she has been defiled.

Historical Context

The Royal Mint at the Tower

Dionysius works at a significant historical moment for English coinage. King Edward I, having found the previous reign's coinage debased, is determined to establish a currency that will enhance England's trade and reputation throughout Europe. The Mint has recently been moved from its ancient quarters below St Paul's to the Tower of London, where it operates as the most important of the kingdom's six mints.

The Mint in 1295 is a busy, impressive operation. The process involves assaying coins through careful weighing and melting with molten lead to test their silver content; maintaining huge vats of molten metal; creating blank coins in moulds; and striking them with dies in a single clean hammer blow. The coins produced include farthings, silver pennies, and the newest addition – the rare and heavy fourpenny piece called the groat. For someone who loves money like Dionysius, it is an ideal workplace.

The Silversleeves Family Decline

Dionysius represents the final stage of his family's long decline from prominence. The Silversleeves dynasty, once powerful and wealthy wine merchants, has diminished considerably over the generations. His two elder brothers maintain the family with "more than enough money" to care for their widowed mother, but they have abandoned the wine business entirely and are "far less rich than a few generations ago."

This decline parallels the broader changes in London society during the thirteenth century, where old patrician families like the Bulls found themselves challenged by rising merchants, immigrants, and craftsmen. However, unlike William Bull who bitterly resents these changes, Dionysius seems entirely unbothered by his family's reduced status. As a younger son with no family responsibilities, he is free to live for his own pleasures.

Brothels and the Church

Dionysius's regular patronage of London's brothels takes place within a well-regulated system. The Bankside stew-houses are owned and operated by the Bishop of Winchester, who rules them as part of his Liberty of the Clink. In the reign of Henry II, comprehensive regulations were drawn up – possibly with the assistance of Thomas Becket – for running these establishments "to the honour of God and according to the laudable customs and regulations of the land."

The bishop's bailiff conducts monthly inspections of all the prostitutes, checking for disease and maintaining standards. Any woman found infected is expelled from the Liberty. This system, which most Londoners agree is advantageous because Church-run brothels are better regulated, provides the context for Dionysius's pursuits. The expulsion of the Jewish community in 1290, which included the Jewish doctor who had known how to treat venereal disease with mercury, makes Dionysius's eventual infection more serious.

When Parliament sits at Westminster, King Edward orders the Bankside brothels officially closed, recognizing that "prostitutes and politicians are inevitably drawn together" and wishing to prevent trouble. This closure affects Dionysius's regular schedule and adds to his eagerness to visit the Dog's Head when he hears about the virgin.

Themes and Significance

The Grotesque and the Marginal

Dionysius embodies the grotesque figure who exists on the margins of respectable society yet moves through it with surprising freedom. His severe acne, his shameless pursuit of prostitutes, his playful torment of caged lions – all mark him as an outsider. Yet he holds a responsible position at the royal Mint and moves between the Tower, the brothels, and the city without apparent social consequences. He represents those who, either by choice or necessity, live beyond conventional boundaries.

Oblivious Harm

His character illustrates how harm can be done through sheer obliviousness rather than malice. When he dramatically announces at the gallows that he slept with Joan, he genuinely believes he is performing a public service by testifying to the truth, while simultaneously seeking attention and glory for himself. He has no understanding that he is devastating Joan, Martin, and Joan's father. His self-centered worldview cannot comprehend the pain he causes others. This makes him both comic and tragic – a buffoon whose buffoonery has real consequences.

Cosmic Justice and Irony

The story of Dionysius embodies a kind of cosmic irony. He seeks a virgin for a rare, clean experience and instead contracts venereal disease. He seeks glory and public attention at the gallows and instead becomes a figure of ridicule (once the truth emerges about what actually happened that night). He believes Joan actually liked him – perhaps his happiest moment in life – when in fact she was executing an elaborate deception in which he was merely an obstacle. The universe seems to conspire to mock his pretensions while using him as an unwitting tool in Joan's salvation plan.

Freedom from Family Obligation

Unlike many characters in the novel who are burdened by family duty, inheritance concerns, or dynastic ambitions, Dionysius is refreshingly free. His elder brothers carry the family line; his mother is cared for; he has no wife to answer to. This freedom could have been used for many purposes, but Dionysius devotes it entirely to sensual pleasure. He represents a particular kind of medieval bachelor – one who has enough money and status to avoid poverty, enough freedom to pursue his desires, but not enough consequence to matter in the larger social or political sphere. His contentment in this role is both admirable and pathetic.

The Comedy of Physical Desire

Dionysius's character contributes to Rutherford's exploration of how physical desire drives human behavior across the centuries. His relentless pursuit of prostitutes, his delight at the prospect of a virgin, his happiness when a woman seems to like him – these are timeless human desires, made simultaneously comic and poignant by his grotesque appearance and social ineptitude. The elaborate deception played upon him by the Dogget sisters, swapping one woman for another in the darkness, becomes a farce that depends entirely on his drunkenness and desperate desire.

Key Quotes About Dionysius Silversleeves

"Dionysius Silversleeves was twenty-nine. His hair was dark, his nose long, his body thin; his cheek was red, his eyes were oddly bright, and he had acne. The fiery pimples were everywhere: on his neck, on his forehead, on his shoulders, around his chin, and all over his long nose which, after he had been drinking, glistened with them."
"It was because of the heat, the noise, the constant business, or the fact that it was money, which he so loved, that was being made, Dionysius Silversleeves counted himself lucky in his work and a happy man."
"There was not a stew-house on Bankside, nor in Cock's Lane, that did not know him. Even in a little alley off the West Cheap, known as Gropeleg Lane, where some unlicensed prostitutes resided, he would turn up every month or so, 'like a bad penny,' the women would remark."
