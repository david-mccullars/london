Overview

Sister Dogget is a nun and the sister of the waterman Dan Dogget, appearing briefly but significantly during the turbulent period of Henry VIII's dissolution of the monasteries. A seller of religious images, she represents both the traditional Catholic faith and the self-protective pragmatism of ordinary Londoners caught up in the religious upheavals of the 1530s. Her unwillingness to care for her troublesome father Will Dogget reveals a certain hardness alongside her piety.

Physical Description

No physical description provided in the text.

Character and Personality

Sister Dogget displays a character marked by "aggressive piety" combined with practical self-interest. Her profession as a seller of religious images suggests both religious devotion and commercial acumen, making her living from the Catholic faith's emphasis on sacred imagery and the veneration of saints.

Her religiosity appears performative and protective—when called to the Charterhouse monastery to deal with her drunken father's disgrace, she swears "by all the saints whose images she sold" that she can do nothing for him. This invocation of her wares in her oath suggests both genuine religious feeling and a keen awareness of the commercial value of appearing pious.

Her refusal to take in her father demonstrates a firmness that borders on hardness. Unlike her brother Dan, who has repeatedly tried to accommodate their troublesome parent, Sister Dogget simply will not have him. She places the burden squarely on Dan, telling him firmly: "It's up to you, Dan." Her religious vocation appears to provide her with both justification and protection from family obligations.

The text's description of her piety as "aggressive" suggests a defensive, performative quality to her religious expression—particularly significant during the dangerous 1530s when the old Catholic practices were coming under threat from royal reformers.

Life and Events

1533-1535: The Father's Disgrace

Sister Dogget enters the narrative when her father Will Dogget, who had been placed in the Charterhouse monastery by Dan to keep him out of trouble, commits a spectacular drunken escapade. The old man goes to local taverns, gets thoroughly drunk, and upon finding the monastery gates closed, attempts to break them down. When finally admitted, he sits under a nut tree in the yard and sings bawdy waterman's songs never before heard within the Charterhouse walls.

When the monks summon the family to deal with the scandal, Sister Dogget appears alongside her brother Dan before the sub-prior. She offers pious platitudes—"Lord have mercy on his soul, poor old man"—but immediately makes clear she will not take responsibility for him. She had already sworn to the monks "by all the saints whose images she sold" that she could do nothing for her father, using her religious profession both as an oath and as an excuse.

Faced with the crisis, she firmly places the burden on Dan: "It's up to you, Dan." Her refusal is absolute, leaving Dan in an impossible situation since he has no room for the old man either. This forces Dan to rely on the prior's mercy, with Will being allowed to remain at the Charterhouse only on condition that he never leaves the gates again.

Relationships

Dan Dogget

Sister Dogget's relationship with her younger brother is marked by her willingness to let him bear all the family's burdens. While Dan is "devoted to his tubby wife Margaret and their brood of happy children" and "kind to his sister's family," Sister Dogget offers no reciprocal support. When Dan tries to find a place for their father, having failed himself, he "had tried his sister, but she wouldn't take him."

At the Charterhouse, she reinforces this refusal publicly and firmly. The relationship appears one-sided, with Dan extending kindness to her family while she refuses to help with their shared father. Her religious vocation may provide her with justification for this refusal, though the text suggests it is more a matter of unwillingness than genuine inability.

Will Dogget

Sister Dogget's relationship with her father is characterized by complete rejection. Despite Will's advanced age and need for family care, she refuses to take him in under any circumstances. Her pious expressions—"Lord have mercy on his soul, poor old man"—ring hollow given her absolute unwillingness to help.

Will himself seems aware of his daughter's hardness. When he tells Dan "your sister won't have me," there is a note of resignation, as though her refusal is no surprise to anyone. Unlike Dan, who struggles with genuine conflict between duty and impossibility, Sister Dogget simply draws a firm line and refuses to cross it.

Historical Context

Sister Dogget's profession as a seller of religious images places her at a particularly vulnerable point in England's religious history. The 1530s saw Henry VIII's break with Rome, the assertion of royal supremacy over the English church, and the beginning of attacks on traditional Catholic practices.

The veneration of saints through their images was one of the practices that Protestant reformers particularly despised, seeing it as idolatry. The dissolution of the monasteries, which forms a crucial backdrop to this chapter, would soon be followed by widespread iconoclasm—the deliberate destruction of religious images in churches. Sister Dogget's livelihood depends on a form of devotion that is about to be violently suppressed.

Her "aggressive piety" and her swearing "by all the saints whose images she sold" can be understood as defensive gestures in an increasingly dangerous religious climate. For someone whose income depends on traditional Catholic devotion, appearing extremely pious might be both a sincere expression of faith and a form of self-protection.

The Charterhouse monastery where she and Dan must deal with their father is itself doomed. The chapter depicts the martyrdom of the Charterhouse prior and monks who refuse Henry VIII's Supremacy oath. Sister Dogget, like most ordinary Londoners, watches these great events unfold while trying to preserve her own position and livelihood.

Her religious vocation as a nun, combined with her commercial activity as an image seller, represents a traditional Catholic world that is rapidly disappearing. Within a few years, her profession would likely become impossible as reformers stripped the images from churches and banned their sale.

Themes and Significance

Sister Dogget represents several important themes in the novel's portrayal of the English Reformation's impact on ordinary people:

Religious Commerce and Survival: Her profession selling religious images embodies the commercialization of medieval piety—faith as both genuine devotion and economic opportunity. Her invocation of "all the saints whose images she sold" collapses the spiritual and commercial into a single gesture, suggesting how thoroughly religion permeated every aspect of life, including making a living.

Pragmatism Over Charity: Despite her religious profession, Sister Dogget shows none of the Christian charity one might expect from a nun. Her absolute refusal to help her father, while expecting her brother to shoulder the burden, reveals the gap between religious appearance and Christian practice. She is one of many characters in the chapter who must navigate between religious ideals and practical survival.

The Gendered Division of Family Obligation: The contrast between Dan's repeated efforts to care for his father and sister's family, and Sister Dogget's flat refusal, illuminates different expectations and possibilities for men and women. Her religious vocation provides her with a socially acceptable reason to refuse family obligations that her brother cannot escape.

The Old Order Under Threat: As a nun and seller of saint images, Sister Dogget represents the traditional Catholic world that the Reformation will sweep away. Her aggressive piety suggests an awareness, perhaps unconscious, that her entire way of life is threatened. Within the novel's pattern of religious change, she is on the losing side, though her fate remains unknown.

Key Quotes About Sister Dogget

"The old man would have been ejected that morning if his daughter had not sworn by all the saints whose images she sold that she could do nothing for him."

This description encapsulates Sister Dogget's character—the commercialization of her piety (swearing by the very images she sells) and her firm refusal to help her father despite her religious vocation.

"'Lord have mercy on his soul, poor old man,' his sister offered, with aggressive piety. 'It's up to you, Dan,' she added firmly."

The juxtaposition of pious platitudes with the firm refusal to help reveals the performative nature of her religiosity and her determination to make Dan bear the family burden.
