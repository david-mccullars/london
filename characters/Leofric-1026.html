Overview

Leofric is a prosperous Saxon merchant of London whose cautious nature and strong sense of honor define both his greatest strengths and his deepest conflicts. A descendant of the ancient trader Cerdic, he has built a successful business importing French wine and exporting English wool, though his operations are precariously balanced on the edge of financial ruin. His struggles during the Norman Conquest of 1066 force him to make painful choices between his word of honor, his daughter's happiness, and his family's survival, ultimately revealing the moral compromises required in times of political upheaval.

Physical Description

Leofric is described as a well-set man of about forty years in 1066, with thinning hair compensated for by a rich golden beard. He has a broad chest, a broad Germanic face, and an air of cheerful, sturdy self-control. His hard blue eyes can spot a short measure of goods at a hundred paces. He suffers from a painful back injury due to a fall from his horse, though he is proud that only those closest to him know of his frequent suffering. His green cloak is trimmed with red squirrel fur, marking his status as a wealthy merchant.

Character and Personality

Leofric embodies the Saxon virtue of keeping one's word to an almost obsessive degree. His reputation rests on the fact that no man has ever known him to break his word, and this becomes both his defining virtue and his greatest burden during the crisis of the Conquest. He is extremely cautious – some find this a virtue, others a fault – and approaches business and personal matters with careful calculation.

Despite his prudence in business, Leofric has overextended himself financially, showing a certain recklessness beneath his careful exterior. He genuinely loves his daughter Hilda and struggles deeply with the prospect of using her as a bargaining chip to save his estates, revealing a capacity for moral anguish unusual in his pragmatic world.

He is fundamentally a man of simple moral principles: for him, as for his Saxon ancestors, a thing is either right or wrong, and that should be the end of it. When faced with complex moral dilemmas that don't fit this framework, he experiences profound internal conflict. His common sense and practical nature ultimately override his emotional attachments, making him willing to accept hard political realities like William's conquest when resistance becomes futile.

Life and Events

Early Life and Business (before 1066)

Leofric descends from Cerdic, who once shipped slaves from the ancient trading post at the Aldwych. Like his ancestor, Leofric has built his fortune through trade. He operates two main lines of business: importing French wine (primarily through the merchant Becket in the Norman city of Caen) and selling English wool for export to the clothmakers of Flanders. His operations have grown too large, however, and small price fluctuations in wine or wool can critically affect his fortunes.

He lives in a spacious hall on the West Cheap, halfway along on the southern side, beside a tiny Saxon church dedicated to St Mary. A lane leads down to an old well beside his handsome homestead, which is graced with a heavy hanging sign depicting a bull. People often refer to him as "Leofric, who dwells at the Bull."

His family maintains the old Bocton estate in Kent, as many successful London merchants keep such estates. The revenues from this land are currently the only thing keeping his business afloat after he lost a cargo of wool at sea and had to borrow from Hrothgar Barnikel to cover the loss. He also owes money to Becket in Caen for his last shipment of wine.

January 1066: The Succession Crisis

On January 6, 1066, Leofric attends the funeral of King Edward the Confessor at Westminster Abbey with his close friend Barnikel. Despite his financial troubles, he maintains his status among London's merchant elite. Both he and Barnikel desire an English king and are united in their opposition to William of Normandy's claim to the throne.

When the Witan chooses Harold II as king, Leofric feels hopeful. However, his relief is marred by a disturbing message he received from Silversleeves, suggesting the Norman merchant knows about his financial troubles and wants to offer help.

The Terrible Dilemma

Leofric faces an agonizing choice regarding his daughter Hilda, who is betrothed to Barnikel's son. His debts to both Barnikel and Becket threaten his survival, and if England is attacked and Harold loses, his estates will likely be confiscated. With his finances on a knife's edge and his young son Edward only ten years old (too young to marry well and protect himself), Leofric contemplates the unthinkable: breaking his word to Barnikel and marrying Hilda to Silversleeves's family instead.

When he broaches the subject with Hilda, asking if she would help if needed, her gentle obedient response – "I will do whatever you wish, Father, if you are in need of help" – fills him with a sinking heart. Despite his moral conflict, he cannot bring himself to betray Barnikel. Yet a small voice inside cautions him: "You never know what may happen."

The Invasion and Battle of Hastings

As the invasion threat grows throughout 1066, Leofric watches the preparations with increasing anxiety. When the English fleet disperses in September for harvest, he knows the kingdom lies open to invaders. He stands with Barnikel at Billingsgate, watching the last vessels tie up, aware of the coming disaster.

When news arrives that the Norwegians have attacked in the north and King Harold has marched to meet them, followed by William's landing in the south, the city arms itself frantically. Barnikel storms into the armoury daily with fresh demands.

At a crucial council meeting, Barnikel urges immediate attack, crying "Strike now!" But Leofric is more cautious, advising: "The army's exhausted after the march south. Our London contingent is brave, but it's no good pretending they're a match for trained mercenaries. However, if we burn all the crops between here and the coast and destroy his transport, we can starve them. Then we should kill them all."

Unable to fight himself due to his injured back, Leofric watches as Barnikel and his son march out with King Harold's army on October 11. He remains in London, anxiously awaiting news.

After Hastings: The Fateful Decision

When news arrives that Harold has been killed at Hastings on October 14, Leofric makes his way to Silversleeves's stone hall below St Paul's. There, in an impressive display of wealth – fine Norman gowns, green German glass windows, beeswax candles, and multiple course changes of the tablecloth – Silversleeves makes his offer: Henri Silversleeves wishes to marry Hilda, with no dowry required. More astonishingly, Silversleeves will take over Leofric's debts to both Barnikel and Becket, freeing him entirely.

"But why?" Leofric asks simply. "All for love," Silversleeves replies softly.

Though profoundly relieved when he learns it is Henri (not the brutal Ralph) who wants Hilda, Leofric still cannot bring himself to betray Barnikel. He considers the offer weakly, saying only "I will consider the matter," while a truly base thought crosses his mind for the first time: if by chance Barnikel or his son were killed in battle, he would be free of his promise and the family fortune saved.

December 1066: The Betrayal

As William approaches London in December, Leofric recognizes the political reality with grim common sense. The Norman circles the city, burning farms, and Leofric notes: "A few more days and he'll come to our land." When William offers terms preserving London's ancient rights and privileges, Leofric publicly states at the Folkmoot beside St Paul's: "We should accept."

Barnikel, furious at what he sees as capitulation, bellows at Leofric before half of London: "Traitor!" He adds, so all can hear: "As for your daughter, keep her. My son will marry no traitor's child. Do you hear?"

Leofric hears. Though sad about the broken friendship, he supposes in the circumstances he should thank his stars. "As you wish," he replies, and walks away.

Three days later, Leofric accepts Silversleeves's offer, and Hilda is betrothed to Henri. The sole condition is that Leofric must henceforth import his wines through Silversleeves and cease doing business with Becket of Caen – a small price for his freedom from debt and the preservation of his family's future.

When Barnikel learns of the betrothal, his rage is legendary, causing massive destruction in his hall. Though Leofric had technically been released from his promise by Barnikel's own words, the Dane realizes that Leofric knew he didn't really mean it – and had proceeded anyway with the marriage to Silversleeves.

Later Years (1067-1083)

Despite his fears, Leofric loses the Bocton estate in Kent after the Conquest, just as he had predicted. However, Silversleeves's intervention helps cushion the blow. By 1083, Leofric is still active in business and maintains his hall by the sign of the bull on the West Cheap.

After a fire along the West Cheap some years earlier had destroyed several properties (though sparing Leofric's house), the little Saxon church of St Mary next to his hall is being rebuilt in stone – a fine new building that becomes one of the few stone churches in the area. Leofric watches its progress daily with satisfaction.

He remains on civil, if not warm, terms with his daughter Hilda, who by then is married to Henri Silversleeves. When she visits him during an illness in the mid-1080s, he treats her with paternal concern, though their relationship bears the weight of the choices he made during the Conquest.

Relationships

Hrothgar Barnikel

Leofric's friendship with the giant Danish merchant Hrothgar Barnikel is deep and longstanding. It is said that nothing can shake their friendship, though events prove this optimism false. Despite owing Barnikel money – a debt that would trouble most friendships – their bond remains strong, reinforced by the betrothal of Leofric's daughter to Barnikel's son.

The two men are described as united by a common desire for an English king, representing the successful merger of Saxon and Danish cultures in London. However, their friendship cannot survive the political crisis of 1066. Barnikel's temperament is bold and aggressive, while Leofric's is cautious and pragmatic. When Leofric publicly supports accepting William's terms, Barnikel denounces him as a traitor and breaks off his son's betrothal to Hilda. Though this technically releases Leofric from his promise, Leofric's subsequent alliance with Silversleeves – within days – destroys any possibility of reconciliation. The friendship, which seemed unshakeable, proves that "enmity lasts. Friendship is less certain."

Silversleeves

Leofric's relationship with Silversleeves begins with wariness and ends in an alliance born of necessity. As a Saxon merchant dealing with a Norman one, Leofric is initially cautious. He knows Silversleeves is wealthy and well-connected, but doesn't fully appreciate the extent of his power until he visits his stone hall.

Silversleeves has clearly been studying Leofric's financial situation and orchestrates events with cold precision. The Norman's offer – taking over all debts, requiring no dowry, and asking only that Leofric switch his wine importing to Silversleeves's business – is so generous that it reveals a larger game. Leofric never fully understands that he is merely a pawn in Silversleeves's long campaign against his rival Becket of Caen.

Despite benefiting from the arrangement, Leofric enters it with "some regret" at ceasing business with Becket. The relationship remains professional rather than warm, though Leofric comes to have "a fine position" through his daughter's marriage into the Silversleeves family.

Hilda

Leofric genuinely loves his daughter Hilda and thinks of himself as a good father. Her gentle simplicity and calm innocence charm him, though he wonders if there is fire within her. When he contemplates using her marriage as a bargaining chip, he feels profound guilt, thinking: "How innocent she looks. I have always thought of myself as a decent man. A man of his word. A good father. How, then, could I betray her like this?"

Her obedient response to his tentative request for help – agreeing without protest to do whatever he wishes – fills him with sorrow rather than relief. He initially decides he cannot go through with betraying Barnikel, showing that his love for her has moral weight.

However, when circumstances provide him an opening (Barnikel's public renunciation of the betrothal), he seizes it without consulting Hilda about her feelings regarding Henri Silversleeves. His relationship with his daughter survives into her married years, though it must carry the weight of his choices. When he is ill in 1083, she comes to stay with him, suggesting they have maintained a functional if not deeply close relationship.

Historical Context

Leofric represents the prosperous Anglo-Saxon merchant class on the eve of the Norman Conquest. His ancestry traces back through generations of Saxon traders, embodying the continuity of English commercial life from Roman times through the Anglo-Saxon period. His comfortable integration with Danish merchants like Barnikel illustrates the successful cultural merger that occurred in London after the Danish settlements and the reign of King Canute.

His business operations – importing wine from Normandy and exporting wool to Flanders – show London's position as an international trading hub connected to the broader European economy. His financial troubles reflect the risks inherent in medieval commerce, where a single lost cargo could threaten even a wealthy merchant's survival.

The Norman Conquest devastates men like Leofric. Though he survives by accepting reality and making necessary compromises, he loses his ancestral estate in Kent, and his world of Anglo-Saxon merchant aristocracy is permanently altered. His choice to ally with a Norman family through marriage represents the pragmatic accommodation that many English families made to survive the Conquest, though at the cost of old friendships and promises.

Themes and Significance

Leofric embodies the moral costs of political upheaval and survival. His story explores how ordinary men of principle are forced to compromise their values when caught in the machinery of historical change. His greatest virtue – his word of honor – becomes his greatest burden when circumstances make keeping his promise potentially ruinous.

The novel uses Leofric to examine the difference between rigid moral codes and situational ethics. His belief that things are simply "right or wrong" serves him well in normal times but proves inadequate for the moral complexities of conquest and survival. His internal struggle reveals that even fundamentally decent men can rationalize betrayal when their family's survival is at stake.

His broken friendship with Barnikel illustrates the Norman Conquest's destruction of social bonds. Two men who seemed inseparable, united by a daughter's betrothal and years of trust, are torn apart by differing responses to political crisis. The fact that Leofric technically receives permission to break his promise (through Barnikel's angry words) yet still feels like a betrayer highlights the distinction between legal release and moral obligation.

Leofric also represents the practical survivors of conquest – those who accept reality, make accommodations, and preserve what they can. Unlike Barnikel's futile rage or romantic resistance, Leofric's pragmatism allows him and his family to continue, albeit diminished. The novel suggests that history is shaped not only by heroes and villains but by cautious, troubled men like Leofric who make the best of bad situations.

His manipulation by Silversleeves demonstrates how crises create opportunities for the ruthless and clever to advance their own schemes while appearing to help. Leofric never realizes he is merely a tool in Silversleeves's revenge against Becket, showing how ordinary people become pawns in games they don't fully understand.
