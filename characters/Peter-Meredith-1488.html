Overview

Father Peter Meredith was a beloved parish priest who became a Carthusian monk and died a martyr's death during Henry VIII's break with Rome. The eldest of the Meredith family and half-brother to Susan and Thomas, Peter served as the family's moral conscience. His unwavering Catholic faith and ultimate sacrifice to save his brother-in-law Rowland Bull exemplified the terrible choices faced by devout Catholics during the English Reformation. Through an extraordinary deception orchestrated in his final days, Peter gave his life so that Rowland could live, with Rowland assuming Peter's identity after his death.

Physical Description

Peter was a man of good height, balding and pleasantly stout by his forties. His comfortable, solid appearance made him a reassuring presence to his parishioners. He bore a superficial physical resemblance to his brother-in-law Rowland Bull, both men sharing a stout build and prematurely balding heads – a resemblance that would prove crucial in the events of 1535.

Character and Personality

Peter was the embodiment of solid, principled Catholic faith. Described as comfortable and dependable, he served as the family conscience after their father died young. His personality combined genuine warmth with unwavering moral conviction. As a parish priest, he was known for his bonhomie with the women of his parish (who trusted his celibacy), his ability to drink companionably with the men while maintaining dignity, and his compassionate practice of holding dying parishioners' hands until they passed.

He was a clever man who, but for "a streak of laziness in his youth," might have been a fine scholar. Yet this lack of worldly ambition suited his calling perfectly. His sermons were simple, his conversation matter-of-fact. He was not a man for theological complexity or political maneuvering – he was a shepherd to his flock.

However, beneath this comfortable exterior lay an absolute moral clarity. When challenged about Henry VIII's claim to supremacy over the Church, Peter did not hesitate: "Either something is right, or it's wrong." He could not be swayed by political expediency or threats. Yet this firmness was balanced by pastoral wisdom – he advised laymen like Rowland to leave martyrdom to the monks, recognizing that family men had different obligations.

In his final days, Peter faced a profound spiritual crisis. His plan to save Rowland by feigning apostasy required him to appear to betray everything he believed in before his brother monks. The text notes that as a priest, "Father Peter Meredith faced a terrible dilemma: he did not know if he was doing right or wrong... he himself, now, was in danger of losing his immortal soul." Yet he chose love and mercy over the certainty of martyrdom.

Life and Events

Early Life and Priesthood

Born around 1488 to a respectable merchant family, Peter was from his mother's earlier marriage, making him the eldest of the Meredith siblings. When their father died young, Peter naturally assumed the role of family patriarch. He entered the priesthood, which surprised none who knew him, leaving his younger half-brother Thomas to pursue worldly success.

Parish Ministry at St Lawrence Silversleeves (c. 1510s-1532)

For many years, Peter served as parish priest of St Lawrence Silversleeves, a modest church in London. He excelled at pastoral work, knowing the name of every child in his parish. Under his wardenship, the church gained two fine new stained glass windows and he restored its dark rood screen. The parish was not a place for ambitious men, but Peter was content with his work.

His influence extended beyond his parish. He arranged the marriage between his half-sister Susan and Rowland Bull, recognizing that they would be well-suited. "Peter who had found Rowland, Peter who had quietly sent him to her with a message: This is the one." He also counseled Rowland, who had contemplated religious life, telling him: "There are other ways to serve God, you know, than in holy orders" and "Better, according to St Paul, to marry than burn."

Retirement and Pilgrimage (1532-1534)

In 1532, Peter fell seriously ill and announced: "I can't keep up the pastoral work any more." He chose to retire to the great Charterhouse monastery in London, the strictest religious house in England, home to the Carthusian order. Before taking up residence there, he made a pilgrimage to Rome, cheerfully remarking, "See Rome and die, though I shan't die yet, I dare say."

His pilgrimage extended through much of 1533 and into 1534. He visited Rome, where he fell ill again, as well as other great shrines including Assisi in Italy and Chartres in France. He would have liked to journey to Compostela in Spain, but it was too far. At Assisi, he witnessed what he believed was a miraculous cure of a woman.

At the Charterhouse (1534-1535)

Peter finally took up residence at the Charterhouse, arriving there by early 1535. His presence had an unexpected beneficial effect on old Will Dogget, a difficult elderly man who had been placed there by Thomas. Will attached himself to Peter, and "as long as Father Peter's around the old man seems perfectly happy." Peter's failing health meant that Will often tended to him in his cell.

The Crisis of the Oath (February-April 1535)

In February 1535, Peter visited his family at Chelsea for what would be their last reunion. The atmosphere was joyous as he entertained them with tales of his travels and blessed their meal. But the conversation turned dark when he revealed that King Henry and Cromwell were planning to demand a special oath from the Charterhouse monks, asserting the king's supremacy over the Church.

When asked what he would do if he were prior, Peter answered without hesitation: "I should refuse." He explained his position clearly: "Parliament can decide many things. It can decide the succession, certainly. But Parliament is not competent to alter Man's relationship to God."

Yet Peter showed pastoral wisdom in advising Rowland, who agonized over what a layman should do. Peter counseled: "I think there's no necessity for the laity to intervene. It's the monks who are being challenged, and it's up to us to respond." He advised Rowland to focus on his family responsibilities: "We are warned not to seek martyrdom. It's a spiritual error."

The Martyrdom of the Priors (4 May 1535)

On 4 May 1535, three Carthusian priors – including the prior of the London Charterhouse – were executed at Tyburn for refusing to take the oath. They were hanged, drawn, and quartered in the most brutal fashion. Peter attended the executions, then returned to the monastery feeling very tired.

The king's men nailed the severed arm of the London prior to the monastery gate as a warning.

The Terrible Choice (May 1535)

That afternoon, royal commissioners arrived at the Charterhouse to demand the oath from all the monks. Every monk refused – except one. To the astonishment of the entire community, Father Peter Meredith, "looking tired and ill and having, after the horrors of the morning, it seemed, lost heart," stepped forward alone and took the oath.

The other monks were horrified. To them, Peter had betrayed everything. He was now an outcast in the community he had joined.

The Secret Plan (May-June 1535)

What no one but a few knew was that Peter's apostasy was a deception designed to save Rowland Bull. Rowland had been arrested for treason after refusing to swear the oath when examined by Cromwell and Thomas. He was condemned to the same horrific death as the priors.

In the night after the priors' execution, Peter had conceived a desperate plan. He would take the oath, appearing to be a coward. Then he and Thomas would stage Rowland's death and substitute him for Peter. The physical resemblance between the two men would make this possible. Rowland would become "Father Peter Meredith" and live in isolation at the Charterhouse until he could be moved to safety.

The plan required Peter's death. Thomas was to kill him, making it appear he had died in his sleep. Old Will Dogget and his son Dan were brought into the conspiracy to help execute the plan and care for the man they believed to be Peter.

But before Thomas could carry out the killing, Peter died of natural causes – an apoplexy. "I thank God the Lord took him instead," Thomas told the bewildered Rowland, who had been drugged and brought to Peter's cell, not knowing Peter was already dead.

Peter had left a final message for Rowland: "He wants you to live. Your family needs you. He reminds you of what he said: you have already earned the martyr's crown because you were ready to die. By doing this, however, he has prevented you."

Final Departure (June 1535)

In June 1535, the frail "Father Peter Meredith" (actually Rowland Bull) was quietly conveyed out of the Charterhouse to another religious house in the north, accompanied by old Will Dogget. The real Peter Meredith was dead, having sacrificed his reputation and possibly his soul to save his brother-in-law's life.

Relationships

Susan Meredith

Susan was Peter's half-sister, but of all his siblings, she felt closest to him. While she loved her clever, charming brother Thomas, it was "comfortable, solid Peter" who was her anchor and confidant. Peter had served as a father figure to her after their father died young, and she continued to rely on his moral guidance throughout her life. When facing difficult decisions, she would write to him for advice. His absence during his pilgrimage left a void in her life, and his return in February 1535 brought her great happiness – though it would be brief.

Thomas Meredith

Thomas was Peter's younger half-brother (or possibly full brother), and they represented opposite paths in life. Thomas was "quick, brilliant, charming, wilful" – a man of the world who became a successful courtier under Cromwell. Peter was the family conscience, Thomas the pragmatist. Yet there was deep affection between them. Peter understood Thomas's nature, remarking with gentle reproach: "This is the trouble with being in power, like Thomas here... They want to get things done and sooner or later they always forget their principles."

Thomas, for his part, respected Peter even while pursuing his worldly advancement. In the end, Thomas risked his own life to help execute Peter's desperate plan to save Rowland, demonstrating that beneath his pragmatism lay loyalty to family and some remnant of principle.

Rowland Bull

Peter recognized that Rowland, a devout scholar of law, would be the perfect husband for Susan. He sent Rowland to her with the message: "This is the one." Peter had counseled Rowland through his vocational crisis, helping him understand that marriage was a legitimate path of service to God. The two men looked remarkably alike, sharing stout builds and balding heads – a physical resemblance that Susan's brother Thomas noted with amusement but which would prove fateful.

Peter's relationship with Rowland was that of spiritual father and guide. Rowland trusted him absolutely and repeatedly wished for his counsel during the crisis of 1534-1535. In the end, Peter gave his very identity and life for Rowland, the ultimate expression of love and sacrifice.

Will Dogget

Old Will Dogget was a difficult, wandering man whom Thomas placed in the Charterhouse hoping the monks could manage him. Against all odds, Will attached himself to Peter and became his devoted companion. As Peter's health failed, Will took to sleeping by the door of his cell and tending to him. Will's loyalty proved crucial – he became one of the few trusted with the terrible secret of the identity switch, caring for the man he believed to be Peter (actually Rowland) and ultimately accompanying him into exile in the north.

Historical Context

Peter Meredith lived during one of the most traumatic periods in English religious history. The English Reformation began not from theological conviction but from Henry VIII's need for a male heir and his desire to marry Anne Boleyn. When Pope Clement VII refused to annul Henry's marriage to Katherine of Aragon, Henry broke with Rome and declared himself Supreme Head of the Church in England through the Act of Supremacy (1534).

This created an impossible situation for devout Catholics. They had been taught that the Pope was Christ's vicar on earth and that the unity of Christendom under Rome was essential to salvation. Yet their king – who had been named "Defender of the Faith" by the Pope himself – now demanded they reject papal authority and accept his supremacy in religious matters.

The Carthusian order, to which Peter belonged, was known for its strictness and devotion. The Charterhouse monks were among the few religious communities who resisted Henry's demands. The execution of the three Carthusian priors in May 1535 was designed to terrorize the other monks into submission. The method of execution – hanging, drawing, and quartering – was deliberately savage, and displaying the prior's severed arm on the monastery gate was psychological warfare.

Peter's decision to apparently take the oath was seen as complete betrayal by his fellow monks. They could not know it was a deception designed to save an innocent man. The other monks would suffer greatly – three more would be executed, and the rest subjected to constant humiliation and pressure. Other Carthusian houses submitted to the oath, and even the head of the order in France advised the London house to do likewise, making their continued resistance seem futile.

The case of Thomas More, the former Lord Chancellor who was executed in July 1535, showed that even the most prominent and learned men were not safe if they refused the oath. More's execution sent a clear message: no one, regardless of status or past service to the crown, would be spared if they defied Henry's will.

In this context, Peter's sacrifice was extraordinary. He gave up not just his life but his reputation and possibly (as he feared) his immortal soul to save Rowland Bull. He died having betrayed everything his fellow monks believed he stood for, knowing that history would record him as a coward who broke under pressure while his brethren stood firm.

Themes and Significance

The Cost of Faith

Peter embodies the terrible price of religious conviction during the Reformation. Unlike martyrs who died publicly proclaiming their faith, Peter's sacrifice was hidden and ambiguous. He could not die with the honor of martyrdom because his death had to appear natural and his apostasy had to seem real. He traded eternal glory for eternal shame – or so it appeared to all who knew him.

Moral Complexity vs. Moral Clarity

Peter represents a paradox: a man of absolute moral clarity ("Either something is right, or it's wrong") who nonetheless engaged in elaborate deception in his final days. His plan required him to lie, to feign apostasy, and to help Rowland deceive the authorities. The text explicitly notes that Peter "did not know if he was doing right or wrong" and feared he was "losing his immortal soul."

This captures the impossible moral situations created by tyranny. Peter could not both save Rowland's life and maintain his reputation for integrity. He could not both prevent a terrible injustice and remain pure in the eyes of his community. He chose mercy and love over certainty and reputation.

Pastoral Wisdom vs. Heroic Witness

Peter distinguished between the duties of monks and laymen. While he would not compromise his own conscience, he counseled Rowland that laymen with families had different obligations. This reflects the Catholic tradition of different vocations with different responsibilities. Monks could seek martyrdom; family men could prioritize their dependents.

Yet Peter's final act collapsed this distinction. He didn't just counsel Rowland to avoid martyrdom – he actively prevented it through deception, and he did so at the cost of his own martyrdom. Love trumped vocation.

The Hidden Saint

Peter Meredith is significant as a "hidden saint" – someone whose greatest act of virtue appeared to others as vice. While the martyred priors would be remembered and venerated (and indeed, the Carthusian martyrs were later canonized), Peter would be remembered only as the one monk who broke and took the oath. His true sacrifice was known only to a handful of people.

This reflects a recurring theme in London: that history records the public acts but misses the private sacrifices, and that some of the greatest acts of love and courage are never recognized as such.

Family Bonds

Peter's story emphasizes the power of family loyalty even in an age of religious division. Despite their different paths – Peter the priest, Thomas the courtier, Susan the devoted wife – the Meredith siblings remained deeply connected. Thomas risked his position (and possibly his life) to help Peter save Rowland. Peter sacrificed everything for his brother-in-law. These bonds of kinship proved stronger than the forces trying to tear English society apart.

Key Quotes About Peter Meredith

"And Peter, comfortable, solid Peter. Though actually her half-brother from an earlier marriage, he was the one to whom she felt closer. It was Peter, the eldest of the Meredith family, who had taken the place of their father when he died young. Peter who was still, and always would be, the family conscience."

This summary from Susan captures Peter's essential role in the family.
