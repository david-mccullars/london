Overview

Charlotte Dogget is the eldest of Silas Dogget's four daughters, who married sea captain Jonas Barnikel and lived a comfortable middle-class life in Camberwell Grove. Plain in appearance but possessed of a fortune from her father's successful dust-heap business, she represents the typical trajectory of her family's dramatic rise from poverty to respectability. Her practical nature and shrewd financial judgment, inherited from her father, manifest most clearly in her bold bet against her own husband's clipper ship in 1889.

Physical Description

Charlotte is described as plain in appearance, a trait she shares with her two elder sisters. No specific physical features are detailed in the text beyond this general assessment. The book notes that "with their plain looks, the eldest three Dogget girls had all found good husbands" primarily through the attraction of their substantial fortunes rather than physical beauty.

Character and Personality

Charlotte is the only one of Silas Dogget's daughters who truly remembers their origins in poverty. She "could really remember the dingy lodgings in Southwark when he came home stinking from the boat" and would "sometimes, alone... shudder at the memory before pushing it from her mind." This awareness of her family's humble beginnings sets her apart from her younger sisters, particularly Mary Anne, who had a governess and no memory of their father's dredging days.

Despite her plain appearance and the social embarrassment of her origins, Charlotte possesses a practical, grounded nature. She is observant and socially aware, as demonstrated when she notices and comments on Mary Anne's inappropriate attention to young Meredith at the Guv'nor's dinner party, whispering "You squeezed his hand!" and warning her sister: "Well, you'll never see him again, make no mistake."

Most remarkably, Charlotte demonstrates considerable financial acumen and a pragmatic understanding of risk - traits clearly inherited from her father Silas. While devoted to her husband Jonas Barnikel, she does not let sentiment cloud her judgment when it comes to betting on clipper ships.

Life and Events

Early Life and Marriage (c. 1818-1840s)

Charlotte was born around 1818, the first daughter of Silas Dogget during his years as a dredger and corpse-fisher on the Thames. She experienced her family's poverty firsthand, living in "dingy lodgings in Southwark" and witnessing her father return home "stinking from the boat." By the age of ten, however, the middle girls were attending "a little private school for young ladies," suggesting the family's circumstances had begun to improve.

When Charlotte reached marriageable age, the family was still in Lambeth, and Silas "had not really done much to bring her out into the local society because he wasn't quite sure how to do it." Nevertheless, like her sisters, Charlotte's substantial fortune - derived from her father's successful dust-heap business - proved attractive to suitors. She married Jonas Barnikel, a capable sailor who acknowledged: "I'd still be living down there, like as not, if it wasn't for her."

Married Life in Camberwell (1850s-1880s)

Charlotte's money enabled her husband to purchase a share in a vessel and become a captain. The couple established themselves in "a pleasant Georgian house and garden... in Camberwell Grove, on the genteel wooded slopes that gazed down on the crowded docklands of Deptford." Though Barnikel was "making a fortune of his own" by this time, he recognized that Charlotte's wealth had provided the foundation for his success.

The Barnikels had children, as evidenced by references to them accompanying the couple on various family occasions. Their marriage appears solid if unromantic - a practical partnership between a plain woman of fortune and a capable seaman who genuinely appreciated what she had done for him.

The Crystal Palace Exhibition (1851)

In August 1851, Charlotte attended the Guv'nor's birthday dinner at his Blackheath mansion, arriving with her husband and children. During the meal, she demonstrated her social awareness by noticing Mary Anne's inappropriate flirtation with young Meredith. When Mary Anne squeezed the young man's hand as the ladies withdrew, Charlotte confronted her sister immediately, showing both her moral concern and her understanding of social consequences.

She also provided a moment of social rescue during an awkward silence at dinner, exclaiming "Ah! Here comes the meat!" when conversation had grown uncomfortable following Meredith's controversial statements about Hindu religion.

The Clipper Ship Era (1889-1890)

By 1889, Charlotte was in her early seventies but still active. Her husband Jonas, though he "should have retired years ago," was still making brilliant runs as captain of the clipper Charlotte Rose (named in Charlotte's honor), which Barnikel co-owned with the Earl of St James and Colonel Meredith's father.

When the Charlotte Rose departed for Australia in the wool race against the Cutty Sark, Charlotte made a remarkable decision: she secretly bet a substantial sum on the Cutty Sark to win, betting against her own husband. When Barnikel returned after losing the race (having been caught in a storm and forced to put in for repairs), Charlotte revealed her wager.

"I put a bet on the race. Well, I had our son do it for me," she told him calmly.

"You bet on me? Like St James?" Barnikel asked incredulously.

"No, dear. I bet on the Cutty Sark."

"You bet against your own husband, woman?"

"Well, somebody had to. I knew you couldn't win. The Cutty Sark had too much sail," she explained practically, adding with a smile: "We made a thousand pounds!"

This bold financial move demonstrated Charlotte's inheritance of her father's business acumen. Rather than losing money like the Earl of St James, the Barnikels actually profited from the race - but only because Charlotte had the clarity of judgment and courage to bet against sentiment.

Relationships

Jonas Barnikel

Charlotte's relationship with her husband is one of mutual respect and practical partnership rather than romantic passion. Jonas openly acknowledges his debt to her: her fortune enabled him to become a captain, and her money purchased their comfortable home in Camberwell. He honored her by naming his clipper the Charlotte, telling the Earl of St James: "The earl and I have just named the clipper after Charlotte."

Yet their marriage is characterized by genuine affection. After losing the race in 1889, when Charlotte revealed her bet on the Cutty Sark, Barnikel's response was to laugh into his grog and say: "You're as bad as your old Guv'nor sometimes!" This was clearly meant as a compliment, and Charlotte's reply - "I hope I am" - suggests her pride in inheriting Silas's practical shrewdness.

Silas Dogget

Charlotte's relationship with her father is complex. As the eldest daughter, she alone truly remembers his days as a dredger, memories that still make her shudder. Yet she also seems to have inherited his financial acumen and his unsentimental approach to money. Her practical nature and her willingness to make a shrewd bet regardless of sentiment echo Silas's own business methods.

Mary Anne Dogget

Charlotte appears to take a somewhat protective or monitoring role toward her younger sister Mary Anne, who is described as different from the rest of the family. At the 1851 dinner, Charlotte is quick to notice and confront Mary Anne's inappropriate behavior with young Meredith, demonstrating both sisterly concern and an understanding of the potential scandal that could affect the family's newly respectable status.

Historical Context

Charlotte's life spans a transformational period in Victorian England and embodies the dramatic social mobility possible in that era. Born around 1818 during the poverty-stricken years of her father's dredging business, she witnessed and remembered the squalor of early industrial London. By the time of the Great Exhibition in 1851, her family had achieved middle-class respectability, and she was married to a successful sea captain.

Her marriage connected her to the golden age of the clipper ships - those magnificent sailing vessels that raced tea from China and wool from Australia in the decades before steamships dominated the seas. The naming of the Charlotte and later the Charlotte Rose after her represents her importance to her husband's career and the maritime trade that enriched Victorian Britain.

By 1889, Charlotte was living through the twilight of the clipper era. The Cutty Sark - the ship she shrewdly bet on - would become the last and most famous of the great clippers. Her recognition that even the finest ship and captain could not beat the Cutty Sark showed her understanding of the technical and practical realities of maritime competition.

Themes and Significance

Charlotte Dogget embodies several key themes in London:

Social Mobility and Its Costs: As the only daughter who truly remembers poverty, Charlotte represents the psychological cost of rapid social advancement. The memories that make her shudder - of dingy lodgings and her father's stinking clothes - are the price of the family's rise. Yet she successfully maintains her new social position, never allowing those memories to undermine her family's respectability.

Practical Wisdom Over Sentiment: Charlotte's decision to bet against her own husband demonstrates a triumph of practical judgment over romantic sentiment. Like her father, she understands that money and risk must be managed rationally. Her bet on the Cutty Sark ensures that while her husband's reputation might suffer from losing the race, the family's finances actually benefit. This pragmatic approach to life and money is distinctly Doggett in character.

Women and Financial Agency: Though Charlotte's initial fortune came from her father, and though she used her husband's son to place her bet (suggesting women could not openly gamble on such things), she demonstrates significant financial agency. Her willingness to act independently in financial matters, even betting against her husband's ship, shows that Victorian women of means could exercise considerable economic power, albeit often through indirect means.

The Maritime World: Through her marriage to Jonas Barnikel, Charlotte provides a connection to the world of clipper ships and maritime trade that was central to Victorian Britain's commercial empire. The ships named for her represent both her personal importance and the role of merchant vessels in British prosperity.

Memory and Forgetting: Charlotte's relationship with her past - remembering but suppressing the shameful memories of poverty - reflects the broader theme of how families and individuals reconstruct their identities during periods of dramatic social change. Her younger sisters can live entirely in the present of respectability; Charlotte cannot entirely forget the past, but neither does she allow it to define her present.
