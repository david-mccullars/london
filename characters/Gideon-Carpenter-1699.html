Overview

Gideon Carpenter is a young boy, one of O Be Joyful Carpenter's seven grandchildren. At age nine in 1708, he is described as one of his grandfather's "two favourites," along with his eleven-year-old sister Martha. Named after his great-great-grandfather Gideon Carpenter, the young boy embodies the strict Puritan values of his family and displays remarkable compassion and courage despite his tender years.

Physical Description

No detailed physical description is provided in the text beyond the characterization of having "quiet but determined characters, their serious faces and rather solemn eyes."

Character and Personality

Young Gideon demonstrates several remarkable qualities for a child of nine. He possesses a deeply religious nature, having been "strictly brought up in the Puritan manner" by his family. His serious demeanor reflects the somber Puritan upbringing, yet beneath this gravity lies a tender heart and fierce loyalty to his family.

What most distinguishes Gideon is his compassionate response to his grandfather's confession of cowardice. When O Be Joyful admits to having failed to save Martha in the Great Fire forty years earlier, young Gideon's reaction reveals both his moral clarity and his love. While his sister Martha takes a stricter theological view about election and salvation, Gideon focuses on his grandfather's humanity and suffering. His declaration that he would "go down and rescue him" from hell, if necessary, shows a willingness to brave even eternal damnation out of love—an extraordinary statement from such a young Puritan child.

The boy also displays intellectual curiosity and a desire to understand his grandfather better. When confused by O Be Joyful's work on the bishop's throne, he asks earnestly whether his grandfather "could not refuse," seeking to reconcile his love for his grandfather with his Puritan principles.

Life and Events

1708: Visit to St Paul's Cathedral

On the day of the Lord Mayor's procession in October 1708, Gideon accompanies his sister Martha and their grandfather O Be Joyful to St Paul's Cathedral. This is the first time either child has ever entered an Anglican church, and they hesitate at the door of the "popish-looking cathedral."

Inside, O Be Joyful shows the children his finest work—the carved bishop's throne he created for the cathedral. When the children express disappointment at learning their grandfather made a throne for a bishop (anathema to their Puritan beliefs), Gideon tries to defend him by asking if he could have refused the commission. His grandfather's lame justification—that one must follow the master's direction—leaves the boy unconvinced.

Attempting to restore his grandfather's reputation in his own mind, Gideon asks O Be Joyful to tell the famous family story of how he tried to save old Martha Carpenter in the Great Fire of 1666. Instead of the heroic tale, however, O Be Joyful confesses the truth: that he had been afraid and let her burn.

O Be Joyful then leads the children up to the Whispering Gallery, a hundred feet above the cathedral floor. From opposite sides of the dome, the children's whispered conversation is carried around the curved surface to their grandfather's ears. In this conversation, young Gideon defends his grandfather, saying "He was just afraid. That's all." When his sister Martha suggests their grandfather may not be among the elect and might go to hell, Gideon makes his remarkable declaration: "If he's sent to hell, I shall go down and rescue him."

The boy then seems to think deeply, and after a pause asks, "Martha. Do you think he'll still go to heaven?"

When the church bells ring out, drowning even the dome in sound, Gideon stands with his grandfather and sister. O Be Joyful, moved by his grandchildren's love despite knowing his unworthiness, hugs them both as he cries out in exultation at the sound of the bells.

Relationships

O Be Joyful Carpenter

Gideon is one of his grandfather's two favorite grandchildren. O Be Joyful finds pride in both Gideon and Martha for their "quiet but determined characters" and sees in them the return of the original Gideon and Martha's spirits. The relationship is warm despite the generational gap, with young Gideon regularly asking his grandfather to tell stories of Puritan heroes like old Gideon who "fought with Cromwell against the king."

When O Be Joyful confesses his long-held secret about failing to save Martha in the Fire, young Gideon's response is more compassionate than judgmental. Unlike his sister who focuses on theological questions of election, Gideon focuses on his grandfather's fear and humanity. His promise to rescue his grandfather from hell, should it be necessary, profoundly moves the old man and gives him unexpected comfort in his final years.

Martha Carpenter

Gideon's relationship with his older sister Martha reflects a typical sibling dynamic, though filtered through their strict Puritan upbringing. Martha, at eleven, is more theologically rigid than her nine-year-old brother, quick to judge their grandfather's lack of courage and faith. Gideon is more forgiving and emotional, focusing on love rather than doctrine. Their whispered conversation in the Whispering Gallery reveals both their different temperaments and their genuine concern for their grandfather's fate.

Historical Context

Gideon lives in the era following the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and the Act of Toleration of 1689, which granted freedom of worship to Protestant Dissenters like his family. No longer forced to attend Anglican services, families like the Carpenters established their own meeting houses and raised their children in the Puritan tradition without interference.

However, Dissenters still faced legal restrictions. They could not hold public office, attend university, or practice certain professions without taking Anglican communion. The Occasional Conformity controversy—where some Dissenters took Anglican communion just once to qualify for public positions—divided the community between pragmatists and purists. Gideon's family clearly fell into the purist camp, as evidenced by the fact that neither he nor Martha had ever entered an Anglican church before the 1708 visit to St Paul's.

The London of Gideon's childhood was experiencing a Protestant confidence following William III and Mary II's reign. The threat of Catholic monarchy had been banished, and England was at war with Catholic France. The Puritan tradition, while no longer dominant as it had been under Cromwell, remained a significant force in English life, particularly in London's merchant and artisan classes.

Themes and Significance

Young Gideon represents several important themes in the novel. He embodies the continuity of family values across generations—his name deliberately evokes his great-great-grandfather, the original Gideon Carpenter who fought in Cromwell's armies. The family's hope is that this new Gideon will be "purer, more godly" than his compromised grandfather O Be Joyful.

More significantly, Gideon's promise to rescue his grandfather from hell stands as one of the most moving moments in the chapter. It represents a child's pure love transcending strict Calvinist theology about predestination and election. While his sister Martha worries about whether their grandfather is among the elect, Gideon simply offers unconditional love and loyalty—a fundamentally Christian response that ironically cuts through all the theological complexity of their faith.

The scene also illustrates the transmission of family history and memory. Young Gideon has grown up hearing heroic stories about old Gideon and Martha, creating an idealized past that his grandfather struggles to live up to. The boy's reaction to his grandfather's confession—compassion rather than rejection—suggests that the values of the family will survive even as the stories are corrected.
