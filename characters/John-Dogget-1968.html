Overview

Dr. John Dogget is a curator at the Museum of London in 1997 and a respected archaeologist leading excavations in the City. A "Londoner through and through" with distinctive physical features including a white flash in his hair and webbed fingers, he represents the culmination of the Dogget family's long connection to London. He is particularly noted for his eloquent ability to communicate the meaning and significance of archaeological work to the public, making history come alive through his poetic understanding of London's layered past.

Physical Description

John Dogget has several distinctive physical features. He has a white flash in his hair, a hereditary trait that runs through the Dogget family line. He also has webbed fingers, another hereditary characteristic, about which he makes light, joking that they are "Good for swimming and digging." He has a cheerful temper and is described as popular and easily recognized, both for his physical features and his engaging personality.

Character and Personality

John Dogget is characterized by his cheerful temperament and passion for his work. He is popular among colleagues and visitors alike, combining serious scholarship with an accessible, warm manner. His enthusiasm for archaeology and London's history is infectious, and he has a particular gift for making academic subjects engaging to diverse audiences, from schoolchildren to trustees.

He displays intellectual depth combined with humility and humor. Rather than being self-conscious about his distinctive physical traits, he jokes about them good-naturedly. He is hardworking and "always so busy," constantly engaged in both his curatorial duties at the museum and fieldwork at archaeological excavations.

Most notably, John possesses a poetic sensibility about his work. He doesn't see archaeology merely as the recovery of objects, but as a means of connecting with the lives of past Londoners. His ability to articulate the profound meaning of archaeological work reveals a philosophical and reflective nature beneath his cheerful exterior.

Life and Events

1997: The River Excavation

In the spring of 1997, Dr. Dogget leads an archaeological excavation on a small site overlooking the Thames in the City of London. The dig takes advantage of the demolition of an inferior postwar building to explore what lies beneath. The excavation has already gone down ten feet below the old basement, reaching a layer of gravel that would have been the surface in the time of Julius Caesar.

The dig attracts attention from potential patrons of the Museum of London. Sir Eugene Penny, chairman of the Penny Insurance Company and an alderman of London who has become interested in the museum, visits the excavation site one mid-afternoon in spring. Dr. Dogget personally explains the excavation to Sir Eugene, walking him through exactly what they are doing. He handles this interaction professionally, answering intelligent questions and demonstrating both his expertise and his ability to communicate effectively with non-specialists.

Sarah Bull, a new archaeology graduate recruit to the Museum of London, has specifically wanted to join this particular dig because it is being conducted by Dr. Dogget. She hopes that working on the excavation might give her an opportunity to be noticed by the popular curator, though as a junior staff member she has had little direct interaction with him thus far.

Work at the Museum of London

Dr. Dogget serves as a curator at the Museum of London, which is located on a pedestrian area near St Paul's Cathedral, its windows overlooking a large fragment of the old Roman wall. The museum is arranged as a walk through history, featuring recreated scenes from different periods, and behind its popular exhibits lies serious scholarship.

John gives talks to visiting groups, including parties of schoolchildren. In one such talk to older students, he delivers a memorable explanation of archaeological work that reveals his philosophical approach to the field. He describes how human habitation leaves compressed layers in the earth, comparing it to the natural process of fallen leaves accumulating year after year. He emphasizes that the objects archaeologists find – a Roman tile, a coin, a clay pipe from Shakespeare's time – belonged to real people "who lived, and loved, and looked out at the river and the sky each day just like you and me."

Relationships

Sarah Bull

Sarah Bull, a recent archaeology graduate who has come to work at the Museum of London, is attracted to both Dr. Dogget's professional reputation and his personal qualities. She specifically wanted to join his excavation dig, hoping he might notice her for the first time. As a junior recruit, she has had little interaction with him, though she finds him appealing – questioning to herself whether he likes "blue-eyed blondes" in addition to Roman artefacts. She has heard him give talks and is impressed by his eloquent description of archaeological work, which she particularly likes and remembers while working in the trench.

Sir Eugene Penny

Dr. Dogget interacts professionally with Sir Eugene Penny, a trustee and patron of the Museum of London. When Sir Eugene visits the excavation site, John provides him with a thorough explanation of their work, demonstrating his ability to engage with potential supporters of the museum. This represents the institutional side of his role, balancing fieldwork with the need to cultivate relationships with donors and trustees.

Historical Context

Dr. John Dogget practices archaeology at a particularly exciting time for London archaeology. The latter decades of the 20th century have seen major discoveries in London, including the Temple of Mithras and, just a few years before 1997, the discovery that the old Guildhall stands on the site of a huge Roman amphitheatre. The devastation of the Blitz and subsequent rebuilding has provided numerous opportunities for excavation when older buildings are demolished and replaced.

The Museum of London itself represents a modern approach to public history, making scholarship accessible through experiential exhibits and active engagement with schools and tourists. As a curator there, John is part of a institution that bridges academic research and public education.

His work also takes place in a London that is being rediscovered and revalued. The city is cleaner than it has been for centuries – fish can be caught in the Thames again. The flood barrier has addressed the centuries-old problem of gradual flooding. There is a renewed appreciation for London's layered history and multicultural heritage, as evidenced by exhibitions like "The Peopling of London" that challenge simplistic narratives about British racial and ethnic identity.

John's grandfather having been a fireman in the Blitz connects him personally to one of London's defining moments of the 20th century, grounding his historical work in family memory.

Themes and Significance

Dr. John Dogget embodies several key themes in the novel's exploration of London's history. Most fundamentally, he represents the idea of continuity and connection across time. His poetic articulation of archaeology as "a huge and endless compression of lives" captures the novel's central metaphor – that London is built of layers, with each generation leaving traces that persist beneath the surface.

His hereditary physical traits – the white flash in his hair and webbed fingers – link him visibly to his Dogget ancestors throughout the novel's span of two millennia. These distinctive features have marked Doggets across centuries, and in John they appear in a modern professional who uses them humorously ("Good for swimming and digging"). The webbed fingers, once perhaps a curiosity or even a stigma, become in John's self-deprecating joke a practical advantage for an archaeologist – a perfect symbol of how the past adapts and persists in new forms.

John's work as a curator and archaeologist literalizes the novel's project. Just as Edward Rutherford excavates and displays London's history through fiction, John Dogget excavates and displays it through archaeology. His ability to make the past meaningful to contemporary audiences – schoolchildren, trustees, colleagues – mirrors the novel's own ambition. His reflection that each artifact belonged to someone "who lived, and loved, and looked out at the river and the sky each day just like you and me" could be the novel's own motto.

The description of archaeological excavation as opening "one of the millions and millions of windows, hidden in the ground" parallels the novel's structure of opening windows into different periods through its characters' stories. John, working at the Museum of London and digging in the City, quite literally uncovers the lives of people we have met in earlier chapters – perhaps even his own ancestors.

Finally, John represents the modern inheritor of London's long history. As someone whose grandfather fought fires during the Blitz, he carries recent memory, but as an archaeologist working down to the level of Julius Caesar's time, he reaches back to the city's origins. He is both a product of London's history and an interpreter of it, making him a fitting figure for the novel's conclusion.
