Overview

Hrothgar Barnikel, known as Barnikel of Billingsgate, is a towering Danish merchant-noble of London whose massive physical presence is matched only by his legendary temper and generous heart. A descendant of Vikings who earned their family name from a compassionate warrior's cry of "Bairn ni Kel" ("Don't kill the children"), Barnikel embodies the successful merger of Danish and Saxon cultures in 11th-century London. Living on the eastern hill near Billingsgate wharf, this wealthy trader commands respect through both his commercial success and his position in the city's Defence Guild. His friendship with the Saxon merchant Leofric represents the Anglo-Danish unity of London, though both this friendship and his hopes for his son's marriage will be tested by the Norman Conquest.

Physical Description

Barnikel is a giant of a man who towers over others. He has a great mane of red hair and a huge red beard that is two feet wide and three feet long. This massive descendant of the Vikings can lift a grown man with each hand. His periodic rages, when his face becomes as red as his hair, are legendary. His blue cloak is trimmed with costly ermine from the Viking state of Russia, a sign of his considerable wealth. When he pounds his fist upon the table, strong men blanch; at his bellowing roar, lines of doors along the street hastily close.

Character and Personality

Barnikel possesses a commanding personality shaped by his Viking heritage, yet tempered by unusual compassion. His physical strength and legendary rages give him a fearsome reputation, but he is held in affection by his neighbors—a trait inherited from his compassionate Viking ancestor. His huge face softens and breaks into a smile when he sees Leofric's daughter Hilda, revealing a gentle, paternal side beneath his intimidating exterior.

He demonstrates remarkable generosity and perception, rescuing the starving boy Alfred and recognizing in him "the heart of a hero" because "he won't give up." This ability to see potential in a desperate vagrant shows both his wisdom and his kindness. He takes pride in his family heritage and eagerly looks forward to Hilda joining his household, planning to secretly cancel Leofric's debt to him as a wedding gift.

Barnikel is fiercely patriotic and proud of London's independence. Unlike the more cautious Leofric, he favors immediate, aggressive action against threats. At Harold II's coronation, his relief leads him to make a crucial error: he contemptuously tells Silversleeves to keep his "great French nose out of our business," an insult he doesn't like people referring to his nose. This impulsive rudeness, born of jubilation rather than malice, will have consequences in Silversleeves's elaborate schemes.

His temper is truly spectacular. When Leofric breaks their children's betrothal to marry his daughter to Henri Silversleeves instead, Barnikel's rage is legendary. He destroys his own home for over an hour—smashing barrels, pitchers, beds, doors, and even breaking the shaft of a battle-axe. Neighbors gather to watch from a safe distance, and even those who remember the reigns of Canute and Ethelred the Unready confess they've witnessed nothing better.

Yet beneath this volcanic exterior lies a man of principle. He believes in fighting for what is right and serving his adopted homeland. His quiet warning to Alfred after William's coronation—"Wait and see"—suggests depths of determination and perhaps plans for resistance that his more demonstrative rages might mask.

Life and Events

Early 1066: The Crisis of Succession

As England faces the succession crisis following Edward the Confessor's death, Barnikel stands with his friend Leofric outside Westminster Abbey on the cold January morning when the Witan chooses a new king. Both men want an English king, united in their Anglo-Danish London identity despite their different ancestries. Barnikel is flushed with relief when Harold II is crowned.

It is at this moment that he makes his great mistake. Seeing the hooded figure of Silversleeves near the Abbey door, Barnikel, "flushed with relief," growls contemptuously: "We have an English king, thank God. So keep your great French nose out of our business." He stomps out while Leofric looks embarrassed. The strange figure says nothing, but he does not like people referring to his nose. Though Barnikel is unaware of it, Silversleeves is playing a deeper game, and this insult ensures Barnikel will be fitted into the Norman's perfect schemes.

Spring and Summer 1066: Kindness to Alfred

In the approach to Easter 1066, as England becomes agitated with reports of William of Normandy's invasion preparations, Barnikel discovers the fourteen-year-old Alfred huddled by the London Stone, starving and desperate after three months as a vagrant. Through a searching catechism of questions—name, origin, trade, whether he's free, when he last ate, if he's stolen—Barnikel assesses the boy's character.

When Alfred declares he won't give up and won't go home despite the certainty of starvation, Barnikel recognizes something special. With a great guffaw and a back-slap that nearly sends the boy into his broth, he declares: "In this boy dwells the heart of a hero. A mighty warrior. Because he won't give up. He just told me. He means it. The little fellow won't give up!" He takes Alfred into his household and arranges an apprenticeship at the armoury for him.

All summer, as the Saxon fleet patrols the Channel, Barnikel takes Alfred about with him like an adopted son. He strides each morning from his house on the eastern hill down to Billingsgate wharf to inspect the little ships and their cargoes of wool, grain, and fish. He teaches the country boy about London's character, its wealth, its liberties, its pride. "The walls of London touch the sea," he explains, expressing the city's maritime identity and its sense of independence: "No invader can take London. And no king is king unless we say so."

September-October 1066: The Battle

When the English fleet puts in for harvest in September, leaving England vulnerable, and Harold II rushes north to defeat the Norwegian invasion at York, Barnikel storms into the armoury with demands as the city arms itself. After William lands at Pevensey, Barnikel attends a council meeting with the king. Unlike the more cautious Leofric, who argues for starving the Normans out, Barnikel cries: "How can he hold back? Strike now!" He declares firmly: "This family will fight."

On 11 October 1066, Barnikel marches out with Harold II toward Hastings, carrying his two-handed battle-axe. His eighteen-year-old son marches with him. They march in the place of honor, by the king's standard, with the Staller and the London contingent. Alfred notices with concern that not all the London men are well-armed—one carries a window shutter instead of a proper shield.

At the Battle of Hastings on 14 October, Barnikel fights by the king's standard with the Staller and a small group of stalwarts. The tremendous two-handed axe blows from men like Barnikel bite through the Norman cavalry's chain mail, causing them to flee. Hour after hour the battle continues. When the cavalry charges twice more, pretending to flee and luring the English into traps, Barnikel and his son survive among the little group who fight to the bitter end. Both father and son survive to accompany the badly wounded Staller from the field after Harold II's death.

December 1066: Betrayal and Rage

After Hastings, as William slowly encircles London, burning farms and wearing down the city's will to resist, Barnikel is furious with everyone. "Why don't we strike again?" he roars repeatedly. But the situation is hopeless: disease in William's camp has bought time, but the city has little direction, the young prince-king is ineffective, and the northern nobles talk of going home. The Archbishop of Canterbury secretly negotiates with the Normans.

When the Folkmoot meets in St Paul's churchyard in December and Leofric advocates accepting William's terms, Barnikel explodes. In a voice heard halfway down the West Cheap, he bellows at his friend: "Traitor!" Then, in his rage and hurt, he publicly breaks off his son's betrothal: "As for your daughter, keep her. My son will marry no traitor's child. Do you hear?"

Three days later, Barnikel learns that Hilda has been betrothed to Henri Silversleeves. He cannot believe it at first. His son points out that they had refused the marriage, but Barnikel moans: "He might have known I didn't really mean it." Then it occurs to him that Leofric had known exactly what he was doing.

What follows becomes legendary. Barnikel's rage continues for more than an hour. The inhabitants of Billingsgate and All Hallows agree there has been nothing like it in their lifetime. Even men who remember Canute and Ethelred the Unready confess they have witnessed nothing better. People watch from doorways and windows; a few desperadoes gather thirty paces from his door, ready to scatter.

The damage he wreaks is awesome: three barrels of ale smashed, seven earthenware pitchers, six wooden platters, two beds, a cauldron, five wooden stools, fifteen pots of preserved fruit, and a chest destroyed. Three meat hooks and the roasting spit are twisted beyond use. The shaft of a double-handed battle-axe is broken. A trestle table, three wooden shutters, two oak doors, and the larder wall are further destroyed or greatly reduced. "Even his Viking ancestors," people say, "would not have disdained these efforts."

After the Conquest

Barnikel attends William's coronation at Westminster Abbey on Christmas Day 1066, along with Silversleeves and Leofric. Two days later, when young Alfred remarks that the Norman king is making himself master of London, Barnikel gives a quiet warning: "Wait and see." This uncharacteristic quietness suggests he has not accepted the conquest, and may harbor plans for resistance or revenge.

Relationships

Leofric the Saxon

Barnikel's friendship with Leofric represents the successful merger of Danish and Saxon cultures in London. It is said that nothing can shake their friendship, though the hooded Silversleeves who watches them doubts this: "Enmity lasts. Friendship is less certain. Especially at such times as these."

The two men stand together at momentous events—the burial of Edward the Confessor, the coronation of Harold II, the arming of London before Hastings. Barnikel's eldest child, his daughter, is due to marry the Norseman's son, and Leofric owes Barnikel a debt of money, though Barnikel plans to secretly cancel it as a wedding gift. He has lent Leofric money to help with a cargo of wool lost at sea, demonstrating both his wealth and his generosity to friends.

Yet they are different men. Where Leofric is cautious and calculating, Barnikel is impulsive and direct. At the council before Hastings, Barnikel cries for immediate attack while Leofric urges a strategy of attrition. After the battle, when Leofric pragmatically advocates accepting William's terms, Barnikel sees it as betrayal.

The breaking of their friendship is both public and painful. Barnikel's shout of "Traitor!" in St Paul's churchyard is heard by several hundred citizens. His declaration that his son will not marry a traitor's child seems, in his fury, like a genuine rejection. Yet he later moans that Leofric "might have known I didn't really mean it"—suggesting he expected his friend to understand that his words were spoken in anger, not as a final decision. The realization that Leofric did understand, and took advantage of the situation to free himself from the betrothal and make a more advantageous match, is what truly enrages Barnikel. The betrayal is not just political but personal—a deliberate exploitation of their friendship.

Hilda

Barnikel's affection for Leofric's daughter Hilda reveals his warmest, most paternal side. Few things give him greater pleasure than seeing her. Whenever he does, his huge face softens and breaks into a smile. He tells his son with satisfaction: "You're lucky I chose her for you." He praises her qualities: demure, with a pleasant smile and soft, thoughtful eyes, learned in running a household, able to read, and understanding her father's business almost as well as he does.

Already the huge red-bearded Dane feels like a father to her. He looks forward eagerly to when she will sit at his family table "where I can keep an eye on you, and make sure my son is looking after you properly," he tells her jovially. He even confides to his wife his plan to cancel Leofric's debt when the marriage takes place, showing how the match represents both familial affection and generous friendship.

The loss of this prospective daughter-in-law, and the manner of it—seeing her immediately betrothed to the son of Silversleeves, the Norman he had insulted—helps explain the epic fury of his destruction of his own home.

Alfred

Barnikel's relationship with the young smith Alfred demonstrates his ability to see beyond surface appearances and his generous nature. Where others see only a vagrant threatening the community, Barnikel recognizes a hero's heart. His declaration about Alfred—"The little fellow won't give up!"—shows what Barnikel values most: courage, determination, and refusal to surrender.

He takes the boy into his household and arranges his apprenticeship at the armoury. All summer, "it seemed to amuse the Dane to take the country boy about with him," teaching him about London's character and commerce. Alfred comes to feel "almost part of the family" despite being only a poor apprentice in a rich man's house. The boy becomes half in love with Hilda, showing how completely he's been welcomed into Barnikel's world.

This relationship continues after the conquest. Two days after William's coronation, Alfred encounters Barnikel in the East Cheap, and the Dane confides his quiet warning: "Wait and see." That he shares this with Alfred suggests the boy has become a trusted confidant despite his youth.

Silversleeves

Barnikel's relationship with Silversleeves is marked by a single encounter that has lasting consequences. At Harold II's coronation, flush with relief and triumph, Barnikel turns to the hooded Norman and growls contemptuously: "We have an English king, thank God. So keep your great French nose out of our business." He stomps out, leaving Leofric embarrassed.

This insult is more significant than Barnikel realizes. Silversleeves, who has suffered mockery about his nose since boyhood (when it cost him the girl he loved in Caen), does not forget. Though the narrator notes that Barnikel "had been peripheral to the game, a mere pawn," Silversleeves decides "he could be fitted in. The plan would take care of Barnikel quite beautifully, so perfect was its hidden symmetry."

Indeed, Silversleeves's scheme does take care of Barnikel. By offering to assume Leofric's debts (including the one to Barnikel) in exchange for his daughter's hand in marriage, and by making this offer when England's fate hangs in the balance, Silversleeves engineers a situation where Leofric can escape his promise to Barnikel. The fact that Hilda is immediately betrothed to Silversleeves's son after Barnikel's public rejection is not coincidence—it is the payback for that contemptuous growl outside Westminster Abbey.

Historical Context

Barnikel represents the successful integration of Danish and Saxon cultures in 11th-century London. His ancestor earned the family name two centuries before during the Viking raids of the 9th century. "Bairn ni Kel" ("Don't kill the children") was the unusual order of a Viking warrior who "disliked killing children"—a rare compassion that made him famous and gave his descendants a reputation that lasted five generations.

This heritage places Barnikel in the complex history of Viking settlement in England. After King Alfred fought off the Danish invasion, the Vikings still controlled the Danelaw north of the Thames, where Danish customs prevailed and peasants remained independent rather than becoming serfs. When Ethelred the Unready proved incapable of resisting a new wave of Danish attacks, England accepted King Canute as monarch. His long and exemplary reign brought "unity, peace and prosperity," and Danish families like the Barnikels were welcomed at his court.

The Barnikel family's position in London reflects this Anglo-Danish fusion. Three generations of Barnikels have served as captains of the city's Defence Guild. Barnikel's grandfather made three voyages to the Mediterranean and was created a nobleman for his service. This elevation from merchant to noble illustrates London's distinctive character: "A great merchant like Barnikel's grandfather, who had made three voyages to the Mediterranean, had been created a nobleman."

Barnikel's wealth comes from trade conducted at Billingsgate wharf, dealing in wool, grain, and fish. His blue cloak trimmed with ermine from "the Viking state of Russia" signals both his prosperity and his continuing connections to the wider Scandinavian trading world. His house on the eastern hill, with its great oak furniture and two-handed battle-axe on the wall, reflects Viking tradition adapted to urban prosperity.

As a member of London's Anglo-Danish merchant elite, Barnikel embodies the city's independence. London is "taxed like a whole shire" because of its wealth. It has won numerous liberties: trading concessions, fishing rights over miles of the Thames, hunting rights over Middlesex. Its Defence Guild can produce "a formidable force," and the city's walls are so mighty that "even King Canute had respected them." The merchant barons like Barnikel boast: "No invader can take London. And no king is king unless we say so."

This civic pride makes the Norman Conquest particularly bitter for Barnikel. At Hastings, "the tremendous two-handed axe blows from men like Barnikel" bit through Norman chain mail and caused the cavalry to flee, showing that the Anglo-Danish fighting tradition was still formidable. Yet William's superior tactics—trained bowmen, cavalry, and the strategic retreat that lured defenders into traps—ultimately prevailed.

After the battle, London's theoretical independence proves hollow. The city is "full of armed men, but there seemed little direction." The northern nobles talk of going home. The archbishop negotiates secretly. William doesn't attack the city but circles it, burning farms, wearing down resistance. When London finally accepts his terms, Barnikel's cry of "Traitor!" reflects not just personal hurt but the collapse of everything his family and the Anglo-Danish elite had built over two centuries.

Themes and Significance

The Tragedy of Fusion

Barnikel embodies the successful merger of Danish and Saxon cultures in London—a fusion that the Norman Conquest destroys. His friendship with Leofric, sealed by a marriage alliance between their children, represents this Anglo-Danish unity at its best. Yet under the pressure of conquest, this partnership fractures. The marriage is broken, the friendship shattered, and Barnikel is left raging impotently in his ruined hall.

This personal tragedy mirrors the larger historical loss. For two centuries, London had been a place where Danish and Saxon traders lived as "English," where the Folkmoot and Hustings combined Saxon and Danish traditions, where churches bore both Anglo-Saxon and Scandinavian names. Barnikel's story shows how the Norman Conquest swept away not just one culture but a unique hybrid civilization.

Passion Versus Prudence

Throughout the chapter, Barnikel's passionate directness contrasts with Leofric's caution and Silversleeves's calculation. Before Hastings, Barnikel cries "Strike now!" while Leofric urges attrition. After defeat, Barnikel roars "Why don't we strike again?" while others negotiate. His public declaration that his son won't marry a traitor's child is spoken in fury, not as careful policy.

The novel suggests that both approaches have merit and limitations. Barnikel's courage at Hastings nearly breaks the Norman cavalry, but his impulsive insult to Silversleeves gives his enemy motivation for revenge. His assumption that Leofric will understand his words as anger rather than final rejection shows an emotional honesty that is both noble and naive in a world of Norman political calculation.

The Destruction of Honor

Barnikel's story illustrates how conquest corrupts traditional bonds of honor. He has lent Leofric money in friendship and planned to secretly cancel the debt as a wedding gift—an act of pure generosity. Leofric has given his word that his daughter will marry Barnikel's son, and for men like them, a word given is sacred. But the Norman invasion creates pressures that break these bonds.

Leofric's estates are in danger; his debts threaten ruin. Silversleeves offers a way out, but only by breaking his promise to Barnikel. When Barnikel, in fury, publicly releases him from that promise, Leofric seizes the opportunity. The fact that he "might have known I didn't really mean it" makes Leofric's action not legally wrong but morally questionable—a betrayal of friendship's spirit if not its letter.

The novel leaves ambiguous whether Leofric or Barnikel is more at fault. Barnikel's public insult created the situation; Leofric exploited it. Both men act according to their natures—passionate versus prudent—but the conquest creates circumstances where these traits lead to rupture rather than accommodation.

Violence Turned Inward

The legendary destruction of Barnikel's hall provides a darkly comic yet poignant image. This man who fought heroically at Hastings, whose axe blows bit through Norman mail, who embodies Viking warrior tradition, ends not in glorious battle but smashing his own possessions in impotent fury. The neighbors who gather to watch, the old men who compare it favorably to anything in living memory, the catalog of destroyed goods—all this suggests a violence that has lost its proper outlet and target.

In the context of the conquest, this is particularly significant. William doesn't storm London; he wears it down. There's no climactic battle where men like Barnikel can test themselves against the enemy. Instead there are negotiations, compromises, and the slow realization that resistance is futile. Barnikel's rage, with nowhere to go, turns on his own household—a metaphor perhaps for how the conquest forces the English to destroy themselves.

Yet even in this destruction there's a kind of magnificence. The sheer scale of Barnikel's rage, the fact that it lasts over an hour, the breaking of a battle-axe shaft—all this suggests a spirit that refuses to be diminished even in defeat. His quiet words to Alfred—"Wait and see"—hint that this rage may yet find direction.

The Persistence of Character

Barnikel's rescue of Alfred early in the chapter establishes his character: generous, perceptive, valuing courage over circumstance. His declaration that "in this boy dwells the heart of a hero" because he "won't give up" reveals what Barnikel himself represents. Despite crushing defeat, betrayal by his friend, and the collapse of the Anglo-Danish world he knew, Barnikel doesn't give up either.

His survival of Hastings, fighting by the king's standard to the bitter end and emerging alive with his son, suggests more than luck. Men like Barnikel, with their tremendous strength and fierce determination, are hard to kill. The epic destruction of his hall shows that his spirit remains unbroken. And his quiet warning to Alfred—so different from his usual bellowing—suggests that he has learned something from defeat and may yet prove a force to be reckoned with in William's England.
