Overview

Harriet Dogget is the youngest of the four daughters of Silas Dogget, the self-made millionaire who rose from being a Thames dredger to a wealthy gentleman known as the "Guv'nor." Unlike her three older sisters who married after age thirty, Harriet married at twenty-three to a cautious, ambitious young man in the insurance business. She represents the comfortable middle ground of the Dogget daughters – neither as plain as her eldest sisters Charlotte and Esther, nor as vivacious and pretty as her younger sister Mary Anne.

Physical Description

No physical description provided in the text.

Character and Personality

Harriet is characterized by her contentment and practicality. She is sensible and patient, having waited to visit the Great Exhibition until she could go with her husband rather than rushing to see it with her sisters. This suggests a woman who values shared experiences with her spouse and is willing to delay gratification.

She is observant and perceptive, noticing the attraction developing between her sister Mary Anne and the young Colonel Meredith at the family dinner, even going so far as to murmur "Mary Anne, you are incorrigible" when her sister makes provocative statements about religion.

Harriet appears comfortable in her role as wife and mother, lacking the restlessness or rebellious streak of Mary Anne. She accepts the social conventions of her time and seems genuinely content with her marriage and social position.

Life and Events

Early Life (c. 1828-1851)

Harriet was the youngest of Silas Dogget's four daughters. By the time she was growing up, her father had already made his fortune through dust heaps and moved the family from humble beginnings to respectability. Unlike her eldest sister Charlotte, who could remember their father coming home "stinking from the boat" in their dingy Southwark lodgings, Harriet's childhood was spent in relative comfort.

While her older sisters Charlotte and Esther did not marry until they were over thirty, Harriet met her future husband when she was twenty-three. She was immediately attracted to the bespectacled young man with his "cautious, quiet, determined manner."

Marriage to Penny (c. 1851)

Harriet married Penny in approximately 1851, when she was twenty-three and he was twenty-one (two years her junior). Her husband's father, the banker Eugene Penny, had made handsome provision for all his children in several trusts, but young Penny had ambitions of his own in the insurance business.

The marriage was built on mutual respect and practical compatibility. While Penny did not need Harriet's fortune – his family was already wealthy – "it would never have occurred to him to marry a woman without a fortune, and she liked this about him too." This suggests Harriet appreciated his adherence to social conventions and his practical approach to marriage.

Unlike her older sisters Charlotte and Esther, who married "ambitious, younger men" apparently for their fortunes, Harriet's marriage appears to have been based on genuine attraction and compatibility, though still within the bounds of appropriate social expectations.

The Crystal Palace Visit (August 1851)

On a sunny August day in 1851, shortly before her father's birthday celebration, Harriet and her husband visited the Great Exhibition at the Crystal Palace. Though the exhibition had been open since May and her three sisters had already seen it, Harriet had waited to go with her husband. Her husband carefully calculated that they could afford "two hours and forty minutes of pleasure" before having to leave for the Guv'nor's birthday dinner at Blackheath.

The couple toured the exhibits arm in arm for two and a half hours, viewing the wonders from around the world. They marveled at the stuffed elephant from India, the Koh-i-noor diamond, Colonel Colt's revolvers, and countless other marvels. As they emerged back into Hyde Park, they looked at each other "with a mixture of amusement and trepidation" and wondered "what's happened to Mary Anne?" – showing Harriet's awareness of her sister's unpredictable nature.

The Guv'nor's Birthday Dinner (August 1851)

That same evening, Harriet attended her father's birthday dinner at his Georgian mansion in Blackheath. She was seated at the table with her husband, her three sisters and their husbands (Jonas Barnikel, Arnold Silversleeves, and Edward Bull), and young Colonel Meredith, whom Captain Barnikel had brought along.

During the dinner, Harriet observed the developing attraction between Mary Anne and Meredith. When Mary Anne made provocative statements about Hindu gods and other religions, Harriet gently chided her: "Mary Anne, you are incorrigible." She also witnessed Charlotte catching Mary Anne squeezing Meredith's hand as the ladies retired to the drawing room.

Her husband Penny also participated actively in the dinner conversation, particularly criticizing Meredith's plan to study Hindu religion in India. When Meredith spoke of spending months studying Hinduism, Penny remarked disapprovingly: "One does not just gad about for months, halfway round the globe, without some definite object, surely. Or are you travelling for pleasure?"

Later Life (1851-1889)

By 1889, Harriet and her husband had at least one son, referred to as "young Penny," who worked as an actuary for the Penny Insurance Company. The family continued to attend gatherings with the extended Dogget clan.

At a dinner party in 1889 or 1890, Harriet was present when the wealthy American Gorham Dogget of Boston inquired about families in London. When Mary Anne revealed that her maiden name was Dogget, Harriet "glanced at her nervously," suggesting she was concerned about the revelation of the family's humble origins. However, Mary Anne successfully navigated the situation by describing their father as "an investor."

When Gorham Dogget praised young Penny as "a sound, forward-looking young man" after hearing him enthusiastically describe the insurance business, Harriet must have felt pride in her son's accomplishments.

Soon after, the Bostonian invited young Penny to accompany his family on a three-month cruise down the Nile to keep his daughter Nancy company. Harriet excitedly told her sister Esther: "Just up sticks at the drop of a hat, take the boat from Southampton and be off for three months – down the Nile! I do believe he means our son to keep his daughter company. And he's going!" When Esther responded in awe, "We shall be getting quite above our station," it reflected the family's continued awareness of their social ascent.

Relationships

Penny (husband)

Harriet's marriage to Penny appears to have been stable and content. She was attracted to his "cautious, quiet, determined manner" and appreciated his practical approach to life and business. While he was two years younger than she – unusual for the time – their marriage seems to have been built on compatibility and shared values.

Penny was ambitious in the insurance business and appears to have been successful, as by 1889 the Penny Insurance Company was established enough to employ the younger Arnold Silversleeves son as an actuary. Penny's Calvinist and Scottish nature made him somewhat judgmental and cautious, as shown by his disapproval of Meredith's plans to study Hinduism.

Silas Dogget (father)

Harriet's relationship with her father appears respectful and dutiful. She attended his birthday celebrations and family gatherings, maintaining the family connections. Unlike Charlotte, who could remember their father's dredging days, Harriet had known only his prosperity and respectability, which likely made her relationship with him less complicated.

As the youngest daughter, Harriet received a substantial fortune from her father, sufficient to make her an attractive match even for a man who did not need to marry for money.

Sisters

Harriet maintained close relationships with her three sisters throughout her life. She observed Mary Anne with a mixture of affection and exasperation, understanding her sister's rebellious nature while gently trying to restrain her improprieties. Her comment "Mary Anne, you are incorrigible" shows both familiarity and resigned affection.

Mary Anne noted that "Harriet had remarked to her with a sigh: 'He always did let you get away with anything, Mary Anne,'" suggesting that Harriet was aware of the special place Mary Anne held in their father's affections.

Young Penny (son)

By 1889, Harriet had at least one son who worked in the family insurance business. She was proud of his accomplishments and excited about his opportunity to travel with the wealthy American Doggets, seeing it as a chance for social advancement and possibly a good match with Nancy Dogget.

Historical Context

Harriet's life spans the Victorian era at its height – from the late 1820s through at least the end of the 1880s. She witnessed and benefited from the massive expansion of the British Empire, the industrial revolution, and the rise of the middle class.

Her father Silas represented the Victorian ideal of self-made success – rising from absolute poverty (as a Thames dredger and dust heap operator) to wealth and respectability. Harriet's generation consolidated this wealth and respectability, marrying into established families and moving comfortably in middle-class and upper-middle-class society.

The 1851 Great Exhibition at the Crystal Palace, which Harriet attended, symbolized Britain's industrial and imperial dominance. The exhibition showcased British engineering prowess and the wealth flowing from the empire – the very forces that had enabled her father's rise and secured her own comfortable position.

Her husband's insurance business was part of the Victorian expansion of financial services. As Britain's economy grew more complex and middle-class wealth increased, insurance became increasingly important. The Penny Insurance Company represented this new form of middle-class entrepreneurship – neither manufacturing nor traditional banking, but providing professional services to the expanding commercial classes.

By the 1880s, when wealthy Americans like Gorham Dogget were making the Grand Tour and seeking to marry their children into British aristocracy, Harriet and her family had risen far enough that they could realistically aspire to such connections. Her son's invitation to travel with the Doggets and possibly court Nancy represented the ultimate validation of the family's social ascent.

Themes and Significance

Harriet represents the successful consolidation of newly acquired wealth. While her father Silas made the dramatic leap from poverty to riches, Harriet's role was to solidify that social position through an appropriate marriage and the maintenance of respectability. She is neither the plainest (Charlotte and Esther) nor the most beautiful (Mary Anne) of the Dogget daughters, and similarly, her life story is neither tragic nor dramatically exciting – but rather steady and successful.

Her contentment with her conventional role contrasts with Mary Anne's restlessness and rebellious streak. While Mary Anne chafed at the limitations of her position and briefly fantasized about adventure with Meredith, Harriet seems genuinely satisfied with her life as wife to an insurance man and mother to his heir.

The fact that Harriet waited to visit the Crystal Palace with her husband, rather than rushing to see it earlier with her sisters, illustrates her values: patience, propriety, and the importance of shared experiences within marriage. This patience and adherence to convention served her well, as her family continued to prosper and rise socially.

Harriet's nervous glance when Mary Anne revealed their Dogget surname to the American Gorham Dogget shows the family's ongoing awareness of their origins and the need to carefully manage their social presentation. Despite their wealth, the possibility of exposure – that someone might know about Silas's past as a dredger – remained a concern.

By the 1880s, with her son working in the family insurance business and being courted by wealthy Americans, Harriet had successfully fulfilled her social role: she had maintained and built upon her father's wealth, raised a respectable son, and positioned the next generation for continued success. She represents the Victorian middle class at its most stable and successful – neither dramatically rising nor falling, but steadily maintaining and modestly improving their position across generations.
