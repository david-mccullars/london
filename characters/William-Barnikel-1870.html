Overview

Admiral Sir William Barnikel is a larger-than-life naval commander who, during the Blitz of 1940, takes charge of a large part of the London Auxiliary Fire Service. Standing six foot three with a massive red beard and the chest "reminiscent of the prow of a battleship," he is exactly the descendant of Vikings his appearance suggests. His leadership style is loud, fearsome, and effective – chosen not for diplomacy but for his ability to raise morale among the beleaguered firefighters defending London.

Physical Description

Admiral Barnikel is an imposing physical presence. He stands six foot three with a chest described as reminiscent of a battleship's prow. His most distinctive feature is his huge red beard, which appears almost to be on fire itself when illuminated by flames. His overall appearance is that of a Viking descendant, which the text confirms he is. His great form and commanding presence make him seem even larger than life, particularly when silhouetted against walls of flame during the Blitz.

Character and Personality

Barnikel embodies the archetype of the fearless naval commander whose bellowing voice could "astound a frigate." He is not diplomatic – as bureaucrats gently note, he is "not always diplomatic" – but Winston Churchill himself recognized that what was needed was not diplomacy but "a man to raise morale," and Barnikel's "mighty heart and mighty temper" were perfect for the task.

Despite his intimidating presence and volcanic temper, Barnikel is fundamentally compassionate and deeply concerned about the welfare of those under his command. His surprise inspections demonstrate this care: he doesn't merely check uniforms and equipment but personally ensures his men are properly fed. When he discovers that firefighters at Crystal Palace have been supplied with spoiled corned beef, his fury is genuine and protective. He doesn't just complain – he personally hurls the tainted meat away (reportedly it might have landed in Berlin), orders proper food brought immediately, and gives Percy Fleming direct authority to call him personally if such negligence occurs again.

His leadership style combines intimidation with genuine warmth. He roars commands, pounds tables hard enough to break their legs, and can twist a fork almost into a knot when angry. Yet he also joins in singing "Nellie Dean" with the men, personally commends Herbert's piano playing, claps men on the back, and eats supper with the crews. His bellowing declarations that the men are heroes are delivered with evident sincerity.

Barnikel understands what motivates men. From his naval experience, he knows that "a well-fed ship is a contented ship," and he applies this wisdom to the fire service. He recognizes that many firefighters still feel that "nobody really cares about them," and every aspect of his conduct is designed to prove otherwise.

Life and Events

1940: Commander of the London Auxiliary Fire Service

By September 1940, when Hitler's Blitzkrieg begins devastating London, Admiral Sir William Barnikel has been placed in charge of a large portion of the London Auxiliary Fire Service. The authorities take what they acknowledge is a "calculated risk" in this appointment, given his lack of diplomatic skills, but Churchill's endorsement proves decisive.

Barnikel conducts surprise inspections of fire stations throughout his domain, arriving unannounced "as was his habit." During one such visit to the Crystal Palace substation, he transforms what could have been a terrifying inspection into a morale-building experience. He commands more sandbags, joins the singing, insists Herbert's piano be tuned, inspects uniforms and boots, and produces a fresh helmet when one proves defective.

His inspection of the kitchen provisions, however, triggers his legendary temper. Upon discovering greenish, spoiled corned beef, he explodes in protective fury. His response is characteristically dramatic: he twists a fork almost into a knot, pounds the table so hard a leg falls off, hurls the entire tray of corned beef over the station roof, and immediately orders proper food sent from headquarters – offering his own supper if necessary. He then entrusts Percy Fleming with direct authority to contact him personally if such negligence occurs again, demonstrating that his rage is not merely theatrical but grounded in genuine concern.

On the night of the conflagration, Barnikel continues his rounds through the burning city. When he comes upon St Paul's and observes what he believes is an act of extraordinary heroism – Charlie Dogget descending into a bomb crater containing an unexploded bomb to rescue an unconscious Neville Silversleeves – the admiral doesn't hesitate. Standing on the crater's edge with a wall of flame behind him making his red beard appear to be on fire, he thunders his approval: "Well done that man! By God, you deserve a medal! Hold fast there. I'm coming!"

True to character, Barnikel immediately strides down into the crater himself to help, demonstrating the physical courage he expects from his men. Together with Charlie, he hauls Silversleeves to safety, flags down a passing ambulance, and wisely gets them all away from the area moments before the bomb explodes. His recommendation of Charlie for the George Cross follows naturally from his belief in rewarding genuine courage.

Relationships

Percy and Herbert Fleming

Though Barnikel likely meets the Fleming brothers only during his single inspection visit to their station, he makes a significant impact on their lives. His fury over the spoiled corned beef protects not just them but all the men under his command. His specific trust placed in Percy – telling him to call directly if food issues arise again – elevates an ordinary auxiliary volunteer to a position of genuine authority. His genuine appreciation of Herbert's piano playing ("Best I've heard in any station") and his promise to return and sing together show his ability to recognize and value individual contributions.

Charlie Dogget

Barnikel encounters Charlie at the moment he believes the fireman is performing an act of supreme gallantry. His immediate personal intervention – descending into the crater himself to assist in the rescue – demonstrates that he leads from the front rather than merely commanding from safety. His insistence on taking Charlie's name and station, and his subsequent recommendation for the George Cross, show his determination to ensure valor is properly recognized and rewarded.

Historical Context

Admiral Barnikel represents the British naval tradition at a crucial moment when that expertise is being redirected to meet the unprecedented challenge of the Blitz. His appointment to lead firefighters rather than ships reflects the desperate adaptation of all British institutions to total war. The Auxiliary Fire Service itself had suffered from poor morale and even suggestions that volunteers had joined to avoid military service, but the Blitz was proving their courage – and leaders like Barnikel were essential to sustaining their spirit.

The admiral's leadership style – loud, physical, unapologetically masculine, and hierarchical – embodies the Royal Navy's centuries-old culture. His Viking appearance links him to the ancient warrior traditions that had shaped both the Barnikel family and British maritime power. Yet his genuine care for his men's welfare and his insistence on proper provisions show the paternal responsibilities that the best British commanders traditionally accepted.

His presence during the Blitz also represents the British establishment's recognition that this was an existential struggle requiring the nation's best leaders, regardless of age. That an elderly admiral would be personally present in burning London, striding into bomb craters beside unexploded ordnance, embodied the "we're all in this together" spirit that sustained British morale.

Themes and Significance

Admiral Barnikel embodies the continuity of British warrior culture from Viking raiders through naval dominance to the defense of London itself. His physical presence – the red beard, the massive build, the Viking appearance – makes him a living link to the Barnikel family's ancient origins. The text explicitly notes his descent from Vikings, and his character seems to channel that martial heritage into modern service.

He represents leadership through personal presence and genuine concern rather than bureaucratic distance. In contrast to the officials who supply spoiled food and the bureaucrats who worry about his lack of diplomacy, Barnikel actually leads – showing up unannounced, eating with his men, descending into bomb craters. His "mighty heart" proves more valuable than diplomatic skill.

The admiral also symbolizes the adaptation of British institutions to total war. Naval expertise applied to firefighting, elderly officers in active danger, personal leadership in chaotic conditions – all reflect Britain's improvisational response to Hitler's unprecedented assault. That this adaptation works, that Barnikel does raise morale and does inspire courage, suggests both the resilience of British military culture and its relevance even in radically changed circumstances.

His modesty about his family origins – acknowledging his grandfather Jonas was "an ordinary sea captain" and that before that they were "common fishmongers" – reflects both genuine humility and ironic understatement, since he lacks knowledge of the ancient importance of the Fishmongers Guild. This connects him to the novel's broader themes about London families rising, falling, and rising again across centuries, with their true significance sometimes unknown to themselves.
