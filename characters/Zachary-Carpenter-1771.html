Overview

Zachary Carpenter is a successful furniture maker and passionate political radical who becomes a prominent voice for reform in early 19th-century London. Operating from St Pancras, he is known for his fiery oratory calling for democratic rights and representation. His unexpected political alliance with the conservative Lord Bocton helps advance the cause of parliamentary reform, making him a significant figure in the movement that culminated in the Great Reform Bill of 1832.

Physical Description

Zachary is described as having "a small stout body and large round head." His physical appearance during speeches is characterized by energetic movement - when emphasizing points, his body and head "fairly bounced."

Character and Personality

Zachary is a fervent idealist driven by a passion for political justice rather than religious conviction. Unlike his father who was a religious reformer, Zachary "sought purity, but he wanted to find it in the institutions of men." He is an accomplished orator with a distinctive rhetorical style, particularly his emphatic use of the word "acknowledge" as a trademark flourish.

As a businessman, Zachary demonstrates pragmatism alongside his idealism. While fighting for the rights of working men, he runs a successful furniture workshop employing journeymen, apprentices, and child workers. He justifies this practice by arguing that until schools exist, he at least keeps children from starving or entering the workhouse. This reveals the complex moral compromises of even reform-minded employers in the era.

Politically, Zachary evolves from a pure radical to a more moderate position. While he once would have supported universal male suffrage, by his late fifties he has doubts about whether all men, regardless of condition, are ready for the vote. He settles on the belief that "men who pay taxes should vote" - solid citizens like himself - representing the middle-class reformist position rather than pure democracy.

Zachary is also loyal and trusting, maintaining a friendship with the eccentric Earl St James and eventually being manipulated by Lord Bocton into helping assess the earl's mental state.

Life and Events

1789: Political Awakening

At age eighteen, Zachary witnesses the outbreak of the French Revolution with its promise of Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity. This event profoundly shapes his political consciousness.

1792: Joining the Radical Movement

At age twenty-one, within a week of reading Tom Paine's "The Rights of Man" with its demand for "One Man One Vote," Zachary joins the London Corresponding Society, a network providing organization for radicals across England.

1796: Emerging as a Speaker

By age twenty-five, Zachary gains recognition as an orator, beginning a lifelong career of public speaking for radical causes.

1819: Speech at Fitzroy Square

In 1819, Zachary delivers a rousing speech from the window of the Earl of St James's house in Fitzroy Square to a crowd of four hundred. He invokes the Rights of Man, condemns the closing of the St Pancras vestry, and draws parallels between the treatment of parish residents and slavery. His presence at an aristocrat's house, speaking with the earl's support, creates a striking spectacle.

During this period, Zachary operates a successful furniture-making business in St Pancras with a workshop employing three journeymen and two apprentices, specializing in davenports (miniature domestic writing desks). He had previously served his apprenticeship with Chippendale and worked briefly as a journeyman for Sheraton.

After the speech, Lord Bocton confronts Zachary and his father, warning that they risk provoking a riot in the tense atmosphere following the Peterloo Massacre. Lord Bocton views Zachary as a dangerous agitator, though the two men engage in a philosophical debate about the meaning of "the people" and revolution.

1822: Business Expansion

By 1822, Zachary's furniture business has grown significantly. He employs ten journeymen and an extra apprentice, with his total workforce twice that number due to the use of child labor. Among these children are Lucy Dogget and her brother Horatio Dogget. Zachary makes an exception to his usual practice of not employing children under seven, allowing little Horatio to sweep wood shavings for a farthing.

During this same period, Zachary protests the construction of St Pancras's expensive new Grecian-style church, declaring "God will not be troubled by any prayers from poor people in there." When the church's cost forces the vestry to triple parish taxes, and the Earl of St James refuses to pay, Zachary supports his aristocratic friend's defiant stand.

1829: Approaching Lord Bocton

Around 1829, Zachary speaks at a hall in St Pancras, using Buckingham Palace as his proof of governmental corruption and waste. He has spent "half a lifetime agitating for reform" by this point and privately believes he is "wasting his time," though he continues to speak with eloquence.

To his astonishment, the conservative Lord Bocton approaches him after the speech, declaring agreement and revealing he intends to stand for Parliament on a platform of reform. The two men, former enemies, strike a political deal. Bocton, frightened by the Catholic Emancipation Act and fearing chaos, has decided that "sensible reform is better than chaos."

Zachary is somewhat manipulated during this meeting. Bocton subtly asks him to report on the Earl of St James's mental state, suggesting the family worries the earl may not be "quite sound of mind." Zachary defends the earl, though he privately notes, "For a lord."

1830-1831: Electoral Campaign

The year 1830 brings revolutionary upheaval across Europe and riots in England. For Zachary, it brings "growing excitement." He watches the formation of early trade unions in northern England and sees them as signs of coming change.

When King William IV succeeds to the throne and Wellington calls an election, Zachary and Bocton contest the St Pancras seat together. Their campaign tactic is simple: Bocton speaks at public meetings alongside the establishment Tory candidate, agrees with everything said, then declares "But unfortunately, it won't work" before painting dire pictures of revolution. He concludes: "Revolution or reform. The choice is yours."

Meanwhile, Zachary tells radicals and reformers: "Bocton's a Tory but he's seen the light. He's our best bet. Vote for him."

During one of Bocton's speeches, Zachary encounters the elderly Earl of St James, now in his mid-eighties and showing signs of decline. When Zachary cheerfully asks if the earl has come to support his son, St James explodes: "Support Bocton? That traitor? I'm damned if I will!"

Lord Bocton wins the St Pancras seat with a large majority. Across the country, reformers sweep contested seats. Zachary declares: "I do believe the tide is turning."

As the Swing Riots continue through the autumn, terrifying the countryside, the Duke of Wellington's government falls. Bocton personally rides to Zachary's workshop to deliver the news: "The king is sending for the Whigs, Mr Carpenter. You have your reform."

1831-1832: The Great Reform Bill

On March 23, 1831, Zachary witnesses the historic vote in the House of Commons that passes the Great Reform Bill by exactly one vote - with Bocton claiming it was his vote that made the difference. When a wrecking amendment destroys the bill, Zachary correctly predicts the Whigs will win the subsequent election and reform will become inevitable.

During the new election, Zachary and Bocton discover that the Earl of St James has bought a rotten borough seat for his grandson George without consulting Bocton. When confronted, the confused earl seems vague about whether George will even vote for reform, asking instead about betting odds on the election and remarking he hasn't been to the races "for some time" - further evidence of his declining mental state.

The Great Reform Bill finally passes into law in the summer of 1832, abolishing rotten boroughs, giving representation to new industrial towns, and extending the vote to the middle class. Zachary's decades of agitation have finally succeeded, though women remain entirely excluded from the franchise.

Relationships

Earl St James

Zachary maintains an unlikely friendship with this wealthy, pleasure-loving aristocrat. The earl allows Zachary to deliver radical speeches from his Fitzroy Square house and supports his causes. Zachary calls him "my lord" and defends him when Lord Bocton suggests his father may be going mad, though privately he adds the qualification "For a lord." In later years, Zachary sees less of the earl and observes his decline with regret.

Lord Bocton

Initially antagonists, these two men forge a pragmatic political alliance. Bocton views the people as a dangerous mob and Zachary as an agitator, while Zachary sees Bocton as a reactionary. However, shared fear of revolution and chaos brings them together. Bocton manipulates Zachary somewhat, using him to gather intelligence about his father's mental state. Nevertheless, their partnership proves effective in advancing reform, with Bocton providing respectability and Zachary delivering radical votes.

Lucy Dogget and Horatio Dogget

Zachary employs these siblings as child workers in his furniture workshop. He makes an exception to his usual age limit for sickly young Horatio, allowing him to sweep wood shavings for small payments. While he justifies child labor as preventing starvation, he acknowledges "they ought to be in schools."

Historical Context

Zachary's life spans one of the most tumultuous periods in British political history. Born in 1771, he comes of age during the American Revolution's aftermath and the French Revolution's outbreak. His father's religious reformism connects him to the Wesleyan movement, while his ancestor Gideon Carpenter's career as a Roundhead links him to England's Civil War radical tradition.

The political landscape of Zachary's adulthood is shaped by reaction to the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars. The government's fear of radicalism leads to repression of reform movements. The Peterloo Massacre of 1819, where troops charge a crowd and kill over a dozen people, exemplifies the tensions between reformers and authorities. The context also includes the Catholic Emancipation Act, the Swing Riots of 1830, and economic hardship following the Napoleonic Wars.

Zachary's career as a cabinetmaker places him in a traditional London craft, working in St Pancras where many furniture makers operated. His training with Chippendale and Sheraton connects him to the great names of English furniture making. His use of child labor, while troubling to modern sensibilities, was standard practice in the era before compulsory education.

The parish vestry system that Zachary protests represented a genuine democratic concern. When St Pancras changed from an "open" vestry (elected by householders) to a "select" vestry (self-nominating), it removed local control. Similarly, the "rotten boroughs" that Zachary campaigns against allowed tiny populations to send members to Parliament while growing industrial cities had no representation.

The Great Reform Bill of 1832, which Zachary helps achieve, is a watershed in British democracy. While it doesn't establish universal suffrage (excluding the working class and all women), it breaks the aristocratic monopoly on Parliament and establishes the principle that representation should reflect population and property rather than ancient privilege.

Themes and Significance

Zachary embodies the English radical tradition stretching from the Civil War Levellers through the American and French Revolutions to 19th-century reform movements. His family history - from Gideon the Roundhead through his own father's religious dissent to his political activism - illustrates how radical ideas persist across generations while changing form.

His character explores the contradictions of middle-class reformism. Zachary fights for "the people" while employing child labor, advocates for rights while excluding the poor and women from his vision of who should vote, and partners with a conservative aristocrat to achieve reform. These contradictions aren't personal failings but reflect the limited nature of 19th-century middle-class radicalism, which sought to expand rights to property owners like themselves rather than establish universal democracy.

The unlikely alliance between Zachary and Lord Bocton demonstrates how political change often results from strange coalitions formed under pressure. Bocton doesn't embrace reform out of conviction but out of fear - fear that without moderate reform, revolution will sweep away everything. This pragmatic conservatism combines with Zachary's idealistic radicalism to achieve what neither could accomplish alone.

Zachary's story also illustrates the long, frustrating process of political change. He spends decades agitating with little apparent success, convinced at times he is "wasting his time." Yet his persistence, combined with historical forces beyond any individual's control (European revolutions, economic crisis, the Swing Riots), eventually bears fruit. The Great Reform Bill of 1832 doesn't create the pure democracy Zachary once envisioned, but it represents genuine progress toward representative government.

Finally, Zachary represents the craftsman-intellectual, a figure characteristic of London radicalism. His furniture-making workshop grounds him in the practical world of commerce and labor, while his reading of Paine and his oratorical skills connect him to Enlightenment ideas. This combination of manual skill and intellectual engagement typifies the artisan radicals who drove much of 19th-century reform.
