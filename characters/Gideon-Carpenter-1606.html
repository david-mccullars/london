Overview

Gideon Carpenter is a devout Puritan craftsman who becomes a soldier in Cromwell's New Model Army and a moral leader in his London parish during the Commonwealth period. Named by his aunt Martha Carpenter after the biblical warrior for the Lord, he embodies the militant Protestant fervor of mid-17th century England. His life is marked by conflict with the Royalist Julius Ducket, service in the Civil War as one of Cromwell's "Saints," and his efforts to establish godly rule in the parish of St Lawrence Silversleeves during the Commonwealth.

Physical Description

Gideon has steady, brown eyes that are frequently noted in the text. He is described as a "solid craftsman" and "a sober citizen." In his youth he is whipped with a cat o' nine tails for insulting the Spanish Ambassador, a punishment severe enough to kill one of his fellow apprentices, though he survives.

Character and Personality

Gideon is characterized by stern religious conviction and a deep sense of moral righteousness. He becomes increasingly zealous in his Puritan faith as he matures, developing into what is described as "sternly religious." His personality combines the practical skills of a craftsman with the ideological fervor of a religious revolutionary.

He displays remarkable patience and strategic thinking in his conflicts with Julius Ducket, giving his opponent "a chance" before taking action - a measured approach that contrasts with his otherwise rigid moral stance. During the Civil War, he finds his calling among Cromwell's New Model Army, identifying deeply with the "Saints" who saw themselves not just as soldiers but as warriors for Christ building a new godly order.

Despite his strict morality, Gideon shows human complexity. He tolerates his wife's strategic "sickness" to avoid emigrating to America, and he keeps the secret of John Dogget and Jane Fleming's relationship for years before being forced to reveal it. His embarrassment when confronting this situation suggests an uncomfortable awareness that rigid morality can conflict with human relationships.

Life and Events

Early Life (c. 1606-1620s)

Gideon is born around 1606, the first son of Cuthbert Carpenter, after an elder daughter. His name is chosen by his aunt Martha Carpenter, who declares "Call him Gideon. For he was a warrior for the Lord" - a prophetic choice given his later military service. His father Cuthbert had been a theatre-goer until Martha persuaded him to swear off the playhouse in exchange for housing the family.

1615: The Apprentice Incident and Whipping

As a young apprentice, Gideon participates in an incident where he and other apprentices insult the Spanish Ambassador. Julius Ducket witnesses the event and, pressured by his brother Henry Ducket, reports the culprits to the authorities. The apprentices are publicly whipped with the cat o' nine tails - a brutal punishment. One of them dies from the whipping. Gideon survives but bears both physical and emotional scars from this injustice.

From that day forward, Gideon fixes Julius with an implacable gaze whenever they meet at church, creating a lasting enmity. This incident becomes a defining moment in his life and Julius's guilty conscience.

1616: Father's Death and New Business

Shortly after Martha Carpenter and John Dogget return from their voyage to Virginia, Gideon's father Cuthbert suddenly dies. Gideon suggests that he and Dogget go into business together. They find lodgings and establish a small workshop near the top of Garlic Hill, where they repair anything people bring them. Though Dogget misses his boats, they keep busy with this work.

Late 1620s: Marriage and Family

Gideon marries around 1628 or 1629 (described as married "the previous year" in a scene set around 1629). His wife, whose name is never given, proves to be a shrewd woman who strategically develops "sickness" whenever the subject of emigrating to America arises. Martha notes that "She is exactly sick enough not to travel."

The couple has five daughters in succession: Charity, Hope, Faith, Patience, and finally Perseverance - each name expressing the family's mounting frustration at not having a son. The daughters arrive with "monotonous regularity, every two years."

1629-1630: Confronting High Church Practices

When Bishop Laud's reforms reach St Lawrence Silversleeves, Gideon and Martha are summoned to church by order of Henry Ducket and the vestry. Witnessing the vicar Edmund Meredith in full vestments and the altar moved to the chancel, Gideon is horrified by what he sees as popery.

After the service, Gideon confronts Julius, challenging the authority of the vestry committee: "If the congregation elected the vestry, we should have godly men there and a godly minister. You sit in the vestry as though by Divine Right. You have no right. You are imposed upon us." This represents his emerging belief in congregational church governance.

1636: Birth of O Be Joyful

At the very end of 1636, Gideon's wife finally gives birth to a son. The family's joy is so great that Martha suggests the unusual Puritan name "O Be Joyful" - an entire biblical phrase rather than a traditional name. The boy is baptized O Be Joyful Carpenter.

Recognizing his son's talent for woodcarving, Gideon later apprentices him to the finest woodcarver he can find rather than taking him into his own repair business.

1637: Martha's Departure to America

Martha, driven to emigration by witnessing William Prynne's brutal punishment and her own public protest, prepares to sail to Massachusetts. Gideon and his family cannot yet travel - his wife uses their infant son as an excuse, knowing that no one would risk O Be Joyful on a dangerous sea voyage during his most vulnerable years. It's agreed that John Dogget will remain in London to help Gideon with their business.

1640-1641: Parliamentary Crisis and Political Awakening

During the Long Parliament crisis, Gideon becomes politically active. He collects signatures for a petition demanding "the abolition of bishops and all their works, root and branch" - gathering nearly fifteen thousand signatures. When he brings it to Julius, he tells him: "When you had me whipped, you didn't give me a chance. But I'm giving you one."

Later, during the Strafford crisis, Gideon demonstrates political sophistication. When Julius suggests the king won't execute Strafford, Gideon smiles and asks "Which king?" He explains: "There are two kings now. King Charles in his palace, and King Pym in the Commons... King Pym will have it so." Julius is shocked to hear this radical language, but within days it becomes common parlance.

1641: Summer at Bocton

In the summer of 1641, Julius brings several parish families to Henry Ducket's estate at Bocton for hop-picking, including Gideon's wife and children. In the peaceful Kent countryside, even the austere Henry strikes up a friendship with little O Be Joyful as the child toddles about in the sun - an ironic moment of harmony before the coming storm.

1641-1642: Raising the Militia

As tensions escalate toward civil war, Gideon becomes involved in raising Parliament's militia. By November 1641, he is collecting signatures for another petition, boasting "We'll get twenty thousand this time." By April 1642, Julius sees Gideon "solemnly carrying a halberd, leading a little troop of apprentices who were marching down Cheapside."

In late summer 1642, Gideon cheerfully tells Julius: "We're all Roundheads now" - using the derisive term the Royalists had coined for the close-cropped Parliamentary supporters. Soon after, the king raises his standard at Nottingham, and the Civil War begins.

1642-1645: Civil War Service

Gideon serves in the London trained bands at the start of the war. Jane Fleming recalls seeing "Gideon and his men marching proudly off to Finsbury Field or the Artillery Ground outside Moorgate where the city's trained bands would gather" for training, followed by the rattle of musketry and banging of cannon. Great columns of Roundhead troops would depart and return "dusty and bandaged, a few weeks later."

1645: Joining the New Model Army

When Cromwell forms the New Model Army, Gideon eagerly joins this full-time, trained, and disciplined force. The New Model Army becomes more than just a military organization to him - it represents a religious brotherhood. These soldiers call themselves "Saints" and see themselves as fighting to build God's kingdom on earth.

For Gideon, what matters is that "these New Model Roundheads were not just soldiers. They were saints." He identifies completely with these men who "sought justice, soldiers for Christ, men who were fighting so that now at last, even the humble and the meek of the world might achieve the inheritance that the Lord had promised them." They believe God has given them victory and therefore authority to reshape England.

Gideon becomes increasingly radical through his army service, inspired by Martha's letters from Massachusetts describing congregational church government and biblical law. He envisions creating in England what Massachusetts represents - "that shining city on a hill."

1649: The King's Execution

On the night before Charles I's execution, Gideon sits down to write a letter to Martha in Massachusetts. He hesitates repeatedly, uncertain how much to tell her about what is to come. He wrestles with his conscience over the "terrible deed of the coming morning" but ultimately believes the "Saints" are entering the promised land.

Julius notes that after Gideon's arrest of him (as a "Malignant"), he was held with three dozen other prominent Royalist citizens. This shows Gideon's active role in suppressing Royalist sympathizers during the trial period.

1649-1652: Commonwealth Rule and Parish Leadership

With the establishment of the Commonwealth, Gideon's role transforms. The congregation of St Lawrence Silversleeves, now free to elect their own vestry, chooses Gideon as a vestryman three times. He becomes the moral authority in the parish, setting standards for the community.

He continues to keep the secret that John Dogget and Jane Fleming are romantically involved, though this troubles his conscience. He tells himself he hasn't actually seen them together, though he has seen them kissing when they thought themselves alone.

1652: Christmas Raid on the Duckets

On Christmas Day 1652, Gideon leads a troop of Roundhead soldiers to raid Julius Ducket's house. The crime: celebrating Christmas dinner. Under Commonwealth law, holy days should be times for solemn prayer, not festivals. Gideon declares in disgust: "You have profaned the Holy Day."

He orders his troops to search the house for "superstitious images" and "evidence of popery." For half an hour, the Roundheads search every room, opening cupboards and chests, turning over mattresses, even searching the cellar. They find nothing incriminating. Julius, furious at the violation, follows them around remarking contemptuously: "I just want to make sure none of you steals anything."

1652: The Adultery Trial

When Martha returns from Massachusetts around 1651-1652, she initially finds a "joyous homecoming" with Gideon and his family. Martha is thrilled with the Commonwealth, believing they can build "that shining city on a hill" in England even more than in Massachusetts.

However, Martha soon becomes suspicious about John Dogget and Jane Fleming. She directly challenges Gideon, who finally admits: "I do not know, but I think it is so." When pressed about whether the relationship is ongoing, he says "Perhaps." He has known for years but couldn't bring himself to act on it.

Martha organizes a raid during one of Edmund Meredith's sermons. She and Gideon, along with a neighbor as witness, catch Dogget and Jane together in bed. The evidence is incontrovertible. Gideon appears embarrassed during the discovery - understanding the human dimensions of the situation even while supporting Martha's moral crusade.

The subsequent trial of Dogget and Jane for adultery (a capital crime under Commonwealth law) takes place in the Guildhall. Despite Gideon's testimony and that of the other witnesses, the jury acquits them - reflecting ordinary citizens' unwillingness to hang people for adultery. Though legally exonerated, the couple is driven from the parish by community pressure, eventually moving to Petty France.

1650s: The Later Commonwealth Years

During the remainder of the Commonwealth period, Gideon continues to rule the parish of St Lawrence Silversleeves alongside Martha. Even Julius notes that "Martha and Gideon ruled the parish" and acts carefully to avoid provoking them.

The text indicates Gideon continues to correspond with Martha about building the godly commonwealth. However, like many of the Saints, he likely experiences growing disillusionment as Cromwell's constitutional experiments fail and the promise of building a new Jerusalem proves elusive.

1660: The Restoration

When Charles II returns to the throne in 1660, the power of Gideon and the Saints collapses. The text ends with the chapter, but Gideon is still alive at this point. His fate after the Restoration is not detailed in this chapter, though we know from Chapter 14 (St Paul's) that he is remembered as a heroic figure by his descendants.

Later Life (from Chapter 14)

In Chapter 14 (St Paul's), which covers 1675-1708, Gideon appears only briefly as an old man, still alive in the later period. His grandson O Be Joyful references "old Gideon and Martha" and recalls how the grandchildren would ask him to "Tell us how Gideon fought with Cromwell against the king."

Gideon's memory becomes legendary in the family - he is remembered alongside Martha as the heroic founders of the Carpenter family's Puritan tradition. His descendants name their children after him, including little Gideon Carpenter born in 1670, showing the reverence in which he is held.

By the time of Queen Anne (early 1700s), O Be Joyful recalls that Gideon and Martha "had departed, but in a sense they had returned" through the new generation of strictly raised Puritan children bearing their names. The text suggests Gideon represents an idealized image of godly strength and conviction that subsequent generations struggle to live up to.

Relationships

Martha Carpenter

Martha is Gideon's aunt, but she functions as his spiritual mother and guide. She names him "Gideon" and shapes his religious development from childhood. Their relationship is one of mutual devotion to Puritan principles. When Martha emigrates to America, they maintain correspondence, with her letters from Massachusetts inspiring his vision for godly rule in England.

When she returns around 1651-1652, Gideon welcomes her with joy, seeing her as a "beacon, steadfastly shining across the ocean" whose moral guidance is essential to building the new commonwealth. He works closely with her to enforce moral standards in the parish, though he is more conflicted about some of their actions than she is.

Julius Ducket

Gideon's relationship with Julius is defined by an injustice that can never be forgotten. After Julius reports Gideon and other apprentices for insulting the Spanish Ambassador, leading to a brutal whipping that kills one of Gideon's companions, Gideon fixes Julius with an implacable stare whenever they meet.

Yet Gideon's approach to Julius is complex. Rather than seeking immediate vengeance, he gives Julius multiple chances - bringing him the petition to sign, warning him about political changes. He tells Julius: "When you had me whipped, you didn't give me a chance. But I'm giving you one."

During the Commonwealth, Gideon has complete power over Julius, arresting him as a Malignant, raiding his house, and searching it for evidence of popery. Yet even in these actions, there's a sense that Gideon sees himself as executing impersonal justice rather than personal revenge. Their relationship embodies the larger conflict between Puritan and Royalist England.

His Wife

Gideon's relationship with his wife is marked by restraint and duty rather than romance. She is described only in functional terms - the mother of his children and a shrewd woman who manipulates the family through strategic "sickness" to avoid emigrating to America.

The text notes that Gideon recognizes his wife's manipulation but tolerates it. When she finally produces a son, O Be Joyful, "Gideon's wife could now relax," having secured her position and prevented the dangerous Atlantic voyage. Their marriage appears to be one of practical accommodation rather than deep partnership.

O Be Joyful Carpenter

Gideon's relationship with his son is characterized by pride and religious devotion. He recognizes O Be Joyful's talent for woodcarving and apprentices him to a master craftsman rather than keeping him in the family business - showing both ambition for his son and practical wisdom.

The text notes that O Be Joyful has "such a sweet and religious nature" that Gideon is deeply grateful to God. He sees his son as evidence of divine favor. When Gideon sits down to write to Martha before the king's execution, he glances at "the light of his life, O Be Joyful, a short, stocky youth now, reading a bible."

John Dogget

Gideon works in partnership with Dogget after his father's death, establishing their repair business together. He appears to genuinely respect and care for Dogget. When he becomes aware of Dogget's relationship with Jane Fleming, he keeps the secret for years despite his position as a moral leader in the parish.

When Martha finally forces him to reveal what he knows, Gideon appears "embarrassed" during the confrontation. This suggests genuine conflict between his affection for Dogget and his duty to enforce moral standards. After Dogget and Jane are driven from the parish, Gideon presumably accepts this as necessary but may carry some guilt about it.

Historical Context

Gideon's life spans one of the most tumultuous periods in English history. Born during the final years of Elizabeth I's successor James I, he comes of age during the escalating religious and political conflicts of Charles I's reign. His formative experience - being whipped for a minor offense against Spanish diplomatic dignity - exemplifies the arbitrary exercise of power that fueled resentment against the Stuart monarchy.

His religious formation occurs during the height of Archbishop Laud's campaign to impose high church practices on England's parishes. For Puritans like Gideon, Laud's reforms - moving altars, requiring vestments, emphasizing ceremony - represented a betrayal of the Protestant Reformation and a slide back toward Catholicism. This period radicalized many English Protestants.

The English Civil War (1642-1651) was fought between supporters of Parliament and the king, but it was also fundamentally a religious conflict. Gideon represents the Puritan vision that Parliament's victory could create a godly commonwealth. The New Model Army, which he joined, became a vehicle for radical religious and political ideas. Its soldiers genuinely believed they were building a new Jerusalem.

The execution of Charles I in 1649 was an unprecedented act - killing an anointed monarch shocked Europe. Gideon's participation in this revolutionary moment shows how completely he embraced the radical transformation of English society. The Commonwealth period (1649-1660) attempted to create a Puritan state governed by biblical law, but it ultimately failed due to internal divisions and its inability to win popular support.

The Massachusetts Bay Colony, which inspires both Martha and Gideon, represented a Puritan experiment in creating a godly community. Its system of congregational church government and rule by elected "Saints" provided a model for what Gideon hoped to achieve in England.

The collapse of the Commonwealth in 1660 and Charles II's restoration represented the complete defeat of Gideon's vision. The Restoration brought back bishops, the Book of Common Prayer, and royal authority - everything the Saints had fought against.

Themes and Significance

Religious Radicalism and Revolution

Gideon embodies the transformation of English Puritanism from a reform movement within the Church of England to a revolutionary force willing to kill a king and remake society. His journey from craftsman to soldier to parish ruler illustrates how religious conviction could drive political revolution in the 17th century.

The Cost of Moral Absolutism

While Gideon fights for what he believes is God's justice, the novel shows the human cost of his moral certainty. His raids on Julius's house on Christmas, his role in driving Dogget and Jane from the parish, and his enforcement of rigid moral codes reveal how idealism can become tyranny. His embarrassment during the adultery trial suggests some awareness of this tension.

Class and Authority

Gideon's challenge to Julius about the vestry committee - "by whose authority does the vestry committee sit?" - represents a democratic impulse within Puritanism. He argues that congregations should elect their leaders, not have them imposed by wealth and status. This challenge to traditional hierarchy was genuinely revolutionary.

The Failure of Utopian Dreams

Gideon's vision of building "that shining city on a hill" in England ultimately fails. The Commonwealth collapses, the king returns, and the Puritan moment passes. The novel uses Gideon to explore why revolutionary movements fail - not just through external opposition but through internal contradictions and the complexity of human nature.

Memory and Legacy

Through Chapter 14, we see how Gideon is remembered: as a heroic warrior for God, fighting alongside Cromwell. His grandson's generation venerates him as an exemplar of godly courage. The novel thus explores how revolutionary figures are transformed into legend, with their complexities and moral compromises forgotten.

Identity and Naming

Gideon's name - "a warrior for the Lord" - becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. The novel suggests how names and expectations shape identity. Similarly, the unusual name he chooses for his son, "O Be Joyful," reflects the Puritan practice of using biblical phrases as names, turning children into walking sermons.
