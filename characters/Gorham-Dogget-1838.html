Overview

Gorham Dogget is an American financier from Boston who represents the new industrial wealth of post-Civil War America. A Harvard-educated member of Boston's old elite, he trebled his already substantial fortune by investing in the railroads that opened up the American Midwest. In 1889, he brings his wife and daughter Nancy to London as part of an extended Grand Tour, ostensibly for culture and pleasure, but also to explore investment opportunities in London's financial markets. However, by the end of his stay, it is revealed that bad investments have destroyed his fortune, leaving him to return to Boston to rebuild from near-ruin.

Character and Personality

Gorham Dogget embodies the confident, practical spirit of American capitalism at its zenith. He is described as speaking "out of the side of his mouth" in true Bostonian fashion, and his manner is characteristically blunt and direct. When interviewing the Earl St James about his intentions toward Nancy, he dispenses with British circumlocution and asks pointed questions about finances and love.

Despite his wealth and success, Dogget is "adventurous" and forward-thinking. He recognizes London as "the financial capital of the world" despite American expansion, and actively seeks out innovative projects like Arnold Silversleeves's proposed electric underground railway system. He shares the American investors' boldness about new technology that makes him tell Silversleeves: "Electric rails have worked in Chicago. London is the most populous city in the world, with a crying need for more transport. I want you to do me a feasibility study. I'll find the investors. This thing can be done!"

He is notably democratic in his values, particularly regarding inheritance. Unlike the English aristocracy's system of primogeniture, Dogget has always made clear that his son and daughter will share his fortune equally, a practice common among American families but "almost unknown" among old English families.

Most significantly, Gorham Dogget proves himself to be a man of integrity and shrewdness. When the Earl of St James courts his daughter Nancy, Dogget conducts due diligence. Upon learning through Esther Dogget that the earl has bet his fortune on the Charlotte Rose beating the Cutty Sark, and suspecting that St James is a fortune-hunter, he stages an elaborate scene with the help of César Ritz, the manager of the Savoy Hotel, to expose the earl's true motives.

His belief in love matches is genuine and deeply held. He tells St James: "I have to tell you I believe in that, Lord St James. I believe in it strongly. For richer for poorer, as they say." This conviction drives his decision to test the earl's sincerity.

Life and Events

Early Life and Success (c. 1838-1880s)

Born into one of Boston's oldest families – the Doggets "had come over with Harvard" and it was rumoured they "had even embarked on the Mayflower, 'then jumped ship'" according to unkind friends. The family was as established as the Cabots, Hubbards, Gorhams, and Lorings, with "trust funds [that] went down into the bedrock." Like other old Boston families, the Doggets were known for occasionally producing children with webbed fingers, a hereditary trait that "caused no great concern" among families not "renowned for their beauty."

Gorham attended Harvard and married a woman from "a rich old New York family," cementing his position among America's elite. He initially possessed a "solid fortune" but proved himself more enterprising than many of his conservative peers. Recognizing the transformative potential of westward expansion, he invested heavily "in the railroads that had opened up the great Midwestern plains" and "trebled his already solid fortune."

London Ventures (1880s)

Dogget began spending significant time in London, following the example of major American financiers like J.P. Morgan and George Peabody, who "spent most of their careers there and raised the money for huge projects like the American railroads." The City of London, with "its vast imperial trade was still the financial capital of the world," and Dogget's visits "in this connection had given [him] several ideas for further projects."

He became particularly interested in London's infrastructure needs, eventually retaining Arnold Silversleeves to conduct a feasibility study for an electric underground railway system. Dogget paid Silversleeves "cash down, the first part of a fee that had made the engineer blink," demonstrating both his seriousness and his wealth.

The Grand Tour (1889)

Like other wealthy Americans of the new industrial age, Dogget and his family embraced the pleasures of Europe, making the Grand Tour that English aristocrats had pioneered in the previous century. They spent a month in France and another in Italy, where Nancy "made many sketches and acquired a smattering of those languages." Fine paintings were also purchased, as Dogget collected European art.

By late 1889, this was "the third time that mother and daughter had stayed, enjoying London's social life, while Mr Dogget returned briefly to Boston." The family took luxurious rooms at the newly opened Savoy Hotel on the Strand, enjoying the establishment's unprecedented American-style comfort combined with European grandeur, presided over by César Ritz and chef Escoffier.

Financial Collapse (December 1889)

During one of his absences in Boston, Gorham Dogget suffered devastating financial losses through "bad investments." By the time he returned to London in December 1889, his fortune was essentially gone. The severity of the situation became apparent when the Savoy Hotel, after weeks of the family's residence, made enquiries to his bankers in Boston and received a reply that alarmed the usually discreet César Ritz.

However, Dogget still owned his house in Boston and remained determined to rebuild: "I made a fortune once so I dare say I can do it again."

The St James Affair (December 1889)

Around the same time as his financial collapse, the Earl of St James began courting Nancy. Dogget's response to this courtship reveals both his shrewdness and his principles. Through his connection to Arnold Silversleeves, Dogget dined at Esther Dogget's Hampstead home, where he learned that St James and Jonas Barnikel were partners in the clipper ship Charlotte Rose, and that the earl had "bet on it so heavily that I believe his fortune may rest entirely on [Captain Barnikel's] shoulders."

When St James came to the Savoy to formally propose, Dogget orchestrated a revealing test. He asked pointed questions about the earl's ability to support Nancy and about his sincere love "for herself." St James claimed sufficient wealth and absolute devotion. At that moment, César Ritz interrupted with a staged scene about the Dogget family's unpaid hotel bill and the negative reply from Boston bankers, revealing that Dogget's fortune was gone.

St James immediately made excuses and "beat a hasty retreat," exposing himself as a fortune-hunter. After the earl left, Dogget thanked Ritz: "I think we smoked him out."

This incident reveals Dogget's character at its finest: even in his own financial ruin, he prioritized protecting his daughter from a mercenary marriage. His integrity and devotion to his belief in love matches trumped any temptation to secure an aristocratic title for Nancy through a loveless union.

Aftermath and Future Plans (January-February 1890)

Just before Christmas, Dogget announced he was taking his wife and daughter away from "the damp winter for a three-month cruise on the Nile and the Mediterranean." Despite his financial difficulties, he somehow managed to proceed with this plan. He also invited young Penny, son of the respectable City family, to accompany them – presumably as company for Nancy, suggesting Dogget was open to a more suitable match developing.

Before departing, Dogget gave Arnold Silversleeves "a great pile of legal documents to work on" through Esther's son Matthew, a solicitor. This suggests that even in financial difficulty, Dogget was laying groundwork for future ventures, true to his determination to rebuild his fortune.

Relationships

Nancy Dogget (Daughter)

Gorham's relationship with his daughter is marked by genuine protective affection and progressive principles. Unlike English aristocrats who would leave daughters penniless while estates passed to eldest sons, Dogget has always intended that Nancy and her brother "would share his fortune equally." This democratic approach reflects American values over British tradition.

When Nancy becomes the target of the Earl of St James's courtship, Gorham does not simply embrace the aristocratic connection. Instead, he insists on proper vetting, telling St James: "As her father though, I have to ask a few questions." His emphasis on love over material considerations – "you sincerely love my daughter, for herself... For richer for poorer" – shows his commitment to Nancy's happiness over social ambition.

Most tellingly, when his own fortune collapses, Gorham protects Nancy from a loveless marriage by exposing St James's mercenary motives, even though such a match might have secured her future. He later invites young Penny on their cruise, suggesting an openness to Nancy forming an attachment with a respectable, if untitled, young man.

Mrs Gorham Dogget (Wife)

Mrs Dogget, from a "rich old New York family," is Nancy's mother and Gorham's partner in their European adventures. She shares his enthusiasm for culture and society, enjoying London's social life and the theater. She had initially been impressed by St James – "My dear, he's an earl!" – but also counseled Nancy to "think of the man, not his title," suggesting she shared her husband's balanced perspective on the match.

Arnold Silversleeves

Gorham's relationship with the engineer demonstrates his eye for talent and innovation. He recognizes Silversleeves's brilliance despite the engineer's lack of commercial success and social polish. Gorham pays him generously for the electric railway feasibility study and treats him with respect, taking him seriously as a business partner. At dinner at the Silversleeves home, Dogget engages enthusiastically with the technical and business discussions, even when Esther's social pretensions become awkward.

The Pennys

Gorham develops a positive relationship with young Penny during dinner at the Silversleeves home, impressed by the young man's forward-thinking approach to insurance. He calls him "a sound, forward-looking young man" and ultimately invites him on the Nile cruise. This connection suggests Gorham values merit, enterprise, and good character over aristocratic lineage.

César Ritz

The partnership Dogget forms with the legendary hotel manager to expose St James reveals his ability to command respect and cooperation even in difficult circumstances. Ritz's willingness to participate in the staged scene, and his discretion in executing it, speaks to Dogget's character and persuasive abilities.

Historical Context

Gorham Dogget embodies the Gilded Age American capitalist at the height of American economic expansion. The period from the end of the Civil War to the 1890s saw the rise of enormous American fortunes built on railroads, steel, oil, and finance. The opening of the Midwest through railroads – exactly the investments that made Dogget rich – transformed America from a largely agricultural nation into an industrial powerhouse.

The phenomenon of wealthy Americans making the Grand Tour and spending extended periods in Europe was characteristic of this era. Having accumulated unprecedented wealth, American millionaires sought European culture, art, and occasionally, aristocratic connections through marriage. The contrast between American democratic values (like equal inheritance) and British aristocratic tradition (primogeniture and fortune-hunting marriages) was stark and often produced cultural friction, as illustrated in Dogget's encounter with St James.

The American practice of "dollar princesses" – wealthy American heiresses marrying impoverished British aristocrats – was at its peak during this period. Dogget's refusal to allow Nancy to become such a transaction, despite his own financial ruin, represents a principled rejection of this practice.

Paradoxically, the same American railroad investments that enriched Gorham Dogget destroyed the fortunes of many English aristocrats like Lord St James. The "huge quantities of cheap grain that came from the American plains caused grain prices to tumble and with them the value of much agricultural land," forcing English landowners to sell estates at poor prices.

The international financial connections Dogget represents – American capital seeking opportunities in London, the world's financial center – characterized the increasingly globalized economy of the late Victorian era. Major American financiers like J.P. Morgan and George Peabody based themselves in London to access capital markets for American projects, just as Dogget does.

Finally, Dogget's enthusiasm for electric railways and other innovations reflects the American embrace of new technology that contrasted with British investor caution. As Arnold Silversleeves notes, "British investors, so far, were cautious about the new technology. But the Americans were not."

Themes and Significance

Old Money vs. New Money

Gorham Dogget represents the curious American blend of old-family prestige and new-money entrepreneurialism. Though descended from near-Mayflower stock and educated at Harvard, he is not content to live off inherited wealth. His tripling of his fortune through railroad investments shows the American elite's willingness to engage in commerce and risk-taking that British aristocrats often disdained.

American Democracy vs. British Aristocracy

The contrast between Dogget's values and British aristocratic practices illuminates a central theme of the novel. His insistence on equal inheritance for sons and daughters, his belief in marriage for love "for richer for poorer," and his willingness to expose and reject the fortune-hunting earl all exemplify American democratic ideals. The scene where he "smokes out" St James represents a moral victory of American egalitarian values over aristocratic cynicism.

Fortune and Character

Dogget's story arc from wealth to ruin tests his character and reveals its true quality. In prosperity, he is generous, far-sighted, and principled. In adversity, these qualities intensify rather than disappear. His protection of Nancy from a mercenary match, even when it might have solved both their financial problems, shows integrity transcending self-interest. His determination to rebuild – "I made a fortune once so I dare say I can do it again" – reflects the American belief in self-made success and second chances.

Global Capital and London's Role

Through Dogget's presence in London, seeking investment opportunities and retaining local experts like Silversleeves, Rutherford illustrates London's position as the hub of global finance in 1889. American capital seeking returns, British expertise seeking American boldness, and the international connections that funded projects like electric railways all flow through London, even as British political and economic dominance begins its long decline.

Family Across Time and Class

Though Gorham Dogget seems to have no knowledge of his distant London relations, the reader knows he is connected to Lucy Dogget, laboring in East End poverty, and to Esther Dogget, nervously maintaining respectability in Hampstead. The Dogget family name spans from Boston Brahmins to London match-box makers, illustrating the novel's theme of family lines diverging across continents, classes, and centuries while maintaining unexpected connections. The coincidence of Gorham dining at Esther's home – where she desperately claims her father was "an investor" and notes with anxious pride that "my maiden name was Dogget, the same as yours" – creates dramatic irony as these distant relatives orbit each other without recognizing their connection.

The Limits of Nobility

The Earl of St James's failed courtship of Nancy, exposed by Gorham's cleverness, reinforces the novel's recurring critique of aristocratic pretension. The earl's title, estate, and jewels cannot save him from financial ruin or from moral failure. Dogget, though losing his fortune, never loses his principles, proving that true nobility lies in character rather than birth.
