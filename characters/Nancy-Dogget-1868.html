Overview

Nancy Dogget is a twenty-one-year-old American heiress from Boston who becomes the unwitting target of an impoverished English earl's mercenary matrimonial schemes. The daughter of railroad investor Gorham Dogget, she represents the new American wealth that was beginning to intermarry with the declining British aristocracy in the late Victorian era. Her story serves as both a romantic tale and a cautionary narrative about the collision between American democratic ideals about marriage and the financial calculations of the British upper class.

Physical Description

Nancy has a good complexion with golden hair parted in the middle and modestly drawn back in a style that is somewhat childish for her twenty-one years. Her most remarkable feature is her china-blue eyes. Her appearance is described as not particularly remarkable overall, though certainly pleasant and respectable for a young woman of good family.

Character and Personality

Nancy is characterized by a combination of youthful enthusiasm and genuine romantic sentiment. She displays excitement and innocence during the opera performance of The Gondoliers, leaning forward with her fan and thrilling at the possibility of the Earl of St James's hand touching hers. She is clearly caught up in the romance and social prestige of potentially becoming a countess.

Despite being from one of Boston's oldest and wealthiest families, Nancy shows a degree of self-awareness about social hierarchies. When discussing the earl with her mother, she gently but perceptively notes her mother's own interest in the title, asking "you do not object that he is a lord" and seeing her mother blush at the observation. This suggests Nancy possesses more perception about human nature and social ambition than she might initially appear to have.

She is educated and cultured, having made the Grand Tour of Europe with her parents. During a month in Italy, she made many sketches and acquired a smattering of Italian and French. The family has also purchased fine paintings during their European travels, indicating Nancy's exposure to and participation in cultural activities appropriate to her class.

Nancy appears to genuinely like the Earl of St James, finding him "warm, charming, educated" with "a fine house and a lovely old estate in Kent." At thirty, he seems to her old enough to be a man of the world but young enough to be enviable to "the girls back home." Her consideration of him seems based on both his personal qualities and his title, showing a mixture of romantic interest and awareness of social advantage.

When the earl proposes to her during a fairy-tale carriage ride through Hyde Park on a cold, clear day with frost sparkling on the ground, Nancy follows proper form by asking for time to consider, though her manner suggests she intends to accept. She also properly reminds him that he must ask her father's permission, showing her adherence to social conventions.

Life and Events

Early Life in Boston

Nancy was born in 1868 into the Dogget family, one of Boston's oldest and most established families. The Doggets are placed among the elite families of old Boston – the Cabots, Hubbards, Gorhams, and Lorings – families who "knew exactly whom their ancestors had married" and "what the family had thought of them at the time." According to family tradition, the Doggets "had come over with Harvard" and it was rumored they had "even embarked on the Mayflower, 'then jumped ship'" as some unkind friends would note.

Nancy grew up in a household of solid wealth, with family trust funds going "down into the bedrock." Her father Gorham Dogget, a true Bostonian who attended Harvard, had married a girl from a rich old New York family and then trebled his already solid fortune by investing in the railroads that opened up the great Midwestern plains. The family also carried the hereditary trait of webbed fingers that occasionally appeared in the old East Coast families, though this "caused no great concern" as "not even their greatest admirers claimed that the old East Coast families were renowned for their beauty."

Nancy has at least one brother, and under American customs quite different from English aristocratic practice, her father has made it clear that his fortune will be divided equally between his children. This makes Nancy an heiress in a way that an English daughter of similar circumstances might not be, as English estates typically went entirely to the eldest son.

The Grand Tour (1888-1889)

Like other Americans made wealthy by the new industrial age, the Dogget family discovered the pleasures of Europe and undertook the Grand Tour, basing themselves in London. Nancy and her mother spent a month in France and another in Italy, where Nancy made many sketches and acquired a smattering of those languages. Fine paintings were also purchased during these travels.

This was the third time that Nancy and her mother had stayed in London, enjoying the city's social life while Mr Dogget periodically returned to Boston on business. The extended stays in London served multiple purposes: cultural enrichment, art acquisition, and – though perhaps not explicitly stated – the possibility of advantageous social connections.

London Season and Courtship (1889)

During the 1889 London season, Nancy and her mother are staying at the luxurious Savoy Hotel on the Strand while enjoying London's high society. Nancy attends fashionable entertainments, including a performance of Gilbert and Sullivan's The Gondoliers where she and her mother have taken a private box. The Earl of St James attends her "very attentively," and during the finale, he allows his hand to brush against hers very lightly, marking the beginning of a courtship.

The earl takes Nancy for a carriage drive through Hyde Park on a cold, clear day when the frosty ground is sparkling. The icy trees seem made of glass as the carriage passes the site where the Crystal Palace once stood, now marked by a tall ornate monument to Prince Albert, and the huge oval shape of the new Albert Hall. When they reach the place where the western section of Hyde Park turns into Kensington Gardens, the earl proposes marriage.

Nancy asks for time to consider – following proper form – but only for a few days, and her manner suggests she will accept. She also reminds him that he must ask her father's permission, showing her proper understanding of social conventions.

The Revelation at the Savoy (December 1889)

In the days before Christmas, Mr Gorham Dogget arrives from Boston and announces that immediately after Christmas he is taking his wife and daughter away from the damp winter for a three-month cruise on the Nile and the Mediterranean. It remains undecided whether Nancy and her mother will return to London afterwards.

When the Earl of St James arrives at the Savoy Hotel to formally ask for Nancy's hand, a devastating scene unfolds. César Ritz, the hotel's manager, interrupts the meeting between the earl and Mr Dogget to insist on resolution of an outstanding matter. It emerges that the Savoy has made inquiries to the Doggets' bankers in Boston and received troubling news. Mr Dogget admits to St James that "some bad investments" have caused his fortune to be gone, though he still owns a house in Boston and hopes "to do something for Nancy in due course."

The Earl of St James, whether out of embarrassment or because Nancy is no longer an heiress, hastily excuses himself and beats a retreat. What Nancy does not witness is that her father then thanks César Ritz, revealing that the entire scene was staged to "smoke out" the earl and expose his mercenary motivations. Mr Dogget says "I think we smoked him out" and confirms the plan worked as intended.

Aftermath and the Nile Cruise (early 1890)

Following the exposure of the earl's true intentions, St James writes Nancy a letter suggesting that "something in his own past" makes it necessary to withdraw from the engagement, implying it is only decent to do so, though he does not specify what the impediment is. He does not mention his own financial ruin or the loss of Nancy's fortune.

Despite the revelation of Gorham Dogget's apparent financial troubles, the family proceeds with their planned three-month cruise on the Nile and Mediterranean. Intriguingly, they invite the son of the Penny family to accompany them, and he accepts. Harriet Penny tells Esther Silversleeves "I do believe he means our son to keep his daughter company," suggesting that despite the performative revelation of lost fortune, the Doggets may not be as financially ruined as was claimed to the Earl of St James, and that Nancy's matrimonial prospects remain intact – though now focused on more genuine suitors.

Relationships

Gorham Dogget (Father)

Nancy's relationship with her father appears affectionate and trusting. She understands that any marriage proposal must receive his permission, following proper convention. What she does not know is the extent to which her father is actively protecting her from fortune-hunters.

Gorham Dogget demonstrates a strong belief in marrying for love rather than material considerations. He tells the Earl of St James: "I have to tell you I believe in that, Lord St James. I believe in it strongly. For richer for poorer, as they say." This philosophy appears to have shaped how he views his daughter's future. When he suspects the earl's true mercenary motivations, he orchestrates an elaborate scheme with César Ritz to expose them, staging a scene where his fortune appears to have been lost to see if the earl will stand by Nancy regardless.

Unlike English aristocrats who would leave estates entirely to eldest sons, Gorham has made clear that Nancy and her brother will share his fortune equally, giving Nancy genuine financial independence and making her a true heiress. This American approach to inheritance reflects his more democratic values about women and property.

The fact that Gorham proceeds with the expensive Nile cruise despite the claimed financial ruin suggests he successfully protected his daughter from an unsuitable match while preserving her actual prospects and fortune.

Mrs Gorham Dogget (Mother)

Nancy has a close, companionable relationship with her mother, who accompanies her to social events and cultural activities in London. They share a private box at The Gondoliers together. Her mother provides gentle guidance on matters of marriage and propriety.

When Nancy asks if St James would be a good husband, noting "That would make me a countess," her mother reminds her "You should think of the man, not his title." However, Nancy perceptively observes "But you do not object that he is a lord" and sees her mother blush, revealing her mother's own attraction to the aristocratic connection. Nancy's observation shows both affection and gentle teasing in their relationship.

Mrs Dogget appears protective of Nancy, having been the one to first discover the Earl of St James and announce to her daughter "My dear, he's an earl!" She provides moral guidance while also being susceptible to the romantic allure of European aristocracy. She reassures Nancy that "I think he is a good man, and I'm sure your father will like him," showing her attempt to balance romantic sentiment with practical considerations.

Earl of St James

Nancy's relationship with the Earl of St James is characterized by romantic interest on her part and calculated deception on his. She finds him appealing – "warm, charming, educated" with "a fine house and a lovely old estate in Kent." At thirty, he seems to her old enough to be a man of the world but young enough to be enviable.

The earl attends the opera with Nancy "very attentively" and during the finale of The Gondoliers allows his hand to brush against hers very lightly, a gesture she has been hoping for throughout the performance. He takes her for a romantic carriage ride through a frost-covered Hyde Park and proposes to her in a fairy-tale setting.

What Nancy does not know is that the earl is desperately in need of money and has been deliberately courting her for her fortune. He has no genuine romantic interest in her, though he acknowledges to his half-sister Lady Muriel that "she's a nice little thing" and tells himself he "really liked the girl a lot." His plan is to keep her interested without committing himself too far until he learns whether he has won a bet on a clipper ship race. If he wins the bet, he will have no need to marry her and intends to "retire with grace."

When he discovers that Nancy's fortune has been lost, he immediately abandons the courtship, excusing himself and "beating a hasty retreat" from the Savoy. He later writes her a letter suggesting some impediment in his past requires him to withdraw, maintaining a veneer of honor while actually motivated entirely by her lost financial value.

Nancy is thus spared a mercenary marriage through her father's shrewd intervention, though the text does not reveal how much she learns about the earl's true character or whether she suffers significant heartbreak over the failed courtship.

Young Penny

Following the exposure and withdrawal of the Earl of St James, Nancy's father invites the son of the Penny family to accompany the Doggets on their three-month cruise of the Nile and Mediterranean. The young man accepts. Harriet Penny believes Gorham Dogget "means our son to keep his daughter company," suggesting this may be the beginning of a new and potentially more genuine courtship for Nancy.

Young Penny is described as "a bright young man in the City, very smartly dressed" who works in his family's insurance business. He has progressive ideas about expanding life insurance to the middle and working classes, demonstrating both business acumen and forward-thinking social awareness. He has impressed Mr Gorham Dogget as "a sound, forward-looking young man," making him a more suitable match than a fortune-hunting aristocrat.

Historical Context

Nancy's story takes place in 1889, during the height of the late Victorian era when the relationship between American wealth and British aristocracy was undergoing significant change. The huge quantities of cheap grain coming from the American plains – made possible by the very railroads in which Nancy's father had invested – were causing grain prices to tumble and with them the value of English agricultural land. Many English aristocratic families found themselves land-rich but cash-poor, with large estates but insufficient income to maintain them.

This economic transformation created a market for marriages between American heiresses and British aristocrats. American families like the Doggets, with enormous new fortunes from industrial enterprises, sought the cultural prestige and social elevation of European aristocratic connections. Impoverished British peers, in turn, needed American money to maintain their estates and lifestyles. This transatlantic marriage market became a notable feature of late Victorian society, with both romantic and mercenary dimensions.

Nancy represents the American side of this exchange. Her family embodies the self-made wealth of the American Gilded Age, where railroad investments could triple already solid fortunes. The Doggets have the confidence of old American lineage (dating to Harvard's founding and rumored to include the Mayflower) combined with the aggressive entrepreneurialism that built industrial America. Her father's time in London connects to the fact that the City of London, despite American expansion, remained "the financial capital of the world" where "American bankers like Morgan and Peabody spent most of their careers" to raise money for huge projects.

The contrast between American and British inheritance customs is significant to Nancy's status. Her father's decision to divide his fortune equally between his son and daughter reflects more democratic American values about women and property. In England, as the Earl of St James observes, "If Nancy Dogget were English, she probably wouldn't be an heiress at all." English great estates went to eldest sons, married daughters often got nothing, and unmarried daughters were supported by family trusts or expected to live at home. This made American heiresses particularly valuable in the marriage market, as they brought not only money but actual control of their own fortunes.

Nancy's experience at the luxurious Savoy Hotel reflects the late Victorian era's highest standards of comfort and service. The recently opened hotel, built on the site where John of Gaunt's Savoy Palace once stood, had "imported an up-to-date level of American comfort, mixed it with European grandeur, and created a masterpiece." The fact that the Savoy represented American standards of comfort speaks to the United States' growing influence on international culture and commerce.

Her attendance at Gilbert and Sullivan's The Gondoliers places her in the context of Victorian London's three-tiered entertainment system: opera at Covent Garden for the apex of society, music halls for the poor, and in between, the operettas that combined easy tunes and charming comedy with music "often worthy of opera" and lyrics of exceptional "verbal brilliance and satire." That Queen Victoria herself had loved The Gondoliers indicates the social acceptability and fashionable status of Nancy's attendance.

Themes and Significance

Nancy Dogget serves as a symbol of the collision between American democratic ideals and British aristocratic pragmatism in the late Victorian era. Her father's insistence that marriage should be based on genuine love rather than financial calculation – "For richer for poorer, as they say" – represents a distinctly American romantic ideal, even as the family pursues the social prestige of European connections.

Her story illustrates the mercenary nature of the late Victorian marriage market, particularly the trade between American wealth and British titles. The Earl of St James sees her purely as a financial solution, acknowledging she is "a nice little thing" but having no real romantic interest. He plans to keep her interested "without committing himself too far" so he can "either advance quickly or retire with grace" depending on whether his bet on a clipper ship race succeeds. Nancy is thus reduced to a commodity to be acquired or discarded based on financial calculations on both sides.

The contrast between appearance and reality in social interactions is central to Nancy's narrative. What appears to be a romantic courtship is actually a calculated pursuit. What appears to be a financial disaster for the Doggets is actually a staged test of the earl's character. What appears to be the end of Nancy's matrimonial hopes may actually be her rescue from a loveless marriage. The theatrical nature of London society – epitomized by Nancy's attendance at an operetta – extends into real life, where both her suitor and her father are playing carefully scripted roles.

Nancy's experience also highlights questions of female agency and protection in Victorian society. She is not consulted about her father's scheme to expose the earl, nor is it clear how much she learns about the manipulation on both sides. She is simultaneously the prize to be won and the innocent to be protected, her own voice and preferences largely absent from the maneuvering around her. When she observes her mother's attraction to the aristocratic title, Nancy shows perception, but whether she understands the full extent of the games being played around her remains ambiguous.

The international dimension of Nancy's story reflects the growing interconnection between American and British society in the late Victorian era. American capital was financing British infrastructure (the tube system Arnold Silversleeves designs for Gorham Dogget) and American grain was undermining British agricultural estates (the same railroads Gorham invested in caused the Earl of St James's financial distress). The marriage market was just one aspect of a larger transatlantic exchange of money, culture, and social prestige.

Finally, Nancy's story contributes to the novel's broader theme of London as a city where fortunes rise and fall, where ancient lineages must adapt or perish, and where new money from unexpected sources (American railroads, Australian wool, industrial enterprises) constantly reshapes the social landscape. The Earl of St James, despite his ancient title and Bocton estate, is financially ruined by forces beyond his control, while the Doggets, whose ancestors may have "jumped ship" from the Mayflower, have the wealth and power to protect their daughter and expose aristocratic pretense.

Key Quotes About Nancy Dogget

"It could not be said that there was anything very remarkable about Miss Nancy Dogget of Boston, Mass. Her complexion, certainly, was good. Her golden hair was parted in the middle and modestly drawn back in a way that was perhaps a little childish for her twenty-one years. But her china-blue eyes were truly remarkable."

This opening description establishes Nancy as pleasant but not extraordinary, with her eyes being her most distinctive feature. The somewhat "childish" hairstyle suggests a youthful innocence that makes her vulnerable to sophisticated manipulation.

"As for the man who was sharing the evening so attentively at her side, he seemed everything that a man could be. Warm, charming, educated; he had a fine house and a lovely old estate in Kent. At thirty he was old enough to be a man of the world, but young enough for the girls back home to envy her."

Nancy's assessment of the Earl of St James reveals her criteria for a suitable husband: personal qualities, property, social position, and the approval of her peers. She combines genuine appreciation of his qualities with awareness of the social prestige he represents.

"'You should think of the man, not his title,' her mother reminded her. 'But you do not object that he is a lord,' Nancy gently remarked, and saw her mother blush."

This exchange reveals Nancy's perceptiveness about human nature and her gentle ability to call out hypocrisy, even in her own mother. She understands the attraction of aristocracy while also recognizing the importance her mother places on it.

"She had asked for time to consider – that was the form, of course – but only for a few days, and he had little doubt from her manner that the answer would be yes."

Nancy's adherence to proper social forms even when her intentions are clear shows her understanding of Victorian conventions around courtship and her willingness to perform the expected social rituals.

"Though Gorham Dogget had a son as well as a daughter, he had always made it clear they would share his fortune equally; but among the old families of England such an arrangement remained almost unknown."

This observation highlights the significant difference between American and British inheritance practices and explains why Nancy is a true heiress with genuine financial independence, making her an attractive target for fortune-hunters.

"'I can't answer for her,' the Bostonian replied, 'but you seem, Lord St James, to be a fine man to me. As her father though, I have to ask a few questions... You sincerely love my daughter, for herself? I have to tell you I believe in that, Lord St James. I believe in it strongly. For richer for poorer, as they say.'"

Gorham Dogget's insistence on genuine love as the basis for marriage represents American democratic values about personal choice and authentic emotion, contrasting with the more pragmatic British aristocratic approach to matrimonial alliances.

"'Thank you, Mr Ritz.' 'Was that all right, sir?' 'Oh yes. I think we smoked him out.'"

This revelation shows that Gorham Dogget orchestrated the entire scene of his financial ruin specifically to test the Earl of St James's true intentions, demonstrating a father's shrewd protection of his daughter from mercenary suitors.
