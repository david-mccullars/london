Overview

Penny is a wealthy businessman and owner of the highly successful Penny Insurance Company in early 20th century London. Through his marriage to Nancy Dogget, daughter of Gorham Dogget, he maintains close family ties to the Bull family. He represents the prosperity and social mobility achieved by the Penny family line by the Edwardian era.

Physical Description

No physical description provided in the text, though it is mentioned that he has "short sight."

Character and Personality

Penny is portrayed as a successful and generous family man who has achieved considerable wealth through his insurance business. His ownership of a Rolls-Royce automobile in 1911 – when such vehicles had only been manufactured for about six years and were exclusively for the very rich – demonstrates his substantial financial success. The fact that he maintains close relationships with his Bull cousins and volunteers to drive old Edward Bull and his grandsons shows a spirit of family loyalty and generosity with his time and resources.

His willingness to make the journey to Charterhouse to collect the boys, only to have to return to London on urgent business, suggests he is actively involved in managing his business affairs even in his later years (he would be approximately 88 years old in 1911).

Life and Events

1911

Penny appears in the narrative on Saturday, 17 June 1911, the weekend before King George V's coronation. He arrives at Edward Bull's residence driving his Rolls-Royce automobile with plans to transport Bull and Bull's two grandsons (the Meredith boys, Henry and Frederick) from their school at Charterhouse back to Bocton for the weekend.

The journey proceeds well – they average nearly twenty miles an hour on the country roads and arrive comfortably before lunch. However, upon arrival at Charterhouse, Penny receives an urgent telephone message requiring him to return to London, apparently on business matters. This disrupts the planned weekend, though the boys ultimately travel to London with their grandfather instead, turning the change of plans into an opportunity to visit their mother and sister.

The following day (Sunday), Penny's original plan had been to drive the boys back to school at tea-time, though this arrangement is superseded by events.

Relationships

Edward Bull

Penny maintains a close relationship with Edward Bull, who is his cousin by marriage through the Bull-Penny family connection. The relationship is familiar and warm, with Edward playfully teasing Penny about his short sight while trusting him enough to be driven in his automobile – still a relatively new and somewhat daring mode of transport for an elderly gentleman of Bull's generation.

Nancy Dogget (wife)

Penny is married to Nancy Dogget, daughter of Gorham Dogget. Through this marriage, he is connected to both the Dogget and Bull families, as the Doggetts and Bulls have longstanding intermarriage and business connections.

Historical Context

Penny's appearance in the narrative in 1911 places him at an interesting moment in British history – just after the death of King Edward VII and the accession of King George V. The coronation weekend serves as the backdrop for significant events, including the massive Women's Coronation Procession organized by the Suffragette movement.

As owner of the Penny Insurance Company, Penny represents the continued success and expansion of the insurance industry that had become such a significant part of London's financial sector. The Penny family's rise from their earlier, more modest circumstances to considerable wealth mirrors the broader economic transformations of Victorian and Edwardian London, where enterprising families could build substantial fortunes through the expanding financial services sector.

His ownership of a Rolls-Royce automobile in 1911 marks him as an early adopter of automotive technology. At this time, motor cars were still primarily the preserve of the very wealthy, and the Rolls-Royce marque in particular represented the pinnacle of luxury and engineering. The narrative notes that while there were some motor-buses and motorized cabs in London by this date, private motor cars remained "only for the very rich."

Themes and Significance

Penny serves as an example of the multigenerational success of the Penny family line. Through business acumen in the insurance industry and strategic marriage alliances, the Pennys have achieved a level of prosperity that allows them to move in the same social circles as landed gentry like Edward Bull, despite the Bulls' aristocratic pretensions and ownership of the Bocton estate.

The detail that "The Penny family had always remained close to their Bull cousins" illustrates the importance of extended family networks among London's commercial and landed classes, and how these relationships persisted across generations and changing social circumstances. Despite differences in the sources of their wealth – Bull through brewing and land, Penny through insurance – these families maintain bonds of kinship and mutual regard.

His brief appearance in the narrative also serves a structural purpose: his automobile and his unexpected summons back to London create the circumstances that lead to Edward Bull and his grandsons being in London on the day of the great Suffragette march, setting up the dramatic confrontation between Bull and his daughter Violet over her treatment of young Helen.
