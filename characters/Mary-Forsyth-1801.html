Overview

Mary Forsyth is the only daughter of Hamish Forsyth, a prominent and formidable insurance underwriter at Lloyd's of London. She marries Eugene Penny in 1827 after a multi-year courtship during which her cautious father carefully evaluated Eugene's financial prospects and character. Though she plays a supporting role in the narrative, Mary is characterized by her intelligence, kindness, and lack of pretension, becoming a steadying influence in Eugene's life as he rises to partnership at Meredith's Bank.

Physical Description

Mary Forsyth has a slim figure and brown hair parted in the middle. Her head is somewhat large, giving her a studious appearance. There is nothing fashionable or coquettish about her presentation. Her beauty lies not in conventional attractiveness but in her kindly nature and high intelligence. Her father himself acknowledges "she's no beauty," a fact Eugene firmly disputes, being genuinely in love with her.

Character and Personality

Mary possesses a kindly nature and high intelligence that set her apart from more superficial society women of her era. She speaks with a soft Scottish brogue, inherited from her father's background. Unlike fashionable women of her class, she is unpretentious and lacks any coquettish mannerisms, presenting herself with a studious and serious bearing.

She demonstrates genuine compassion and generosity, as evidenced by her spontaneous kindness to the poor Dogget children she encounters on the road to Clapham. When she sees Lucy and Horatio tired and walking, she immediately stops to help them and later arranges for her coachman to wait and bring them home – acts of charity that go well beyond a single gesture.

Mary also shows wisdom in her domestic arrangements and family decisions. She is the one who suggests that Jeremy Fleming, Eugene's former godfather who has fallen on hard times, become an extra godfather to their children. She approves of her husband's continued support of Fleming, recognizing it as the right thing to do.

Life and Events

c. 1801-1823: Early Life

Mary grows up as the only child of Hamish Forsyth, the dour but principled Scottish insurance underwriter at Lloyd's. Her upbringing appears to have shaped her into an intelligent, well-educated woman rather than one focused on fashion or social climbing. Her father's solid fortune ensures her comfortable circumstances, though he is not among the wealthiest bankers in the City.

1823-1827: Courtship with Eugene Penny

Mary meets Eugene Penny, a clerk at Meredith's Bank, and the two develop a romantic attachment. Their courtship is long and cautious, dictated by her father's exacting standards. In October 1823, Eugene has his first formal meeting with Hamish Forsyth, during which the older man subjects him to intense scrutiny about his finances, family background, religion, and views on banking.

Despite Eugene's limited means at the time, Forsyth gives permission for him to call on Mary "from time to time," indicating that if she receives no better offer and Eugene establishes himself, they might eventually marry. Throughout the following years, Eugene sees Mary every week, and there is no doubt between them that they will eventually marry.

In September 1827, after Eugene secures a junior partnership at Meredith's Bank (partly by leveraging his forthcoming marriage to Forsyth's daughter), he asks for and receives Mary's hand in marriage. Her father, true to form, advises Eugene not to buy too expensive an engagement ring.

1827-1832: Marriage and Family Life

Mary marries Eugene Penny and they begin their family life together. Their first child is a healthy boy, with a second already on the way when Mary expresses her desire to live outside the metropolis. Eugene finds them a six-bedroom house in Clapham, a respectable village on the Thames's southern bank near the lavender fields. Her father approves of the location, though he considers the house larger than strictly necessary. To celebrate, Hamish gives them a fine set of Wedgwood china – characteristically choosing it because broken pieces can be replaced without losing the set's value.

Mary is particularly delighted by their home's proximity to the great lavender fields on the slope down to Battersea. She tells people she lives "out at Clapham, just beside Lavender Hill."

On an August day around 1829, Mary is driving in her pony trap past Vauxhall when she encounters Lucy and Horatio Dogget, two poor children walking to see the lavender fields. She stops immediately and gives them a ride, learning they are walking all the way from St Pancras. After dropping them at Lavender Hill, she instructs her coachman to wait and bring them home afterward – a generous act that creates one of the most poignant moments in Lucy's difficult childhood.

Around 1832, Mary and Eugene take in the elderly Earl of St James when he becomes confused and unwell. During this trying three-week period, the earl sometimes threatens Mary with violence in his confused state, though she endures this with her characteristic patience. After Meredith removes the earl to the West Country, Mary later suggests that Jeremy Fleming, Eugene's impoverished former godfather, become an extra godfather to their children. She approves warmly of Eugene's continued financial support of Fleming.

Relationships

Hamish Forsyth

Mary's relationship with her formidable father is not detailed in the text, but she is his only child and presumably his heir. Her character – intelligent, unpretentious, and principled – suggests she may have inherited or absorbed his values, though without his harsh exterior.

Eugene Penny

Mary and Eugene share a genuine love match despite the practical considerations involved in their courtship. Eugene sincerely loves her for her intelligence and kindness rather than conventional beauty or purely for her money, though he does not pretend indifference to her inheritance. She supports his career and shows good judgment in family matters. Their marriage appears companionable and affectionate, with Mary providing moral support for Eugene's generous impulses toward those who have fallen on hard times.

Jeremy Fleming

Though they have limited direct interaction in the text, Mary specifically requests that Jeremy Fleming become an extra godfather to her children and approves of Eugene's support for him, showing her charitable nature and good judgment of character.

Historical Context

Mary represents the respectable upper middle class of 1820s London – the daughters of successful City men who, while not aristocrats, enjoyed substantial wealth and social standing. Her father's position at Lloyd's places her family within the elite commercial class that had grown powerful during Britain's expansion of international trade and insurance.

The cautious approach to her courtship reflects the era's norms regarding marriage settlements and financial prudence, particularly after the banking crisis of 1825-1826. Her father's insistence that Eugene establish himself financially before marriage was standard practice for responsible City families.

Her choice to live in Clapham reflects the growing trend of successful City men moving their families to pleasant villages south of the Thames, connected to London by the new bridges but offering fresher air and green spaces. The lavender fields of the area were both a commercial enterprise and a pastoral amenity that attracted middle-class families seeking to escape the city's density while maintaining access to it.

Themes and Significance

Mary embodies the virtues of the conscientious middle class – intelligence, kindness, and lack of pretension. Her spontaneous charity toward the Dogget children provides a sharp contrast to the harsh economic realities facing poor families in the same era. The same lavender fields that are a picturesque amenity near her comfortable home represent a rare moment of beauty and respite for impoverished children walking miles to see them.

Her marriage to Eugene represents the rising merchant and banking class that would dominate Victorian England – respectable, financially sound unions based on both affection and practical considerations. Her support of Eugene's care for Jeremy Fleming illustrates how the successful middle class could exercise private charity toward those who had fallen in status, offering an alternative to the cruel workhouse system.

The detail that Mary lives "just beside Lavender Hill" connects her comfortable domestic life to the chapter's title and central image – the lavender fields that serve different purposes for different classes, from commercial crop to suburban amenity to magical destination for poor children.
