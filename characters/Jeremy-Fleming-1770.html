Overview

Jeremy Fleming is a highly respectable clerk at the Bank of England and the godfather of Eugene Penny. A widower living alone in a narrow house off Fleet Street, he possesses an encyclopaedic knowledge of the City's financial world and generously shares his wisdom with his young godson. His cautious, careful nature defines his career and most of his life, though late in life he attempts a more adventurous business venture that ends in financial ruin.

Physical Description

Fleming has a concave face that creases into a smile. In his later years, after his financial losses, he becomes stooped and presents a sad appearance, wearing scuffed shoes and shuffling along the streets.

Character and Personality

Jeremy Fleming embodies caution and respectability. His philosophy of life centers on the virtue of careful, conservative management - a trait he clearly associates with the Bank of England's success. He is kind and generous, welcoming his godson Eugene into his home with genuine delight and providing him with extensive education about the City's financial world.

Despite his outward caution, Fleming harbors secret regrets about opportunities not taken. He admits to having once dreamed of going into business for himself but lacking the courage to do so. He admires Eugene's ambition and willingness to take risks, seeing in his godson the boldness he himself never possessed.

Fleming displays a quiet, understated intelligence with sharp insights into the financial world. When Eugene rejects the secure Bank position Fleming has arranged, the older man is not offended but rather intrigued, showing "a new look, quite a sharp one" as he discusses alternative opportunities. He can be quite entertaining, "spicing his conversation with all the liveliest gossip of the last forty years" and describing "all the meanest of the dealers' tricks with a quiet relish."

Late in life, inspired by Eugene's example, Fleming attempts to overcome his natural caution, with disastrous results. Even in failure, however, he maintains his dignity and gentle good humor, smiling sadly at his misfortune.

Life and Events

1819: Eugene's Arrival in London

When Eugene Penny arrives in London in 1819 with instructions from his father to seek out his godfather Jeremy Fleming, Fleming is living as a widower in "a pleasant, narrow old house off Fleet Street only a short walk from where his grandfather's cake shop had been." His children have all married and left home. Fleming welcomes Eugene warmly, his concave face creasing into a smile of delight at having company, and assures him he may live in the house for as long as he pleases.

Fleming undertakes to educate Eugene about London and its financial world. He first shows him the sights - the Tower and St Paul's on the first day, Westminster and the West End on the second. On the third day, he begins Eugene's serious education, taking him to Greenwich to view the great docks system and explaining London's position as the hub of the world's greatest trading empire. Fleming explains how the navy underpins all commerce and how even Napoleon's Continental System inadvertently made London "the money centre of the world" through capital flight.

Fleming then shows Eugene the heart of the City - the Royal Exchange, the Mansion House, and, with hushed reverence, the Bank of England. He enthusiastically describes the Bank's history, its role in supporting the government through every crisis, and its careful, cautious management. With "a kind of ecstasy," he declares: "Caution, Eugene, is the key to life."

Through an old friend, Fleming secures Eugene a position at the Bank of England - which he clearly considers the pinnacle of success, offering "security for life." However, when Eugene gently reveals his ambition to make his fortune instead, Fleming surprises him. Rather than taking offense, Fleming falls silent for a time, then that evening calmly enquires about Eugene's specific interests in stockbroking or private banking.

Over the next three days, Fleming proves to be an invaluable mentor. He explains market operations, City politics and conventions, and provides "all the liveliest gossip of the last forty years." He outlines financial virtues to cultivate and describes "all the meanest of the dealers' tricks with a quiet relish." When Eugene ventures to ask why Fleming never went into business himself, Fleming admits: "I dreamed of it once, Eugene," but confesses to "want of courage."

Fleming successfully arranges an interview for Eugene at Meredith's Bank. He coaches him thoroughly on what to expect, including warning him about the Earl of St James's likely questions on free trade and the gold standard. Eugene later silently blesses his godfather multiple times during the interview for this preparation.

1819-1821: Continued Support

During Eugene's first two years at Meredith's Bank, he lives in Meredith's house but visits Fleming on Saturdays and Sundays (along with occasional visits to his parents in Rochester). Fleming continues to serve as a trusted advisor - when Eugene becomes concerned about Meredith's risky investment practices, he discusses his worries with Fleming, who comforts him by pointing out that the Earl of St James backs the bank.

c. 1830-1832: The Brewery Venture and Ruin

After Eugene becomes busy establishing his own household and career following his marriage to Mary Forsyth, he loses touch with his godfather. It is two years before he sees Fleming again, and when he does, the encounter is shocking. Walking down Fleet Street one day (likely around 1832), Eugene sees "a stooped and sad-looking figure in scuffed shoes shuffling along towards St Bride's" and suddenly recognizes with horror that it is Jeremy Fleming.

Fleming explains what happened: "It was Wellington's Beer Act, you see, in 1830. You remember, when everyone was complaining about prices, he made a law that anyone could make and sell beer? Well, I had nothing to do with my life, Penny, so I set up a little brewery myself, up there," indicating the St Pancras area. For a year, Fleming made beer.

When Eugene expresses surprise that someone so cautious would undertake such a venture, Fleming explains his motivation: "But I so admired the way that you had led your life, Penny, I said to myself: 'There, see what you might have done, Jeremy Fleming, but for your want of courage.' And I thought to myself: 'Everyone wants beer.' But they did not want mine. And then I lost caution and pressed on."

Fleming has lost everything. He no longer owns his house off Fleet Street but lives "in a smaller place now. Nearby." He depends on his children's kindness: "They are good children. Better than I deserve, Penny. They give me what they can. I do not starve."

Eugene, feeling guilt for having neglected Fleming for two years despite the kindness he had received, immediately invites him to supper and to stay with them. From that time forward, Eugene pays Fleming's rent and ensures he has at least one new suit made per year. Fleming becomes a frequent guest at the Penny house in Clapham and, at Mary's special request, becomes an extra godfather to the Penny children.

Mary sometimes tells Eugene with approval, "You are good to him," but Eugene can only polish his spectacles, shake his head and reply: "But very late, Mary. To my shame."

Relationships

Eugene Penny

Jeremy Fleming serves as Eugene's godfather, suggesting a prior close relationship between Fleming and Eugene's father in Rochester. When Eugene arrives in London in 1819, Fleming takes him in without hesitation and provides not only lodging but an extensive education in the City's financial world.

The relationship is fundamentally generous and paternal. Fleming clearly delights in having company and takes genuine pleasure in mentoring Eugene. He shows no ego when Eugene rejects the Bank of England position Fleming has secured, instead pivoting to help Eugene pursue his more ambitious goals. Fleming displays real pride in Eugene's boldness and ambition, seeing in him qualities he wishes he had possessed himself.

The relationship becomes more complex after Fleming's financial ruin. Eugene, who had neglected his godfather during his busy career-building years, feels profound guilt and shame when he discovers Fleming's reduced circumstances. He immediately steps in to support Fleming financially and welcome him into his family. While Fleming accepts this help gracefully, there is a poignant role reversal - the mentor who once provided everything now depends on his former protégé's generosity.

Eugene's Children

After his financial ruin and Eugene's intervention, Fleming becomes "an extra godfather" to Eugene and Mary's children at Mary's special request. He is a frequent visitor to their home in Clapham, suggesting he forms affectionate relationships with the younger generation.

Historical Context

Jeremy Fleming represents the respectable middle ranks of Georgian London's financial establishment. As a senior clerk at the Bank of England, he occupies a position of trust and security in what was becoming the world's leading financial institution. His career at the Bank coincides with its growing dominance - he witnesses and participates in the Bank's role in funding Britain through the Napoleonic Wars, managing government accounts, paying the army and navy overseas, and establishing the Bank's notes as the only authorized paper currency for the general public.

Fleming lives through the Industrial Revolution and Britain's emergence as the world's dominant trading power. His knowledge of the City is encyclopaedic because he has witnessed its transformation firsthand - the creation of the great docks system, the establishment of the Stock Exchange in Capel Court, the rise of merchant banks like Baring's and Rothschild's, and London's emergence as the money center of the world.

His failed brewery venture is directly tied to the Beer Act of 1830, a deregulation measure by the Duke of Wellington's government intended to address complaints about high prices by allowing anyone to brew and sell beer. The Act created a brief boom in small breweries, many of which - like Fleming's - quickly failed. His financial ruin reflects both the risks of this deregulated market and the broader economic volatility of early 19th century capitalism.

Fleming's dependence on his children's charity in old age and his later rescue by Eugene reflect both the absence of any social safety net for the respectable poor and the importance of family and personal relationships in providing security. He falls from comfortable middle-class respectability to near-poverty through a single failed business venture, with no pension or savings to cushion the blow.

Themes and Significance

Jeremy Fleming embodies the tragedy of excessive caution and the equal tragedy of caution abandoned too late. For most of his life, he represents the conservative virtues that built institutions like the Bank of England - careful judgment, encyclopaedic knowledge, and risk-averse management. His philosophy that "caution is the key to life" serves him well in his career, providing him with a respectable living and a pleasant house off Fleet Street.

Yet Fleming himself recognizes this caution as a form of cowardice - a "want of courage" that prevented him from pursuing his dreams. His admiration for Eugene's boldness reveals his own regrets. When he finally attempts to emulate Eugene's entrepreneurial spirit, he does so at the worst possible time and in the worst possible way - an elderly man with no business experience entering a deregulated, crowded market. His failure demonstrates not that caution was wrong, but that courage without wisdom is equally destructive.

Fleming's story illustrates the precariousness of middle-class security in early 19th century London. Despite a lifetime of respectable service at the Bank of England, he has no pension that prevents his fall into near-poverty. One bad decision can undo decades of careful living, leaving even a man of his experience and connections dependent on family charity.

The relationship between Fleming and Eugene demonstrates the novel's recurring theme of generational continuity and obligation. Fleming generously mentors Eugene when he is young, asking nothing in return. Eugene, after shamefully neglecting his benefactor, eventually fulfills his moral obligation by supporting Fleming in old age. The relationship transforms from mentor-protégé to a more complex interdependence, with each man ultimately helping the other.

Fleming also represents the quiet knowledge and expertise that underpins London's financial world - the senior clerks and long-serving administrators whose encyclopaedic understanding makes the system work. His knowledge of the City proves more valuable to Eugene than a position at the Bank would have been. He is part of the invisible infrastructure of expertise that allows the more visible merchants, bankers, and traders to function.

Key Quotes About Jeremy Fleming

"Penny was lucky in his godfather. Jeremy Fleming lived in a pleasant, narrow old house off Fleet Street only a short walk from where his grandfather's cake shop had been. A widower whose children had married and left home, his concave face creased into a smile of delight at the thought of having company and he assured Eugene he might live in his house for as long as he pleased. He was also sanguine about Eugene's prospects of working in the financial world; for in his lifetime as a highly respectable clerk in the Bank of England he had acquired an encyclopaedic knowledge of the City."
The narrator's introduction of Fleming, establishing his character, circumstances, and value as a mentor.
"In his quiet way, Fleming seemed almost pleased by his godson's initiative, and a new look, quite a sharp one, came into his eyes as he discussed the merits and drawbacks of the various firms."
Revealing Fleming's hidden sharpness and his approval of Eugene's ambition despite his own cautious nature.
"Once again, Eugene silently blessed his godfather."
Eugene's repeated thought during his interview at Meredith's Bank, acknowledging how Fleming's coaching prepared him for success.
