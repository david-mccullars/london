Overview

Pentecost Silversleeves is an Exchequer clerk from a wealthy London merchant family, whose life is defined by moral cowardice, calculated self-preservation, and ruthless opportunism. Physically weak but possessing phenomenal mathematical abilities, he spends five decades at the Exchequer without ever achieving the advancement he craves. His involvement in a murder as a young man sets the pattern for a career marked by expedient betrayals, culminating in the cold-blooded killing of young David Bull during a political crisis. Despite a lifetime of wickedness, he lives to extreme old age, blind but eventually experiencing a miracle of restored sight in the chapel of St Thomas Becket.

Physical Description

Pentecost has the distinctive Silversleeves family features: dark hair, a large long nose that seems to grow even longer with age, and mournful eyes. His physical inadequacies are pronounced: sloping shoulders, hips broader than his chest, weak limbs. As a boy, he could never catch a ball or hang by his arms. In old age, he becomes stick-like, thin and pale as "dusty old chalk," bent at the shoulders "as if he had been snapped."

Character and Personality

Pentecost is fundamentally a coward who compensates for physical and moral weakness through cunning and self-preservation. Though blessed with extraordinary mental gifts – able to calculate complex sums instantly and recall the entire contents of the Exchequer Pipe Rolls from memory – he lacks the discipline and commitment to excel as a scholar. His teachers consistently complained: "It comes too easily to him, so he won't really work."

His defining characteristic is moral expedience. When accused of lacking courage or manliness as a youth, he joins the wildest gang of young bloods to prove himself, despite not particularly liking them. This need for validation leads him into situations his better judgment would avoid, yet when consequences loom, he always manages to escape – through his clerical status, through betrayal of accomplices, or through calculated shifts of allegiance.

Pentecost is skilled at reading political winds and changing course with remarkable speed. His survival instinct is acute: when facing disaster, he can think with terrifying clarity and cold logic, as demonstrated when he murders David Bull to conceal his own miscalculations. He harbors grudges for decades and pursues revenge methodically, as shown by his destruction of Adam Ducket forty years after the boy's father humiliated him.

Despite his cunning, he is never truly trusted by his superiors at the Exchequer, a fact that puzzles him but that speaks to an essential hollowness in his character that others perceive even if he cannot.

Life and Events

1170: The Coronation Crime

In June 1170, on the night after the coronation of Henry the Young King, Pentecost joins a gang of wealthy young Londoners on a drunken rampage. They attack the wrong house – an armourer named Simon instead of a baker – and in the ensuing fight, Simon's apprentice is killed. Though equally guilty, Pentecost escapes justice by claiming benefit of clergy. His accomplices are hanged at Smithfield after trial by ordeal, but the ecclesiastical court, through bribery and false oaths from family connections, declares him innocent.

The incident has profound consequences. Bishop Gilbert Foliot, though forced to acquit him, vows that Pentecost will never receive church preferment in his diocese. Simon the armourer confronts Pentecost at the execution, whispering: "You're slime... You're a murderer too, as much as them. But you're worse. Because they're going to die and you aren't, Judas. You're too much of a coward."

Most traumatic of all is King Henry II's encounter with Pentecost in Westminster Hall. Recognizing him as a "criminous clerk" who escaped justice, Henry erupts in one of his legendary Plantagenet rages, promising personally to have Pentecost's case reopened and to hang him "until you rot." Pentecost flees in absolute panic, seeking sanctuary in St Mary-le-Bow.

1171-1189: The Long Career at the Exchequer

Becket's murder and subsequent canonization inadvertently save Pentecost. With King Henry desperate to avoid antagonizing the Church further, the issue of criminous clerks becomes untouchable. Pentecost returns to the Exchequer, where his extraordinary calculating abilities make him indispensable despite his lack of trustworthiness. He marries, fathers three children, and inherits a large fortune when his father dies. Yet advancement eludes him.

1189: New Patron and the Jewish Pogrom

With Richard I's accession, Pentecost finds an ambitious patron in William Longchamp, soon to be Chancellor of England. Longchamp recognizes Pentecost's dependence on patronage and takes him up as "a creature to serve him who would be entirely dependent on his good will."

Pentecost proves his value by ferreting out widows and heiresses to sell to the highest bidders for the crusade. Among these is Ida, whom he sells to Sampson Bull. When a pogrom erupts on coronation day, it is Pentecost who, surprisingly, helps Brother Michael save Abraham the Jewish moneylender by taking him to the Tower's protection.

1189-1191: The Longchamp Crisis

Pentecost becomes Bull's unlikely friend, though the relationship is carefully cultivated for political intelligence. He presents himself as the king's man to young David Bull, even giving the boy a tour of the Exchequer. During this period, he notices that Simon the armourer has left a son with a distinctive white patch in his hair – "a score to settle there," he thinks, already plotting revenge decades in advance.

As Prince John begins maneuvering against Longchamp, Pentecost anxiously seeks information from Bull about London's loyalties. Bull skillfully manipulates him, feeding him alarming but vague reports about John's growing influence, using Pentecost to pressure Longchamp into being more accommodating to the city's interests.

October 1191: The Murder of David Bull

When the crisis comes, Pentecost's survival instincts and moral bankruptcy combine in his most heinous act. After joining a patrol that encounters John's knights, Pentecost accidentally kills Sir William de Montvent in self-defense. But when David Bull appears and reveals that the aldermen have betrayed Longchamp by making a deal with John, Pentecost realizes his patron is doomed.

His calculation is swift and cold: he cannot afford to be known as Longchamp's man with John victorious, but David has seen him kill John's knight. "Then, with a single, easy thrust, he plunged the knight's sword into David Bull's heart." He arranges the scene to make it appear the knight and boy killed each other, then immediately goes to Bull to ask him to put in a good word with John's party. When Bull asks if Pentecost has seen his missing son, Pentecost lies: "No, I haven't."

1191-1215: The Surviving Clerk

The commune is proclaimed, Longchamp is deposed, and Pentecost survives by his timely defection. Over the following decades, he continues at the Exchequer, never advancing but never dismissed due to his phenomenal memory and calculating ability. He becomes "a bit of a joke" – stories of Henry II chasing him from Westminster Hall circulate in multiple amusing versions, and his last-minute changes of allegiance are cautionary tales.

1215: The Revenge on Adam Ducket

After the signing of Magna Carta, the elderly Silversleeves takes shelter from rain in a cottage near Windsor and notices the peasant has a white patch in his hair – Simon the armourer's grandson, Adam Ducket. Pentecost immediately begins plotting.

Through legal technicality and manipulation, Pentecost proves before the Guildhall that Adam, despite being a citizen and fishmonger, is technically still a serf because his Windsor land is held by labour service. Using Domesday Book itself as evidence, and producing carefully prepared witnesses, Pentecost succeeds in having Adam expelled from citizenship. Sampson Bull tries to defend Adam, noting that Silversleeves had quarreled with Simon the armourer decades before, but the legal case is airtight.

Adam loses his craft, his citizenship, and his betrothed Lucy Barnikel, who cannot marry a serf. After more than forty years, Pentecost finally has his revenge for Simon's humiliation.

c. 1224: Blindness and Miracle

In his final years, Pentecost goes blind. Sister Mabel, with characteristic charity, takes him for weekly walks despite his past wickedness. She particularly wants him to help the impoverished daughter of the curate of St Lawrence Silversleeves, but the Silversleeves family refuses.

Mabel brings the blind Pentecost to the chapel of St Thomas Becket on London Bridge and insists he pray for forgiveness. There, miraculously, his sight is restored. When he realizes it was Becket – of the family his own family had tried to destroy – who obtained the miracle, the irony is complete. Chastened, he agrees to help the curate's daughter.

Pentecost dies shortly after, having lived far longer than any of his victims.

Relationships

King Henry II

Their single encounter in Westminster Hall in 1170 is seared into Pentecost's memory. Henry's explosive Plantagenet rage – "You long-nosed son of a whore! You hypocritical, half-baked priest... You shall taste my justice, you stitched-up sack of slime. You shall smell death!" – terrifies Pentecost utterly. Only Becket's murder saves him from the king's promised vengeance. The incident becomes legendary at the Exchequer, told in multiple versions as a cautionary and amusing tale about the cowardly clerk.

The Becket Family

The ancient enmity between the Silversleeves and Becket families forms a dark backdrop to Pentecost's life. He grows up with his family's conviction that "if the Beckets drove men to dark deeds, it was they, the cursed Beckets, who were to blame." When Becket becomes Archbishop, Pentecost's father dismisses him as "typical of a Becket... just showing off as usual."

Yet Becket's murder and canonization inadvertently save Pentecost's life, and the family is forced to bless the Becket name "for the first time in many generations." The ultimate irony comes when the blind Pentecost's sight is miraculously restored in St Thomas Becket's chapel – forced to acknowledge a debt to the very family his own tried to destroy.

Simon the Armourer and Adam Ducket

After Simon confronts Pentecost at Smithfield in 1170, calling him "slime" and "Judas," Pentecost carries the humiliation for forty-five years. When he discovers Simon's grandson Adam in 1215, he immediately plots revenge. His destruction of Adam through legal manipulation is methodical and complete, demonstrating both his patience in pursuing grudges and his skill at exploiting technicalities. The revenge is particularly cruel because it strips Adam not just of livelihood but of his very identity as a citizen.

William Longchamp

Pentecost's relationship with his patron is entirely mercenary on both sides. Longchamp recognizes Pentecost as someone who "would be entirely dependent on his good will" and uses him accordingly. Pentecost serves enthusiastically, hoping for the wealth and preferment that eluded him for two decades. When the political winds shift in October 1191, Pentecost abandons Longchamp instantly, even helping compose a letter to Richard defending the chancellor one evening, then deserting to John's party the next.

Sampson Bull

Their unlikely friendship is built on calculation and manipulation from both sides. Pentecost cultivates Bull for political intelligence, trying to be "studiously polite" to Ida and friendly to David. Bull, more cleverly, uses Pentecost to influence Longchamp on London's behalf, feeding him alarming information about John to keep him worried. Neither man trusts the other, yet each finds the relationship useful. The relationship's true nature is revealed when Pentecost murders Bull's beloved son to save himself, then brazenly asks Bull for a favor the same night while lying about David's whereabouts.

Sister Mabel

In his final years, Mabel becomes Pentecost's unlikely caretaker, walking the blind old man weekly despite knowing his wickedness. She represents Christian charity confronting unrepentant evil, yet she is also practical enough to use his vulnerability to pressure him into helping the curate's daughter. When she brings him to Becket's chapel and his sight is miraculously restored, it is Mabel's persistence in confronting him with his sins that creates the conditions for grace to operate.

Historical Context

Pentecost's life spans one of the most dramatic periods in English history: the reigns of Henry II, Richard I, and John, encompassing the Becket controversy, the Third Crusade, the Longchamp crisis, and the signing of Magna Carta. His career at the Exchequer places him at the heart of the Plantagenet administrative revolution that transformed English governance.

The Becket crisis that begins in Pentecost's youth raises fundamental questions about Church and State relations, particularly the issue of "criminous clerks" – men in minor orders who claimed immunity from secular justice. Pentecost is a living example of the system's potential for abuse. Though guilty of murder, he escapes hanging because of a technicality, demonstrating exactly what King Henry II found so infuriating about Becket's defense of clerical privilege.

The Exchequer where Pentecost works represents medieval administration at its most sophisticated. The chequered cloth serving as a manual computer, the Pipe Rolls preserving financial records, the system of tallies and counterfoils – all represent a level of bureaucratic organization remarkable for its time. That Pentecost, for all his moral bankruptcy, is indispensable due to his phenomenal memory speaks to the value placed on administrative competence.

The political crisis of 1191 reflects the instability created when Richard I left his kingdom to crusade, leaving no clear authority structure. Prince John's maneuvering, Longchamp's downfall, and London's emergence as a political force powerful enough to extract the commune in exchange for its support all represent the shifting balance of power between monarchy, nobility, and rising urban centers.

The 1215 Magna Carta, at which the elderly Pentecost is present, represents the culmination of these tensions. His observation that the charter's provisions would make "England just like a commune" with the twenty-five barons as aldermen and the king as mayor is perceptive – London's model of government is indeed influencing national politics. The charter's provision removing kiddles from the Thames represents the kind of specific grievance, accumulated over decades, that fuels broader political change.

Themes and Significance

Pentecost Silversleeves embodies several of the novel's central themes about survival, moral compromise, and the gap between appearance and reality. Unlike the novel's more straightforward villains, Pentecost is primarily a coward rather than actively evil – yet his cowardice produces consequences as terrible as any malicious intent.

His life illustrates how institutional systems can protect the guilty and perpetuate injustice. As a criminous clerk, he escapes justice not because he is innocent but because of a legal technicality. The same mastery of technicality later allows him to destroy Adam Ducket forty years after the original injury. His survival while more honest men fall around him suggests that moral integrity is often a handicap in the bureaucratic and political world.

The bitter irony of the Becket connection runs throughout his story. The family his own family tried to destroy inadvertently saves his life, and ultimately restores his sight through a miracle. It suggests a divine justice operating on timescales and through means that human beings cannot predict or control.

Pentecost's phenomenal mental abilities contrasted with his moral emptiness raise questions about the relationship between intelligence and virtue. He is brilliant at calculation but incapable of genuine thought; his mind can master the Pipe Rolls but cannot grasp why his superiors never trust him. His teachers' complaint that learning comes "too easily to him, so he won't really work" applies equally to moral development – neither success nor failure teaches him anything.

His treatment of David Bull represents the story's darkest exploration of expedience. David is young, innocent, admiring, trusting – everything Pentecost once was before cynicism consumed him. The murder is accomplished with cold calculation, demonstrating how completely moral sense can atrophy when survival is the only value. That Pentecost then asks Bull for a favor the same night, while lying about David's fate, shows depravity so complete it approaches the inhuman.

Yet the novel refuses easy judgments. Pentecost lives to extreme old age while more virtuous characters die young. His blindness and miracle suggest the possibility of redemption even for the seemingly irredeemable, though whether his deathbed help for the curate's daughter represents genuine conversion or final expedience remains ambiguous. Sister Mabel's charitable care for him in old age demonstrates that Christian love extends even to the manifestly unlovable, though her pragmatic manipulation of his guilt suggests that even charity operates strategically in this world.

Pentecost represents the bureaucrat as survivor, the man for whom all principles are negotiable and loyalty always conditional. In the turbulent world of Plantagenet politics, where kings and chancellors rise and fall, where crusades fail and charters are signed, Pentecost endures by perfecting the art of changing allegiance at exactly the right moment. He is contemptible, yet there is a kind of awful perfection to his adaptation. He embodies the question: in a world of competing loyalties and deadly consequences, is there a point where survival itself becomes a form of corruption?
