Overview

Cerdic is a wealthy Saxon merchant and trader who operates from both his ancestral estate at Bocton in Kent and a trading post at Lundenwic on the River Thames. A man of absolute certainty and rigid principles, he sees the world in strict terms of right and wrong with no middle ground. His conversion to Christianity and demand that his pagan wife Elfgiva follow suit creates the central crisis of the chapter, nearly destroying his marriage before a reconciliation occurs at the midwinter Yuletide feast.

Physical Description

Cerdic is described as a large, bluff man, a Saxon to the core. He is fair-haired and blue-eyed, with a full beard, though the hair on his head is thinning. His complexion suggests that when angry, he could become flushed even to apoplexy. His broad Germanic face has high cheekbones that suggest a measured, even cold strength and authority. His men say of him: "Strong as a bull, but hard as an oak tree."

Character and Personality

Cerdic's most defining characteristic is his absolute, binary view of the world. Whatever he must decide – a course of action, a man's character, a question of guilt or innocence – he sees only a right answer and a wrong answer, with nothing in between. Once his intelligent mind is made up, it snaps shut like an iron trap. "Things are only black and white to Cerdic, never grey," his associates say.

He is known for never breaking his word once given, which has become a great asset to him as a trader. He is also shrewd and pragmatic in business dealings – he never lies, but he is clever about the exact wording of his commitments.

Cerdic is a man of authority who expects obedience, especially from his family. He can be very frightening when he chooses, using his burly physique and force of character to intimidate. Yet he also has capacity for affection and pride in his family, particularly respecting his youngest son Wistan's courage even while punishing his defiance.

Despite his harsh exterior, there are glimpses of warmth – his face breaks into a "mellow smile of deep satisfaction" when viewing the magnificent landscape at Bocton, and he experiences genuine joy at reconciliation with his wife.

Life and Events

Background and Establishment

Cerdic's family has been in Kent since the first Saxon and Jutish settlement, approximately a century and a half before the events of the chapter. Their ancestral estate at Bocton lies on a great ridge with extensive fields, apple orchards, productive oak woodlands, and a quarry of Kentish ragstone. The estate commands a magnificent view across the Weald of Kent, one of the finest outlooks in southern England.

As a young man, Cerdic established a second residence at the small trading post of Lundenwic on the River Thames. From there he receives and ships goods, setting out with strings of packhorses to visit all parts of the island. This trade has made him rich indeed. His activities include trading in pottery, wool, beautifully worked swords, Saxon metalwork, and hunting dogs. Most profitably, he deals in slaves, whom he sells to Frisian traders.

604: Conversion and Crisis

In 604, Cerdic travels to Canterbury to attend the court of King Ethelbert of Kent, whose family has had long ties to Cerdic's ancestors – Ethelbert gave rings (the most intimate token between lord and his men) to Cerdic's own father. At Canterbury, Cerdic is baptized as a Christian earlier in the year, following King Ethelbert's conversion.

The king and Queen Bertha entrust Cerdic with an important mission: to support the missionary work of Bishop Mellitus, who plans to establish a cathedral at Lundenwic. Though Cerdic trades from Lundenwic (which is technically in the Kingdom of Essex), he is a loyal man of Kent, making him ideally positioned to help the Church's expansion across the Thames Estuary.

While in Kent, Cerdic visits an estate near Bocton where he arranges a potential marriage with a fifteen-year-old girl named Edith. The arrangement is that if Cerdic divorces his wife Elfgiva, the girl will marry him; if not, she will marry his eldest son.

The Ultimatum

Upon his return to Lundenwic, Cerdic issues his wife a stark ultimatum: she must be baptized as a Christian when Bishop Mellitus arrives, or he will replace her with a new wife. His reasoning is absolutist – as a loyal man of King Ethelbert who has become Christian, his wife's duty is clear. Her refusal to follow him seems to him a profound disloyalty after twenty-five years of marriage. In his binary view, there is a right course and a wrong course, black and white, and Elfgiva's duty is obvious.

Cerdic confronts his four sons as well, demanding they take the new religion as he has. When they resist, citing that even the sons of the King of Essex are refusing to follow their father, Cerdic reddened with anger. He is vague about Christianity's details himself and worried by his sons' point about Christ letting himself be killed, but he insists on their obedience to King Ethelbert.

The Incident at St. Paul's

When Bishop Mellitus first visits Lundenwic to choose the site for his cathedral on the western hill of the abandoned Roman city, a humiliating incident occurs. Elfgiva, thinking to please her husband, urges their four unbaptized sons to go forward for communion. The boys, understanding nothing of the ceremony, crudely ask for "magic bread" and reach out to grab it. Mellitus is furious at this desecration of the Host, and publicly rebukes Cerdic for failing to instruct his sons properly.

The public humiliation is devastating for Cerdic. He has never thought of himself as weak or foolish, yet now he has been shamed before the king's man. His throat feels dry, his face flushes red. Though at the back of his mind he realizes Elfgiva did not deliberately disgrace him, it seems to him that this is all her fault – none of this would have happened but for her obstinacy. Coldly and deliberately, he strikes her across the face and tells her: "I see you no longer wish to be my wife."

He then forces his sons to be immediately baptized in the freezing waters of the River Fleet.

The Failed Remarriage

Cerdic sends a messenger to Kent to claim his young bride. However, his youngest son Wistan, deeply troubled by his mother's treatment, secretly rides to the girl Edith's home and tells her everything about the situation. She changes her mind about the marriage.

When news arrives that the bride will not come, Cerdic instantly guesses what happened. In an apoplexy of fury, he seizes a stock whip and flogs Wistan, who flees. Cerdic might have killed the boy if he had not escaped.

For days, Cerdic stomps about in silent fury. Gradually, however, he begins to feel weariness and secretly misses the comfort of his old marriage. Despite this, when he finally offers to take Elfgiva back if she will be baptized, she refuses. He storms out in even greater rage.

Yuletide and Reconciliation

As Yuletide approaches, Bishop Mellitus returns with a large party to preach and baptize. At the service, Mellitus preaches a moving sermon about Christ's sacrifice, forgiveness, and reconciliation. He tells the congregation that if Christ could lay down his life for mankind, they should be ready to sacrifice themselves and be reconciled with one another. "If you have quarrelled with your neighbour, your servant or your wife, go now and make amends."

During the feast that night, the merchant has not slept with his wife for weeks. On the eve of the year's shortest day, he encounters his slave girl Ricola near her hut. Drawn by desire and perhaps by rejection, he spends the night with her.

However, Elfgiva, who witnesses the beginning of this encounter, spends the long midwinter night wrestling with her decision. She realizes her pride has brought her no happiness. As dawn approaches, she decides to take control of her marriage again. The next morning, she tells Cerdic she will be baptized, but adds firmly: "The slave girl goes."

Cerdic embraces her with relief and agrees immediately. "Whatever you want," he says. "After all, she belongs to you."

Business and Baptism

Cerdic demonstrates his shrewd business practices when a Frisian slave trader arrives. Months earlier, Cerdic had bought northern slaves cheaply by letting the merchant think he would have to house and feed them all winter. Now he sells them to the Frisian (the only trader who makes the dangerous winter journey) at a good profit. He also offers to sell Offa and Ricola as a "goodwill gesture" at a discounted price, efficiently removing them from his household as his wife demands.

When Ricola screams that she is pregnant, trying to reach Elfgiva's mercy, Cerdic is tempted to give the girl a present as she leaves but "thought better of it" – a rare glimpse of sentiment restrained by practicality.

In the fourth month of the year, at the spring festival of Eostre, Cerdic leads his wife to the River Fleet where Bishop Mellitus baptizes her, giving her the new name Godiva ("Gift of God"). By this time, Elfgiva is pregnant with what will be their daughter.

Relationships

Elfgiva-Godiva

Cerdic and Elfgiva have been married for twenty-five years and have four grown sons together. By all accounts, it has been a happy marriage until the crisis of his conversion. They have "loved each other as man and wife for so many years," which makes her refusal to convert seem to him all the more disloyal.

Yet Cerdic's approach to the crisis reveals the limitations of his character. He sees only right and wrong, duty and disobedience. He cannot understand that Elfgiva needs tenderness, that she is hurt and afraid. When she desperately tries to win him back through seduction during the weeks of crisis, he allows himself to be "contented" but makes no definitive move to reconcile. He leaves her "like some poor animal tethered and forgotten in a storm."

The Church's prohibition on polygamy and divorce is something Cerdic does not know – the missionaries wisely follow the rule of "First convert them to the faith, then start to change their customs." He believes he is within his legal rights under Saxon law to replace his wife.

Despite the coldness of his ultimatum, there are signs of Cerdic's underlying attachment. He misses "the comfort of his old marriage." When he sees Elfgiva graciously directing the household during Mellitus's visit, "more than once Cerdic glanced at her with quiet admiration." Their reconciliation brings him genuine joy, and their daughter conceived on Modranecht (mothers' night) suggests a renewed intimacy and warmth.

Wistan

Of his four sons, Cerdic has perhaps the most complex relationship with his youngest, Wistan. When Wistan bravely confronts him about his treatment of Elfgiva, Cerdic uses his burly physique and frightening authority to knock the boy aside, bellowing at him to be quiet. Yet as he marches away, he thinks secretly: "But that boy's the best of the lot."

When Cerdic discovers that Wistan sabotaged his remarriage by visiting Edith, he flies into such fury that he seizes a stock whip and flogs the boy. If Wistan had not fled, "Cerdic might almost have killed him." Yet during Bishop Mellitus's visit, when Wistan miraculously reappears to help with preparations, "Cerdic decided to ignore it" – tacit forgiveness of his favored son.

King Ethelbert and Queen Bertha

Cerdic's loyalty to the King of Kent is absolute. His ancestors were loyal companions of the first Kentish kings, and King Ethelbert gave rings to Cerdic's own father. When the king puts his hand on Cerdic's shoulder and says "you, my good Cerdic, I know I can trust absolutely," this trust is entirely warranted. Cerdic's conversion to Christianity stems primarily from this loyalty.

Queen Bertha tells him they are "always so glad to see you at our court at Canterbury," reflecting the esteem in which he is held.

Bishop Mellitus

Cerdic's relationship with the bishop is complex. He deeply respects Mellitus's authority and mission, providing him support and accommodation. However, Mellitus publicly humiliates him over the communion incident, rebuking him for failing to instruct his sons properly. Later, at the baptism, when Mellitus tells him "You must show Christian charity, Cerdic," the bishop attempts to mediate between husband and wife, though this brings limited immediate results.

Despite these tensions, Cerdic provides substantial support for the construction of St. Paul's cathedral, supplying laborers and ensuring work proceeds rapidly.

Historical Context

Cerdic lives during the pivotal moment of Anglo-Saxon England's conversion to Christianity. In 597, Pope Gregory the Great sent the monk Augustine to convert the Anglo-Saxons, beginning at Canterbury in Kent. By 604, the mission is expanding beyond Kent to establish Christianity on both sides of the Thames Estuary.

Cerdic represents the Saxon nobility who, through loyalty to their kings, facilitate this religious transformation despite limited understanding of Christian theology. His pragmatic acceptance of Christianity contrasts with his wife's deeper spiritual resistance, reflecting different responses to imposed cultural change.

As a merchant, Cerdic participates in the extensive trade networks of the early 7th century, dealing with Frisian, Germanic, and Norse traders. His trade in slaves reflects the reality that Britain had become well known for supplying slaves throughout Europe – so much so that Pope Gregory, seeing fair-haired English slaves in Rome, famously said "They are not Angles, but angels."

Cerdic's wealth and position also illustrate the social structure of Saxon England, where loyalty to one's lord (through gifts like rings) created bonds of obligation that extended across kingdoms. His dual residences at Bocton and Lundenwic show how Saxon nobles could maintain both agricultural estates and commercial enterprises.

Themes and Significance

Certainty and Rigidity

Cerdic embodies the dangers and strengths of absolute certainty. His binary worldview – seeing only right and wrong, black and white – gives him clarity of purpose and makes him reliable in business and loyal in service to his king. Yet this same rigidity nearly destroys his marriage and blinds him to his wife's emotional needs.

His character demonstrates how principle without compassion can become tyranny, even when the principle itself may have merit. The bishop's sermon about reconciliation and sacrifice speaks directly to this limitation in Cerdic's character.

Conversion and Cultural Change

Through Cerdic, the novel explores how Christianity spread through Anglo-Saxon England not through theological conviction but through networks of loyalty and obligation. Cerdic converts because he is "a loyal man of King Ethelbert," not because he understands or embraces Christian doctrine. He admits to being "a little vague about the details of Christianity" even after his baptism.

This pragmatic, surface-level conversion – multiply by thousands across England – illustrates how profound cultural transformations often occur through social pressure rather than spiritual awakening. The missionaries understand this, following the rule: "First convert them to the faith, then start to change their customs."

Commerce and Human Value

Cerdic's involvement in the slave trade, treated as routine business, highlights the moral blind spots of his society. He shows the same shrewd calculation in selling Ricola and Offa that he shows in any transaction. His brief temptation to give Ricola "a present" as she leaves suggests some flicker of conscience, but he "thought better of it" – sentiment suppressed by practical considerations.

The chapter juxtaposes Cerdic's mercantile success with the human cost of his transactions, particularly in Ricola's desperate attempts to communicate her pregnancy as she's being sold away.

Authority and Family

Cerdic's crisis reveals tensions between different forms of authority in Saxon society – royal authority, religious authority, paternal authority, and marital relationships. His assumption that obedience is owed to him in all matters runs up against his wife's spiritual autonomy and his youngest son's moral courage.

Ultimately, reconciliation comes not through Cerdic relaxing his demands, but through Elfgiva choosing to yield. Yet the reconciliation is genuine and fruitful, suggesting that in Saxon society, harmony required acceptance of hierarchical relationships even when those relationships were imperfect.

The Merchant as Historical Figure

Cerdic represents the entrepreneurial spirit that maintained trade networks during the early Anglo-Saxon period, a time often dismissed as the "Dark Ages." His operation from Lundenwic, his dealings with Continental traders, and his success in accumulating wealth all demonstrate the continued vitality of commerce even as the old Roman urban civilization lay in ruins nearby.

His shrewdness in business dealings – never lying, but carefully wording his commitments – shows the importance of reputation and trust in an age when trade relied on personal relationships rather than written contracts or legal enforcement.
