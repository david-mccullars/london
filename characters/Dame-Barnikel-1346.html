Overview

Dame Barnikel is a formidable tavern-keeper and brewer who runs the George Inn in Southwark during the late 14th century. Born a Barnikel of Billingsgate, she is twice-widowed by age thirty and renowned for her forceful personality, brewing skills, and independence. Despite marrying the quiet grocer John Fleming, she retains her maiden name and her business, defying convention to remain a member of the Brewers Guild. Her larger-than-life presence dominates the George, where she presides with authority, compassion, and an occasional drunken roar.

Physical Description

At age thirty (in 1376), Dame Barnikel is described as "magnificent." She stands half a head taller than her second husband Fleming, with dark red hair that she pulls back "like an Amazon." Even the critical Gilbert Bull admits she is "a fine-looking woman." Her physical presence matches her personality—she is strong enough to pick up and toss the apprentice Ducket into a storehouse "as easily as if he were a child." Her voice can "boom an indiscretion across the street or share a secret in a husky growl," and when drunk and crossed, "she could roar like a Viking in battle."

She dresses boldly and colorfully, flouting sumptuary laws that restrict dress by social class. If a headdress, bright silk, or rich fur takes her fancy, she wears it regardless of regulations. When beadles come to complain to Fleming about her violations, the grocer only shrugs and suggests they "talk to her" themselves—at which they hurriedly leave.

Character and Personality

Dame Barnikel embodies contradiction: she is both nurturing and fierce, practical yet emotional, conventional in some ways and wildly independent in others. Her force of character is such that even aldermen defer to her, adding the honorific "Dame" to her name as though she were an alderman's wife. One alderman admits: "I'd be afraid she'd cut my head off if I didn't."

Her independence is remarkable for her era. Though widows commonly continued family businesses, she goes further—after marrying Fleming, a grocer, she refuses to give up her own trade as tavern-keeper and brewer, announcing she will continue in defiance of guild custom. "None of the brewers had dared to argue." She maintains strict boundaries: "He has his business and I have mine," she explains, never allowing Fleming to work at the George.

She is happiest when brewing ale, a process she approaches with joy and reverence. She teaches young Geoffrey Ducket about brewing, calling yeast "God-is-Good" and murmuring "Manna from heaven" as she works. Her rich barley ale is renowned throughout Southwark.

Despite her strength, Dame Barnikel has a tender side. She worries deeply about her daughter Amy, though she struggles to understand the quiet, pale girl who is so unlike herself. She can be manipulative in pursuit of what she believes is Amy's good—trying to push Ducket toward her daughter and away from Ben Carpenter—but her intentions come from love.

Once a month she gets drunk, and during these episodes her behavior becomes unpredictable and forceful. In one such state, she locks Ducket and Amy in a storehouse together, declaring "Time you two got to know one another."

Her judgement of character proves sound, if sometimes slow to develop. She initially suspects Ducket of theft, keeping a watchful, cold eye on him for months. But when the truth emerges, she is quick to admit her error and generous in her praise: "I can't get over how wrong I was about that boy. He's a little hero... He's a plucky, loyal fellow."

Life and Events

Early Life and First Marriage (c. 1346-1362)

Born a Barnikel of Billingsgate, she comes from an ancient Danish family of fishmongers. At age thirteen (c. 1359) she marries a tavern-keeper and inherits the George Inn in Southwark when he dies, reportedly of exhaustion. Tavern gossips later joke that "the betting was five-to-two for a third [husband] within seven years" and that "the first man, they said, had died of exhaustion."

Second Marriage to Fleming (1363-1382)

Only a year after being widowed at age sixteen, she marries John Fleming, a quiet, spindly grocer with a "concave face." The match astonishes everyone—people "could never believe they were man and wife" and "never thought he could stay the course." Why she chose him "remained one of the mysteries in an otherwise ordered universe."

She bears a daughter, Amy, who takes after her father in appearance and temperament. Dame Barnikel finds herself puzzled by the pale, quiet girl who "just wants to get married" and shows no interest in the brewery business.

Running the George (1376-1382)

By 1376, at age thirty, Dame Barnikel presides splendidly over the George. The inn features a large hall for poorer travelers, chambers on three floors for better-off guests, and her own small brewery. She sits by the bar in the evenings, with a heavy oak club always within reach for trouble-makers and a huge ancient Toby jug in the shape of an alderman on the bar before her.

She takes a liking to Fleming's apprentice Geoffrey Ducket, admiring his cheerful spirit despite his being "a funny-looking fellow and a foundling." She attempts matchmaking, trying to steer Amy away from the "dull" and "moonstruck" carpenter Ben Carpenter and toward Ducket. She confronts Ducket directly: "Do you really think I'm going to pass all this," she gestures at the George, "to that moonfaced little Carpenter?"

In one memorable drunken episode, she physically forces both Ducket and Amy into a storehouse, tossing the young man in "as easily as if he were a child," and locks them inside together, growling: "Time you two got to know one another."

The Poll Tax Crisis (1379-1380)

When tax collectors arrive to assess the first poll tax, Fleming shocks everyone by confessing he has no money. Dame Barnikel, who has paid her own assessment of two marks, questions him in their bedchamber. She cannot understand how their savings have vanished. Her practical mind immediately suspects theft: "Do you mean there should have been more in the strongbox?" When Fleming mentions only he, Dame Barnikel, and Ducket know where it's kept, she begins to watch the apprentice carefully.

She visits Gilbert Bull to inquire whether Ducket might be a thief. Bull discusses the matter with his daughter Tiffany, and Dame Barnikel begins keeping a cold, watchful eye on Ducket. Her suspicions continue for months, creating tension in the household.

During this period, she also demonstrates political awareness, commenting sharply on the second poll tax in 1380: "We shall actually pay less because we were well enough off to be assessed high last time. But do you realize what this means for the peasant? A shilling for himself. Another for his wife... Altogether, several weeks' wages. How the devil are they going to find that?" She shakes her head: "Bad business."

The Peasants' Revolt (June 1381)

When Wat Tyler's rebels approach London in June 1381, Dame Barnikel prepares to defend the George. She stands before the door with a heavy club, grim-faced, having inspected and barred the premises. Ducket and Carpenter pass by her position as they're swept along with the crowd, but she's unable to stop them.

Fleming's Death and Its Aftermath (1382)

When Fleming's involvement with the fraudulent alchemist Benedict Silversleeves is exposed by Ducket, the grocer hangs himself in shame. Dame Barnikel and Ducket discover him in the storehouse. Fleming's suicide note claims he stole the poll tax money and other funds, trying to make more through alchemy, and asks that Ducket take over the business.

Reading the letter, Dame Barnikel turns to Ducket and admits honestly: "I thought you stole it." When he confirms Fleming didn't really mean to steal—he was trying to multiply their wealth—she accepts this. She remarks "He didn't have to do this," but Ducket understands Fleming died of shame.

Dame Barnikel's response shows her practical nature: "You'd better take over, then," she tells Ducket gruffly. Later, she becomes deeply contrite about her long suspicion of him.

Recognition of Ducket's Worth

In conversation with Amy, Dame Barnikel acknowledges her misjudgment: "I can't get over how wrong I was about that boy. He's a little hero. Look at what he's done. Saved Carpenter's life. Suspected of theft. Took the blame for your father. Tried to save him too, apparently. Then Bull cuts him off... And never a whimper. He's a plucky, loyal fellow. Loyal."

She tries to persuade Amy not to marry Carpenter: "I know you want to get away from me. But don't marry Carpenter. You know you don't love him."

Third Marriage to James Bull (1382)

After Fleming's death, Dame Barnikel notices James Bull, a distant cousin of Gilbert Bull, frequenting the George. He sits morosely, his hopes of winning Tiffany Bull having been dashed by her betrothal to Silversleeves (later broken) and then to Ducket.

Dame Barnikel decides to "take him under her wing." Whenever James comes in, she is "wreathed in smiles," purring at him: "Here's this handsome man again." She makes "the big, spare fellow feel attractive." She tells Amy: "A man is what you make of him... You must learn to bring a man out; you never know what may be in there."

On the eve of Amy's wedding to Carpenter (after Tiffany and Ducket's wedding), Dame Barnikel makes an announcement: "I'm going to marry James." She has decided "she could make something of him," while James has concluded that the George is "a good and solid business" even if not the great fortune he'd dreamed of.

"He's going to become a brewer," she tells the guild, and they do not dare argue. Thus the Bull Brewery is born. The formidable dame "became quite girlish" about the prospect of being a bride again.

Later Life

Years later, when Tiffany (now married to Ducket and wealthy) tries to find a mistress for her widowed father Gilbert Bull to prevent him from remarrying and having more heirs, she approaches Dame Barnikel. Tiffany delicately suggests that Bull "has always had rather an admiration" for her.

Dame Barnikel sees through the scheme immediately: "You're trying to find your father a nice mistress... You're asking if I might know of anyone?" When Tiffany suggests Dame Barnikel herself, the tavern-keeper looks her "straight in the eye" and asks bluntly: "Lot of money at stake?" When Tiffany admits "Yes," Dame Barnikel laughs: "I don't think so. I've got a perfectly good Bull of my own."

Relationships

John Fleming

Her second husband remains a mystery to everyone, including perhaps Dame Barnikel herself. The match seems inexplicable—the spindly, concave-faced grocer with his quiet, scholarly interests seems the opposite of his magnificent, robust wife. Yet she married him freely, only a year after her first widowhood.

She maintains clear boundaries with him about their businesses, never letting him work at the George: "He has his business and I have mine." When his strange behavior and disappearances puzzle her, she tries to understand but cannot. During the poll tax crisis, she is "too concerned to be angry" when he confesses to having no money.

When Fleming's suicide reveals his involvement with alchemy and his shame over his failures, Dame Barnikel accepts it without drama or excess grief. Her practicality dominates—she focuses on settling affairs and ensuring Ducket takes over the business. This doesn't mean she's heartless; rather, she seems to have long accepted that Fleming was not a man she could fully understand or change. She reflects to Amy: "Not... that she had been able to make much of poor Fleming."

Amy Fleming

Dame Barnikel's relationship with her daughter is loving but strained by their opposite natures. Where Dame Barnikel is forceful and independent, Amy is quiet and passive. Where the mother loves brewing and business, the daughter "just wants to get married." Dame Barnikel often feels puzzled by Amy, wondering how to guide a girl so unlike herself.

Her attempts to direct Amy's romantic life show both her controlling nature and her genuine concern for her daughter's welfare. She tries to steer Amy toward Ducket and away from Carpenter, believing the craftsman is "dull" and "moonstruck." She tells Amy: "The reason you like him is that he's the first boy who's taken an interest in you. That's all." When Amy objects that her father likes Carpenter, Dame Barnikel respects this, making it "a point of honour" not to override Fleming's opinion "in matters concerning their daughter."

After Fleming's death, Dame Barnikel tries to prevent Amy's marriage to Carpenter with a direct appeal: "I know you want to get away from me. But don't marry Carpenter. You know you don't love him." Despite her efforts, Amy does marry Carpenter.

Geoffrey Ducket

Dame Barnikel's opinion of Ducket undergoes a complete transformation. Initially she likes the "funny-looking fellow" and "foundling," admiring his "cheerful spirit." She actively tries to matchmake him with Amy, seeing him as a better prospect than Carpenter.

When Fleming's money goes missing, she suspects Ducket of theft and keeps "a watchful" and "cold" eye on him for months. This false suspicion pains her deeply once the truth emerges. She becomes "not often contrite" but genuinely so when speaking to Amy: "I can't get over how wrong I was about that boy. He's a little hero... He's a plucky, loyal fellow. Loyal."

After Fleming's death, she accepts the grocer's designation of Ducket to take over the business: "You'd better take over, then," she says gruffly. This becomes the foundation of Ducket's fortune and eventual marriage to Tiffany Bull.

James Bull

With her third husband, Dame Barnikel seems to have found someone she can actually shape and manage. She sees him sitting morosely at the George, recognizes his need, and decides to "take him under her wing." She actively builds up his confidence, making him "feel attractive" with her attention.

She tells Amy: "A man is what you make of him... But now that young man needs looking after." When she decides to marry him, it's with clear-eyed assessment: "She had decided she could make something of him." For his part, James accepts that while the George isn't the great fortune he'd dreamed of through marrying Tiffany Bull, "it was a good and solid business."

Their partnership proves successful. Together they establish the Bull Brewery, and James becomes established as a brewer through her influence with the guild. Dame Barnikel "became quite girlish" about the prospect of this third marriage.

Historical Context

Dame Barnikel embodies the possibilities and limitations for women in 14th-century London. As a twice-widowed woman, she has unusual independence. Medieval London law and custom allowed widows to continue family businesses, and some guilds accepted female members. Dame Barnikel pushes these boundaries further than most, insisting on continuing her brewing business even after remarrying, when custom dictated she should give up her own trade.

Her membership in the Brewers Guild is significant. Medieval brewing was often women's work, with many "brewsters" operating small-scale operations. Dame Barnikel operates at a larger, more professional scale, running both an inn and a brewery. That the guild accepts her authority—and later accepts James Bull at her insistence—shows her force of personality but also the practical reality that capable women could hold economic power in medieval London.

The George Inn in Southwark places her in a liminal space—outside the city walls but crucial to London's economy. Southwark's inns served travelers, pilgrims heading to Canterbury, and merchants coming to London. It was also home to the Bishop of Winchester's licensed brothels ("the stews"). Dame Barnikel operates in this colorful, slightly lawless environment while maintaining respectability.

Her flouting of sumptuary laws reflects broader tensions in 14th-century society. These laws attempted to maintain social hierarchy through dress codes, but they were widely violated, especially by successful merchants and their families. That Dame Barnikel can wear what she pleases despite beadles' complaints shows both her personal boldness and the general difficulty of enforcing such regulations.

The economic crises of her era—the poll taxes of 1379 and 1380, the Peasants' Revolt of 1381—directly affect her household. Her political awareness is sharp; she immediately grasps the unfairness of the flat-rate poll tax that hits peasants disproportionately. During the revolt, she prepares to defend her property but doesn't panic. Her practical response to crisis is characteristic.

Themes and Significance

Dame Barnikel represents female power and independence in a male-dominated world. She achieves this not by rejecting femininity or domesticity but by expanding their boundaries. She runs a household and a business, nurtures her daughter and manages employees, brews ale with joy and defends her property with a club. Her insistence on keeping her maiden name symbolizes her refusal to be subsumed into her husbands' identities.

She embodies the practical, resilient spirit of London's working people. Unlike the anxious scholars and conflicted courtiers elsewhere in the novel, Dame Barnikel acts with decisive common sense. When crisis comes—whether rebellion, death, or financial ruin—she assesses the situation and moves forward. "You'd better take over, then," she tells Ducket after Fleming's suicide, wasting no time on prolonged grief.

Her character also explores the complexity of judgement. Her long suspicion of Ducket proves entirely wrong, and she admits her error fully. Yet this same judgemental streak helps her see through Tiffany's scheme years later, recognizing immediately that "a lot of money is at stake." She learns from mistakes but never loses her sharp eye for human nature.

The three marriages show different aspects of medieval marriage. The first remains mysterious (likely a conventional match), the second puzzles everyone (suggesting affection or at least tolerance of Fleming's oddities), and the third is openly pragmatic (mutual benefit and companionship). Through her, Rutherford suggests that medieval marriage encompassed many possibilities beyond romantic love.

Most significantly, Dame Barnikel creates her own sphere of authority. The George Inn is her domain, and even aldermen defer to her there. She cannot vote, hold political office, or enjoy full legal independence, but within her world, she rules absolutely. She represents the ways medieval women found agency within restrictive social structures—not by overturning the system but by claiming maximum space within it.
