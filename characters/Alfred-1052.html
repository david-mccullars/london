Overview

Alfred is a skilled armourer who rises from near-starvation as a teenage runaway to become the master armourer of London. Born near Windsor to a family of free smiths, he comes to London at age fourteen seeking opportunity and is rescued from vagrancy by Barnikel of Billingsgate. His remarkable craftsmanship, combined with unwavering loyalty to his benefactor, leads him into dangerous secret work manufacturing weapons for resistance against Norman rule. His webbed fingers, a distinctive family trait, never hinder his exceptional skill at the forge.

Physical Description

Alfred possesses several distinctive physical features mentioned in the text:

  • A white patch in his light brown hair (present from youth)
  • Blue eyes that twinkle with humor
  • Webbed fingers (a hereditary family trait)
  • By middle age: shock of white hair over his forehead became less noticeable as the rest turned grey, became rather stout

Character and Personality

Alfred demonstrates remarkable resilience and determination from an early age. When starving in London at fourteen, he refuses to give up and return home despite having no prospects, declaring "I'm not giving up, sir" to Barnikel—a response that wins the giant Dane's admiration and protection. This stubborn courage defines his character throughout his life.

His most notable quality is loyalty. Despite the enormous risks, Alfred continues to manufacture illegal weapons for Barnikel's resistance activities for over a decade, driven by gratitude for being saved from starvation. However, this loyalty is tempered by growing pragmatism and concern for his family. As he ages and gains more to lose, he becomes increasingly anxious about the danger his secret work poses to his wife and six children.

Alfred is also characterized by kindness and a desire to pass on the help he received. He befriends the disfigured young serf Osric, feeding him weekly and attempting multiple times to buy his freedom. This mirrors how Barnikel rescued him, showing Alfred's belief in paying forward the generosity shown to him.

Regarding his craft, Alfred exhibits both pride and humility. Though extraordinarily skilled—able to work alongside his master as an equal while still an apprentice—he initially conceals his abilities from fellow apprentices to avoid jealousy. His cunning extends beyond the forge: he devises ingenious hiding places for illegal weapons and carefully plans how to smuggle them without detection.

By the time of the Domesday survey, Alfred has developed a harder edge. When visiting his brother who has descended into serfdom, Alfred responds with disgust rather than compassion, revealing the successful man's impatience with poor relations who "gave up."

Life and Events

1066: Early Life and Arrival in London

Born around 1052 near Windsor in a rural hamlet twenty miles west of London, Alfred grows up in a family of free smiths. His family had fled to this area during King Alfred's reign to escape Viking raiders. The family takes pride in their free status—they pay money rent rather than labor service and aren't tied to any lord.

Alfred's grandfather was a smith, as is his father, and by age seven Alfred can shoe a horse. By twelve he handles the hammers nearly as well as his older brother, who is two years his senior. His father teaches him: "You don't have to be big and strong. Skill is what matters. Let your tools do the work for you." Despite his webbed fingers, Alfred becomes as skilled as his brother by age fourteen.

However, with only enough work for one smith in the village, Alfred decides to seek his fortune in London. When his mother warns him about Halley's Comet appearing in January 1066 as a bad omen, Alfred jokes: "You really think that God Almighty sent that star to warn me, Mother?" On impulse, after hearing his parents' concerns, he announces he'll leave the very next morning.

1066: Starvation and Rescue

Alfred's first months in London are desperate. He arrives knowing almost nothing about the city, having never been there before, drawn only by the family saying "There's buried gold in London." For a month he seeks work unsuccessfully; by the second month he's begging for food; by the third he's a vagrant living by the London Stone.

After three days without eating, wrapped in a filthy cloak and too weak to stand, Alfred is discovered by Barnikel of Billingsgate. The massive red-bearded Dane questions him about his trade, whether he's free, and whether he's stolen (only one barleycake that had fallen on the ground, Alfred admits). When Alfred tries to stand but collapses twice, Barnikel tosses him over one shoulder "like a small sack of flour" and carries him home.

At Barnikel's house, while his wife feeds Alfred broth, the Dane tests the boy's spirit. When told he must return home to Windsor, Alfred, though terrified of losing this meal, musters courage to refuse: "I'll not go back... I'll get by... I'm not giving up, sir." Barnikel roars with laughter: "In this boy dwells the heart of a hero... Because he won't give up!" The Dane declares he has work for Alfred.

1066: Apprenticeship at the Armoury

Barnikel takes Alfred to an armoury just outside the eastern wall of London, controlled by the city's Defence Guild. The master armourer, a tall, bony-faced man with mild blue eyes, initially doubts Alfred can do the work because of his webbed fingers, but Barnikel vouches for him.

As the newest apprentice, Alfred starts with menial tasks—fetching water, stoking fires, working the bellows. He lodges with eight other apprentices on Cornhill behind the armourer's sister's house. Though his true skill is considerable, Alfred deliberately makes mistakes to avoid arousing jealousy among the other apprentices. When the master catches him sabotaging a riveting job, Alfred explains his strategy: "If the other apprentices get jealous, they could make my life hell. Even drive me out. So I want them to think they're teaching me until we're friends."

The master begins keeping Alfred late, ostensibly for menial work, but actually to teach him advanced techniques normally reserved for senior apprentices. Working by lamplight, the master shows him the geometry and stress patterns of chainmail, how to achieve the perfect rivet, and the art of case-hardening iron into steel. They speak to no one of these sessions, though Alfred suspects Barnikel is kept informed.

Alfred also lives with Barnikel's family, eating hearty meals weekly and becoming "almost part of the family." He meets and admires Leofric's daughter Hilda, who is betrothed to Barnikel's son, and by midsummer is "half in love with her himself."

1066: Secret Weapons Manufacturing Begins

Some time after Barnikel's wife dies that summer, Alfred's secret career begins. Barnikel asks if he'll do "a little job... it could be dangerous." Without hesitation, Alfred agrees: "Of course"—he owes the Dane everything. The master armourer later reveals this involves making weapons for Barnikel, giving Alfred the chance to refuse. "I'll do it," Alfred says.

After that, master and apprentice often work late in the workshop after curfew. Since their work is ostensibly for Mandeville), their strange hours raise no suspicion. They carefully bar the door and keep official work visible so they can hide illegal arms while opening up.

For Alfred, this training is wonderful. He learns to make helmets, swords, shields, and spearheads by the dozen. His fingers fly through the work. As they store arms under the workshop floor, Alfred puzzles over who they're for. When Barnikel removes them with packhorses before a huge rebellion in the north and east, Alfred realizes he's supporting English resistance against Norman rule. Over the years, arms requests come months before each revolt, suggesting Barnikel's Viking trading network provides advance intelligence.

1066-1078: Establishing a Life

Over the following decade, Alfred's life transforms. He becomes a fully fledged armourer, eventually taking over from the old master. About four years after arriving in London (c. 1070), he marries. By 1078 he and his wife have six children.

Alfred builds a new house adjoining the armoury—a stout timber-framed structure with a large main room and a loft divided into two parts (one for him and his wife, one for their children). His wife is "a jolly, comfortable woman, the daughter of a butcher" who presides over her noisy household with ease and confidence, described as having "a loving husband and exactly one over the number of children she had always hoped for."

Despite increasing prosperity and respectability, Alfred continues his secret work for Barnikel, though with growing reluctance as his family expands. "I still owe it to him," he tells himself, but the fear of discovery weighs on him.

1078-1081: Befriending Osric

Around 1078, Alfred encounters the young serf Osric at the Tower construction site. He's been told he'll make the metal grilles for the drain and well. Watching Ralph Silversleeves brutally berate Osric, Alfred picks up a piece of Osric's fine woodwork and that night has a long conversation with the solemn young man.

Alfred reports to Barnikel: "I think I've found the little fellow we need." When Barnikel asks if Osric can be trusted with their lives and what he wants, Alfred grins: "He wants revenge."

Alfred and his wife take Osric under their wing. He eats with them weekly, and Alfred's wife becomes "a mother to the fellow. God knows he needs one." She presides over these meals warmly, and Alfred brings Osric into his family circle. When he notices Osric seems troubled and in love, his wife investigates and ultimately arranges for Osric to marry the girl Dorkes by promising to care for her crippled mother.

Alfred tries multiple times to buy Osric's freedom from serfdom, but Ralph always finds ways to block the purchase.

1078: The Wagon Smuggling Operation

When Barnikel needs to smuggle weapons out of London under increased Norman surveillance, Alfred and Osric devise an ingenious solution. Working secretly at night in a storeroom at Barnikel's house, Osric—under Alfred's admiring supervision—converts a huge wagon. Rather than creating an obvious secret compartment under the cart, Osric hollows out the solid beams of the wagon's frame, creating sliding panels and stops so perfectly crafted that the work is invisible. "The cart itself is built of arms!" Barnikel exclaims. The operation succeeds when Hilda drives the wagon past Ralph's search party, claiming to carry wine to her father-in-law.

1083: The Crisis of Conscience

By 1083, Alfred's secret activities have created an enormous hidden arsenal. Surveying the hoard under his workshop floor one day, he's horrified: "You could equip a hundred men." For the first time, real panic sets in. If the Normans ever raided the armoury and found these weapons, he could never explain them away.

The crisis comes when his wife surprises him hiding a sword. After her initial horror, she forces him to reveal everything and delivers an ultimatum: "How could you put us all at risk? You must stop helping Barnikel. For good. And the arms must go. If not, I go." Alfred discovers his usually comfortable wife is implacable on this point.

Alfred is secretly rather relieved to have an excuse to end the dangerous business, but faces an obvious problem: "Ralph's men guard the outside of the armoury. His spies are everywhere. Where can we hide the arms now? And even if I wanted to dump the arms in the river, how would we smuggle them out?"

1083: The Tower Operation

When Barnikel consults Osric, the young carpenter devises an audacious plan: hide the illegal weapons inside the Tower of London itself, in the very cellar where legal weapons are stored. Over a week of nights, Osric creates a secret chamber within the cellar's twenty-foot-thick wall, entering through the drain from the riverbank.

Alfred orchestrates the transfer brilliantly. He deliberately delays loading his weapons, making the guards wait until day's end. When he finally loads the carts, there's an even larger quantity than expected. Each rolled cloth contains two weapons—one legal, one illegal. Alfred brings Osric along as a helper, and in the confusion no one notices when Osric vanishes inside the Tower.

Working through the night, Osric transfers the illegal weapons to the secret chamber. Alfred has supplied him with keys to the grilles and tools for the work. The next morning, when Ralph finds Osric in the cellar and demands an explanation, Alfred's planning pays off—Osric claims to have been accidentally locked in, and Ralph, finding nothing suspicious, believes him. "This makes you the very first prisoner of the Tower," Ralph jokes, letting him go.

1083-1086: Growing Caution

The collapse of the Danish invasion in 1085 leaves Alfred increasingly convinced his secret work is futile. "I also think that it's all becoming a waste of time. The truth is, most Englishmen have accepted William now. They might not even fight for the Danes," he tells Barnikel.

When Barnikel accuses him of cowardice and treachery, Alfred bridles but then delivers a sharp blow: "If so, then what are your children?" He knows Barnikel's grown sons have shown little interest in resistance activities, a fact that deeply disappoints the old Dane but validates Alfred's assessment.

1086: The Domesday Visit

In April 1086, Alfred travels to the Windsor hamlet where he was born to ensure his land claims are properly recorded in the Domesday survey. He still holds several strips of land there inherited from his father, for which he pays rent while his brother works them.

The visit proves disturbing. Alfred finds his brother has descended into serfdom, accepting labor service instead of paying rent. "Times were hard; there was not enough work for the smithy and too many mouths to feed," his brother explains sullenly. "What difference does it make?"

To Alfred, it makes all the difference. It means his brother "gave up." When Alfred sees his sister-in-law's look suggesting he should give them his unused land, he feels "a sudden rage" characterized by "meanness, or a deep instinct for survival, or a fear of contamination, or just impatience." Though an inner voice reminds him he too might have starved without Barnikel's help, Alfred counters: "When my chance came, I took it." He tells his brother with disgust: "I hope our father cannot see you now."

Through a clerk's confusion, Alfred is incorrectly recorded in the Domesday Book in the vague category of "villanus" (villein) rather than as a free man paying rent. "It did not seem important, at the time."

1087: The Final Crisis

In October 1087, following William the Conqueror's death, a conspiracy forms to support Robert (William's son) against the new King Rufus. When a small group of men visit Barnikel seeking weapons, the old Dane declares: "I can provide what you need. I have it all under lock and key." He secretly sends for Osric to retrieve the arms from the Tower.

Alfred is drawn into the plan. He provides Osric with keys to the cellar grilles and coordinates the timing. But through a spy's information, Ralph Silversleeves deduces the conspiracy and figures out the arms must be hidden in the Tower itself.

On the night of the operation, as a great fire rages through London, Alfred plays his final role in the drama. Though the exact details aren't specified, he's presumably involved in the coordination that leads to the successful retrieval of the weapons. When Ralph confronts Osric at the riverbank, it's Barnikel who arrives to kill Ralph with his great battle-axe—but this happens because Henri Silversleeves sent a false message to Barnikel (pretending to be from Hilda) telling him to kill Ralph at the Tower.

1087-1097: Later Life and Legacy

Alfred survives the events of 1087 and takes in Osric's young son (also named Alfred, born 1087) after Osric disappears. The boy lives with Alfred's family through at least 1097.

Barnikel dies a month after the great fire from a massive stroke at Billingsgate Wharf. The expected rebellion in 1088 is crushed. Alfred apparently returns to legitimate work as master armourer, having emerged from the dangerous years intact.

Though Alfred himself likely dies around 1087 or shortly after (he would have been about 35), his legacy continues through the young Alfred he sheltered, carrying forward both his name and his craft.

Relationships

Barnikel of Billingsgate

The relationship with Barnikel is the defining connection of Alfred's life. The huge Danish merchant saves fourteen-year-old Alfred from starvation, appreciating the boy's refusal to give up despite desperate circumstances. Barnikel gives Alfred both immediate care (food, work) and long-term opportunity (the armoury apprenticeship).

Alfred never forgets this debt. When Barnikel asks him to undertake dangerous illegal work, Alfred agrees without hesitation: "Of course." For over a decade, he continues manufacturing weapons for Barnikel's resistance activities despite growing fear for his family's safety. "I still owe it to him," Alfred tells himself.

Their relationship combines genuine affection with patron-client obligation. Alfred eats weekly at Barnikel's house and becomes "almost part of the family." Yet there's also tension as Alfred grows more cautious with age while Barnikel remains committed to resistance. When Alfred finally balks in 1083, Barnikel calls him a coward and traitor, and Alfred retorts by pointing out Barnikel's own sons won't join the cause.

Despite these strains, Alfred remains loyal to the end, participating in the final 1087 operation to retrieve weapons from the Tower. When Barnikel dies a month after the great fire, Alfred honors their bond by taking in Osric's son—extending to another generation the cycle of rescue and opportunity that Barnikel began.

Osric

Alfred's relationship with Osric mirrors the help he received from Barnikel. Around 1078, Alfred befriends the young mutilated serf working at the Tower, recognizing both his misery and his potential. Alfred and his wife feed Osric weekly, with Alfred's wife becoming "a mother to the fellow."

Alfred repeatedly tries to buy Osric's freedom from Ralph Silversleeves, though Ralph always blocks these attempts. "I'm sorry," Alfred tells Osric. "There's nothing I can do." Nevertheless, Alfred continues supporting him.

When Osric falls in love with Dorkes, Alfred's wife intervenes decisively, promising to care for Dorkes's crippled mother so the couple can be together. Later, Alfred and his wife take in Dorkes's mother and eventually Osric's young son.

The relationship also has a practical dimension. Alfred recognizes Osric's skills and recruits him for increasingly important tasks in Barnikel's operations—first creating the hollowed-out wagon, then the audacious Tower hiding place. "He wants revenge," Alfred tells Barnikel, understanding what motivates the young man.

When Osric disappears after the events of 1087, Alfred honors their friendship by raising Osric's son, just as Barnikel raised him.

His Wife

Alfred's wife, the jolly daughter of a butcher, is described as comfortable, loving, and commanding presence in their household of six children. She presides over the noisy home "with all the ease and confidence of a woman who has a loving husband and exactly one over the number of children she had always hoped for."

She's perceptive and proactive about her husband's concerns. When she notices Osric's distress, she investigates and orchestrates his relationship with Dorkes by promising to care for the girl's crippled mother. "We'll look after your mother... We'll see she doesn't starve, and if Ralph allows, we'll take her in to live in our house."

However, the relationship has limits. Alfred keeps his illegal arms manufacturing secret from her until she discovers him by accident in 1083. Her reaction is severe: she demands he stop helping Barnikel immediately and forces removal of the hidden weapons, threatening: "If not, I go." Alfred discovers his wife is "implacable" on this point. The ultimatum succeeds—Alfred finally sets limits on his dangerous activities, though he doesn't entirely abandon Barnikel.

The Master Armourer

The master armourer recognizes Alfred's extraordinary talent despite his youth and webbed fingers. When he catches Alfred deliberately sabotaging work to avoid apprentice jealousy, rather than punishing him, the master appreciates his cunning: "Work hard, Alfred, and we'll see."

From that point, the master secretly trains Alfred in advanced techniques normally reserved for senior apprentices, working late into the night by lamplight. He teaches Alfred the mysteries of chainmail construction, case-hardening, and the master armourer's art of fitting a coat of mail like a tailor fitting cloth.

The master also draws Alfred into Barnikel's conspiracy, revealing the secret work one evening and giving Alfred the chance to refuse. When Alfred agrees to continue, they work side by side for years, the master treating the former boy as an equal partner in their dangerous enterprise.

Historical Context

Alfred lives through the Norman Conquest and its immediate aftermath, experiencing the transformation from Anglo-Danish London to Norman-dominated England. His personal trajectory from starving vagrant to master craftsman parallels London's own development from a primarily Anglo-Saxon city to a Norman-controlled center of power.

The year 1066, when Alfred arrives in London, represents England's last moment as an independent Anglo-Saxon kingdom. The appearance of Halley's Comet in January, Harold's coronation and death at Hastings in October, and William's Christmas coronation all occur during Alfred's desperate first year. The city he enters is still predominantly Anglo-Saxon in character; by the time he's established, it's under firm Norman control.

Alfred's trade as an armourer places him at the intersection of military and political power. The Defence Guild that employs him represents old English civic authority, while his work increasingly serves Norman masters like Mandeville. This dual position makes him valuable to Barnikel's resistance—he has access to weapons and inside knowledge of Norman defenses.

The secret manufacture of weapons reflects the continuing English resistance to Norman rule throughout the 1070s and early 1080s. Alfred participates in arming the rebellion of Hereward the Wake and subsequent Danish attempts to reclaim England. His growing doubt about these efforts—"most Englishmen have accepted William now"—reflects the historical reality that by the mid-1080s, active resistance had largely failed and English adaptation to Norman rule was becoming the norm.

The construction of the Tower of London, where Alfred makes grilles and where the secret arms cache is hidden, symbolizes the Norman transformation of England. The great stone keep represents William's permanent military control over London. Alfred's work both serves this Norman power (making legitimate Tower fixtures) and subverts it (hiding weapons inside for future rebellion).

The Domesday survey of 1086 represents William's thoroughgoing administrative control of England. The bureaucratic error that records Alfred as a "villein" rather than a free man paying rent illustrates the Normans' confusion with English customary practices. That it "did not seem important at the time" proves ironic—such records became permanent, legal facts affecting property rights and social status for generations.

Alfred's life also reflects the economic opportunity available in towns versus the countryside. His desperate decision to leave his village for London, despite having a secure trade, shows how urban centers offered greater prospects—but also greater risks. His success validates his choice, while his brother's descent into serfdom shows the alternative fate.

Themes and Significance

Loyalty and Debt

Alfred's story explores the power and burden of personal obligation. His unwavering loyalty to Barnikel stems from being rescued from starvation as a boy. This debt drives him to risk his life, family, and livelihood for years. Yet the narrative questions whether such absolute loyalty is wise. By 1083, Alfred's continued service has created an arsenal that threatens his entire family. His wife's ultimatum forces him to balance competing loyalties—to benefactor versus to family.

The text suggests that while gratitude and honor are admirable, blind loyalty without limits is dangerous. Alfred's eventual resistance to Barnikel's demands (or at least his wife's forcing that boundary) represents necessary growth. Yet he never entirely abandons his old friend, suggesting enduring loyalty has value even when tempered by caution.

Class Mobility and Merit

Alfred exemplifies the medieval city as a place of opportunity for the talented and determined. Born a free peasant's son with a hereditary disfigurement (webbed fingers), he arrives in London with nothing. Through skill, courage, and fortunate patronage, he rises to master craftsman, property owner, and respected citizen.

His trajectory contrasts sharply with his brother's descent into serfdom. When Alfred visits in 1086, his angry response—"What difference does it make? "—to his brother's accepting serfdom reveals his belief that giving up freedom indicates moral failure. Yet the text complicates this: Alfred had the luck of Barnikel's rescue and the city's opportunities, while his brother struggled in the countryside.

The Domesday error that records Alfred as a villein rather than free also shows how precarious status could be. One clerk's mistake could erase the distinction Alfred considers fundamental to his identity.

The Cost of Resistance

Through Alfred's secret arms manufacturing, the text explores the price of political resistance under occupation. For over a decade, Alfred risks torture and death to support English/Danish efforts against Norman rule. He endangers not only himself but also his master, his family, and his friends like Osric.

Yet the rebellions fail repeatedly. By 1083, Alfred himself concludes the cause is lost: "most Englishmen have accepted William now." His growing doubt raises questions about when resistance becomes futile, whether continued struggle is courage or stubbornness, and what leaders like Barnikel owe to followers when success seems impossible.

The final ironic twist—that the 1087 operation succeeds but Henri Silversleeves manipulates it to his own ends—suggests that even successful resistance can be co-opted by the powerful for their own purposes.

Generational Continuity

Alfred's life demonstrates how aid and opportunity should be passed forward. Barnikel saves starving Alfred; successful Alfred helps suffering Osric; Alfred then raises Osric's son. This chain of rescue and elevation spans three generations, with each recipient paying forward the assistance received.

The text emphasizes this pattern by having Osric name his son Alfred in honor of the great English king—but that boy's actual savior is Alfred the armourer, creating a layered echo of names and roles. The continuity suggests that individual acts of generosity can have lasting effects beyond immediate recipients.

Craftsmanship as Power

Alfred's story celebrates skilled craftsmanship as a form of agency and power. His abilities with the hammer give him value to both Norman authorities and English resisters. The detailed descriptions of chainmail construction, the secret training sessions, and the ingenious hiding places all emphasize how craft knowledge creates opportunities and options.

The webbed fingers that initially seem a disability prove irrelevant to his success—"skill is what matters," as his father taught him. The master armourer's recognition of Alfred's talent changes his life. Later, Alfred recognizes the same potential in Osric's woodworking. The text suggests that in a world of physical violence and political oppression, mastery of a craft provides a measure of independence and respect.

The Pragmatism of Survival

Alfred's arc from idealistic teenage resistance to middle-aged pragmatism reflects the broader English accommodation to Norman rule. His evolution mirrors London itself—initial resistance, gradual adaptation, eventual acceptance of new realities.

His bitter response to his brother's serfdom—"when my chance came, I took it"—reveals both his pride in survival and a harder edge of judgment toward those who didn't thrive. The text doesn't entirely endorse Alfred's growing conservatism (his anger at his brother seems excessive), but neither does it condemn his prioritizing family safety over political resistance. It presents the tension without resolving it, suggesting the moral complexity of surviving conquest and occupation.
