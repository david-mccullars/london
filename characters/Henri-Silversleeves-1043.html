Overview

Henri Silversleeves is the eldest son of the Norman merchant Silversleeves and heir to the family wine business in London. Born around 1043, Henri grows up in the cosmopolitan world of London's foreign merchant community during the final years of Saxon rule and the Norman Conquest. While lacking his father's deep strategic vision, Henri excels as a masterful tactician in business, brilliant at improvising swift solutions to commercial problems. His marriage to Hilda, daughter of the Saxon merchant Leofric, represents his father's calculated attempt to consolidate power and eliminate business rivals, though the union proves emotionally hollow for both parties.

Physical Description

Henri bears the distinctive Silversleeves family features: dark hair, a large long nose characteristic of his Norman lineage, and a generally sombre appearance. Like his father and brother, his clean-shaven face follows Norman fashion, in contrast to the bearded Saxons and Danes of London.

Character and Personality

Henri is intelligent, calculating, and emotionally cold. His defining characteristic is his obsessive devotion to the Silversleeves fortune, which he regards "like a fortress of which he was the constable, and which... he would never surrender." He is competent and hard-working in business matters, understanding thoroughly "the details of making, shipping and storing wine" as well as knowing his customers well.

However, Henri's calculating nature extends beyond business into his personal relationships. He is capable of casual cruelty and deception, as demonstrated when he claims credit for his father-in-law's gift of magnificent hawks to King William without hesitation or remorse. This incident, particularly his smile at Hilda afterwards, reveals a fundamental coldness that kills whatever affection might have existed in their marriage. He treats his wife with superficial politeness and attention while maintaining complete emotional detachment, eventually becoming openly unfaithful to her.

Henri displays contempt for his younger brother Ralph, whom he views as stupid and a threat to the family fortune. He discourages finding Ralph a wife, reasoning cynically: "Then he'll have heirs to inherit... I look after the family money. And I intend to outlive him." This reveals both his ruthlessness and his long-term strategic thinking about preserving the Silversleeves wealth.

Life and Events

1066: The Conquest Year

At age twenty-three, Henri lives with his father and brother Ralph in the family's impressive stone hall below St Paul's Cathedral. He regularly plays chess with his father, who uses these games to teach him business principles. Though Henri lacks his father's deep strategic sense, he proves himself "a masterful tactician, brilliant in improvising sudden solutions."

During this pivotal year, his father arranges Henri's marriage to Hilda, daughter of the Saxon merchant Leofric. This marriage is part of his father's elaborate scheme to destroy their business rival Becket of Caen and to control Leofric's debts. Silversleeves offers to take over all of Leofric's debts to both Barnikel and Becket, with the sole condition that Leofric cease doing business with Becket and import his wines exclusively through the Silversleeves family. The marriage is fixed for the following summer, 1067.

1067-1078: Building a Life in Norman London

Henri marries Hilda and by 1078 has given her two children – a boy and a girl. The marriage achieves its business objectives perfectly: when King William confiscates estates in Kent, Leofric loses Bocton as feared, but the Silversleeves family rescues him, ensuring his loyalty. This allows Hilda's father to build a solid fortune for her brother Edward, free of his former debts.

Henri proves himself capable of running the wine business, especially as his father increasingly spends time at an estate near Hatfield. He also navigates the politics of the new Norman regime skillfully. Both he and Hilda attend King William at Westminster during Whitsun court sessions. When Hilda presents herself well, speaking French prettily (which Henri has taught her), the king uses them as an example: "here is a young Norman with an English wife proving that the two can live contentedly together."

However, the marriage is emotionally bankrupt. The defining moment comes when Hilda brings magnificent hawks from her father as a gift for King William. Henri immediately claims credit himself: "I searched far and wide, sire," he says without hesitation, then smiles at his horrified wife. This betrayal kills whatever affection might have existed between them. Hilda later reflects that "she might have forgiven him if he had not smiled at her."

By June 1078, their estrangement is complete. Henri begins going out at night, indicating he might not return – making his infidelity unmistakable to Hilda. She responds by spending nights at her father's house.

1087: The Death of William the Conqueror

By 1087, Henri has at least one more son, a youngest child who plays chess with him. The marriage to Hilda continues in name only, with Henri going out openly at night while Hilda seeks companionship elsewhere. When King William dies that year, Henri and his family must navigate the uncertain transition to the reign of William Rufus.

Later Life (1097 and beyond)

Henri continues as head of the family wine business and eventually has a grandson, Pentecost Silversleeves, through one of his sons. By 1170, when Pentecost is twenty-five, Henri would be approximately 127 years old, suggesting he died sometime between 1097 and 1145.

Relationships

Silversleeves (father)

Henri is his father's chosen heir and confidant in business matters. His father teaches him through chess games, explaining complex principles of trade, debt, and power. While the elder Silversleeves recognizes that Henri lacks his own deep strategic vision, he values his son's tactical brilliance and mastery of detail. Their relationship is primarily intellectual and business-focused, with his father grooming him to run the wine trade while maintaining equal inheritance for both sons.

Ralph Silversleeves (brother)

Henri views his younger brother with contempt, seeing him as stupid and incompetent. Despite this, he has learned to be outwardly polite to Ralph, likely following their father's insistence on family unity. Privately, however, Henri resents that Ralph will inherit half the family fortune and actively works to prevent Ralph from having heirs. When Ralph announces his engagement to Gertha, a German merchant's daughter, Henri is "astonished" – presumably dismayed at the prospect of Ralph producing heirs.

Hilda (wife)

Henri's relationship with Hilda begins as a purely transactional marriage arranged by his father for business purposes. He treats her with superficial courtesy and teaches her to speak French well enough to impress the king. However, his heart is "entirely cold" toward her. The marriage dies completely when he claims credit for her father's gift to the king, smiling at her as he does so. This casual cruelty reveals his fundamental incapacity for genuine emotional connection. By 1078, he is openly unfaithful, going out at night without pretense. Hilda comes to feel only "a sense of duty for Henri. Nothing more."

Historical Context

Henri Silversleeves lives through one of the most transformative periods in English history – the Norman Conquest and its aftermath. As a member of London's Norman merchant community, he occupies a privileged position in the new order established by William the Conqueror.

The Norman merchants in London, particularly those from Caen and Rouen, had long enjoyed commercial privileges in the city. They controlled the western wharf at the mouth of the Walbrook, dealing primarily in the lucrative wine trade with France. After 1066, their position becomes even stronger as fellow French-speakers now control the government and aristocracy.

Henri represents the consolidation of Norman commercial power in post-Conquest London. His marriage to Hilda symbolizes the pragmatic accommodation between Norman and Saxon that William encouraged – at least on the surface. Yet beneath this facade of integration, Henri's treatment of his English wife reflects the fundamental inequality of the Conquest: Saxons like Hilda and her family survive by adapting to Norman ways and accepting subordinate positions in what had been their own city.

Henri also benefits from the broader changes William brings to English church and society. His father takes holy orders and founds St Lawrence Silversleeves church, demonstrating the family's wealth and establishing their prominence for generations. Henri inherits not just a business but an established position in London's new Norman-dominated elite.

Themes and Significance

Henri Silversleeves embodies several key themes in the novel's exploration of London's history:

The Cold Heart of Commerce: Henri represents business stripped of human feeling. His obsession with the Silversleeves fortune as an inviolable fortress reveals how commercial success can hollow out the soul. Unlike his father, whose schemes at least spring from deep emotional wounds and old grudges, Henri's coldness seems innate – a second-generation emptiness that needs no origin story.

The Limitations of Integration: The marriage between Henri and Hilda is meant to demonstrate Norman-Saxon harmony, and the king himself celebrates them as proof that "the two can live contentedly together." But this public success masks private misery. Their union produces children and commercial advantage but no genuine connection, suggesting that the Conquest's wounds cannot be healed by mere proximity or intermarriage.

The Corruption of Tactical Brilliance: Henri's ability to improvise swift solutions and master complex details makes him an effective businessman. But these same qualities, without his father's deeper strategic vision or any moral compass, produce only clever cruelty. His instant decision to claim credit for the hawks demonstrates how tactical brilliance can serve petty selfishness as readily as grand schemes.

Inheritance and Degeneration: Henri is his father's heir but not his equal. Where Silversleeves senior orchestrates elaborate schemes driven by ancient hatreds and wounded pride, Henri simply manages what he inherits. He preserves the family fortune but adds little to it. His treatment of Ralph and determination to prevent his brother from having heirs suggests a family beginning to turn in on itself, more concerned with preserving than building.
