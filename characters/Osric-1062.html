Overview

Osric is a young English labourer with a mutilated nose who works on the construction of the Tower of London under brutal Norman rule. Despite his cruel treatment by overseer Ralph Silversleeves, Osric possesses genuine carpentry talent and an ingenious mind. His secret involvement with Barnikel and Alfred the armourer in smuggling weapons for English resistance makes him a quiet participant in the underground opposition to the Norman Conquest.

Physical Description

Osric is a small fellow, only sixteen years old at the start of the narrative but appearing much older due to his hard life. His short legs are bandy, his fingers stubby, and his solemn eyes are set in a head that is too big for his body. His most distinctive and tragic feature is his disfigurement: his nose has been slit as punishment for setting a snare that injured a Norman knight, leaving a "sad little reddish-blue mess" in the midst of his solemn face. He breathes through his mouth as a result of this mutilation.

Character and Personality

Despite his brutal treatment and mutilation, Osric possesses remarkable resilience and resourcefulness. He is naturally drawn to craftsmanship, coming from a family that always supplied their village with skilled workers. His talent for carpentry is genuine - he creates work so precise that any professional carpenter would be proud to call it their own.

Osric is deeply patient and cunning when necessary. His revenge on Ralph by defecating on him from the Tower garderobe shows both his capacity for satisfaction through small victories and his courage in taking risks. He harbors an understandable hatred for all Normans due to his mutilation and enslavement, yet he maintains enough perspective to form genuine friendships with those who show him kindness.

As a father, Osric is tender and hopeful, dreaming that his son might have a better life than his own, perhaps even becoming the carpenter that he himself was prevented from being. His loyalty to Barnikel and Alfred demonstrates his capacity for devotion and his willingness to risk his life for the resistance cause.

Life and Events

1062-1078: Background and Arrival at the Tower

Osric comes from a village in western England, near the ancient settlement of Sarum. His family were good craftsmen, but amongst hundreds of peasant families on a Norman magnate's vast landholdings, they were of no special significance. The great magnate would never have known of Osric's existence except for his foolish act of setting a snare to trip the horse of one of the magnate's knights, who consequently broke his arm.

Osric might have expected death for this offense, but King William, still hoping to ingratiate himself with his English subjects, told his followers to show clemency. Instead of execution, they only slit young Osric's nose - a permanent mark of his crime and a constant source of pain and humiliation.

The magnate sent the boy to the manor of Chelsea, upstream from London. A year later, his steward sold Osric to Geoffrey de Mandeville. Now uncertain whether he was a serf or a slave, Osric knew one thing for certain: if he gave any trouble, Ralph Silversleeves would cut off his ears.

1078: The Tower Construction

At sixteen, Osric is assigned to work on the construction of the great Tower of London. The work is brutal - hauling carts of rubble and stone up the mound until his small back is almost breaking, working until his face is red from heat and exertion, his mouth and eyes full of dust. His stubby hands, once raw, become covered with calluses.

Despite the drudgery, Osric finds himself fascinated by the carpenters on the building site. Coming from a craftsman family, he instinctively gravitates toward them. Whenever he has a spare moment, he watches them work, and the carpenters, sensing his ability, let him wander among them and sometimes show him the tricks of their trade.

1078: The Failed Carpenter's Dream

After three weeks of practice on ends of wood provided by a kindly craftsman, Osric produces something to be proud of: a simple joint of two pieces of wood, so perfectly planed and neatly fitted that any carpenter would be happy to call it their own work. He presents this to Ralph Silversleeves with the hope of being transferred to work with the carpenters.

Ralph is thoughtful at first, recognizing that the work is remarkable and that a skilled craftsman would please Mandeville. At that moment, Osric is about to get his heart's desire - but then makes a fatal mistake. Hoping to help his cause, Osric eagerly mentions: "My older brother is a fine craftsman. I'm sure I could be one too."

This strikes a nerve in Ralph, who can never hope to equal his own clever older brother Henri. In a moment of cruel identification, Ralph delivers his verdict: "Your brother is a carpenter, Osric. But you are only a beast of burden, and so, my little friend, you shall remain." Then, for no obvious reason, he flicks his whip across the boy's solemn face.

1078: Descent into the Tower

Ralph's cruelty intensifies. He assigns Osric to dig the Tower's well, sending the small labourer down forty feet into the earth with pick and shovel. Then, worse still, he assigns Osric to dig the drainage tunnel - a low, narrow space where Osric must crouch for hours, working like a mole in the ground with his back continually aching.

When Bishop Gundulf visits the site, Osric makes a desperate attempt to appeal to the bishop directly, showing him his carpentry work. The bishop seems inclined to help, but Ralph intervenes furiously, claiming (falsely in Norman French, which Osric does not understand) that the boy is lazy and incompetent. Gundulf, already having given Ralph enough trouble that day about design changes, decides not to press the matter. Osric perceives that the last hope in his young life has been extinguished.

Ralph throws him bodily back into the tunnel with the promise: "This Tower will be your life, Osric, and it will be your death, because I shall make you work building it until you die."

1078-1081: Resistance Work

Alfred the armourer witnesses this exchange and picks up Osric's fallen woodwork sample. That night, following a long conversation with the young serf, Alfred tells Barnikel: "I think I've found the little fellow we need." When asked what Osric wants, Alfred grins: "He wants revenge."

Osric begins secretly working for Barnikel and Alfred, using his carpentry skills to convert a huge wagon so that arms can be concealed within it. Rather than creating a secret compartment under the cart (which would be the first place searchers would look), Osric hollows out the solid beams of the wagon's frame itself, preserving their outside appearance while creating cavities where swords, spearheads and arrowheads can be snugly lodged. The work is so ingenious that Barnikel exclaims with delight: "The cart itself is built of arms!"

1081: Meeting Dorkes

In his twentieth year, Osric becomes aware of Dorkes, a sixteen-year-old girl living in the laborers' quarters. She is small with long dark hair parted in the middle, white skin, and small red lips. Her father had been the cook, but after his death two years earlier, she and her ailing mother have survived on the charity of others.

Osric notices that she, like him, goes down to the Thames bank at dawn and dusk to wash herself. Though aware of her, he does not initially pay her much attention. His life has settled into a bearable rhythm - the daily drudgery of labor is balanced by his fascination with the Tower's construction and his occasional clandestine work for the resistance.

1081-1083: Life with Dorkes

Despite his status and circumstances, Osric's situation improves when Alfred's wife intervenes. She approaches Dorkes and offers to care for her mother, explaining that Alfred has tried to buy Osric out of serfdom and may yet succeed. She promises: "We'll look after your mother. Even Ralph doesn't want a pair of useless hands. We'll see she doesn't starve, and if Ralph allows, we'll take her in to live in our house."

A few nights later, Dorkes approaches Osric by the brazier's glow. They make what private space they can for themselves in the lodgings and begin living together. There is no marriage ceremony or official sanctification - in those conditions, none is expected. They are simply known as each other's man and woman.

A year after Dorkes's mother is taken into the armourer's house, Dorkes quietly tells Osric she is going to have a child. Their first child is a healthy daughter who brings Osric great joy. Thanks to Alfred and his family, she never wants for food or clothing. A second pregnancy results in a stillborn daughter.

1083: The Revenge

Osric's desire for revenge against Ralph culminates in a carefully planned and rather scatological act of defiance. The day before his planned revenge, he visits Alfred's family and eats prodigiously - four helpings of stew, three tankards of ale, twice as much as a normally ravenous apprentice. When Alfred asks if he is building up his strength for something, Osric replies cryptically: "Yes. I need all the food I can get tonight."

The next morning, Ralph discovers his whip lying on the ground near the Tower wall - the same whip that had mysteriously vanished two days before. It appears to be a trap to return it, but Ralph cannot resist. As he bends down to pick it up, Osric, who has been waiting for nearly an hour in a garderobe above, releases a carefully timed and voluminous discharge that lands precisely on the overseer's head.

Ralph races into the Tower, screaming with rage and searching for the culprit, but finds nothing. Osric has hidden himself by pressing his legs against the walls inside the great fireplace, sitting ten feet up in the shadows. Ralph, desperate to clean himself before the masons arrive and make him the laughing stock of London, races out into the morning mist toward the city.

Days later, Alfred's wife approaches Dorkes and offers the bargain that will change her life: the family will care for Dorkes's mother and, in so far as possible, look after any children if Osric dies. Dorkes accepts, and she and Osric begin their life together.

1085: The Secret Chamber

In the spring of 1085, with tension rising over an expected Danish invasion, King William orders a confiscation of all weapons in London. Ralph Silversleeves is assigned to collect arms from house to house and store them in the Tower cellar. This creates a crisis for Barnikel and Alfred, who have been stockpiling illegal weapons in the armoury.

Barnikel remembers Osric's ingenuity from the wagon job and asks the little carpenter for ideas. Osric surveys the Tower and proposes an audacious solution: create a secret chamber within the massive cellar walls, accessible through the drainage tunnel from the riverbank.

For a week, Osric works through the night. Using keys provided by Alfred, he enters the empty Tower after dark and carefully loosens stones in the cellar wall to create a space he can crawl into. He then cuts into the softer rubble behind, creating a chamber about five feet by eight feet - just high enough for him to stand. Each night, he drags sacks of rubble to the well and drops them in, then replaces the stones with shallow mortar that he hopes will not be noticeable in the darkness.

His final task is to prepare the escape route through the drain. He descends through the grille in the cellar floor and works his way down the fifty-yard passage to the riverbank outlet. At low tide, with only a few rats for company, he carefully prises loose the outer grille and refixes it with thin mortar so that accurate hammer blows will allow him to push it open from either side.

1085: Storing the Arms

Three days later, under close armed guard, carts transport arms from various armourers to the Tower. When they come to Alfred, he is not ready, irritating the guards who must return later. Finally, at the very end of the day, Alfred loads an even larger quantity than expected onto the carts.

The guards, accompanied by Alfred himself, rush to the Tower. Many men are needed to carry the heavy loads up into the keep and down the spiral staircase into the cellar. When Alfred curtly calls Osric to help, nobody takes any notice. Even Ralph, watching the arms go into the Tower, is not suspicious.

Nor does anyone notice when Osric vanishes at the end of the day.

All night Osric labours in the locked cellar. Alfred has cleverly wrapped each illegal weapon in a second cloth within the legitimate arms, so even after the illegal weapons are removed, the same number of bundles appears to remain. One by one, Osric extracts the swords, spearheads and other arms and takes them to the hiding place. Two hours before dawn, he has stacked everything in the chamber and replaced the stones, fixing them with mortar.

He is just halfway down the great western cellar, heading for the drain to make his escape, when he hears the heavy oak door creak open behind him. Ralph has been too excited to sleep and has come at dawn to survey his work. Finding Osric apparently asleep on the floor, Ralph searches him suspiciously but finds nothing (Osric has already thrown the grille key into the well).

Concluding that the fellow must have been accidentally locked in, Ralph makes a rare joke: "Well, Osric, this makes you the very first prisoner of the Tower." Then he lets him out.

Later, Barnikel murmurs with satisfaction: "The arms are in the one place in London where no one would ever think of looking for them. And thanks to that drain, we can get them whenever we want."

1087: Birth and Betrayal

In the spring of 1087, Dorkes becomes pregnant for the third time. Osric is certain it will be a son - "Maybe," he tells Dorkes, "if he has better luck than me, he'll be a carpenter." When she asks what he will call the boy, Osric replies: "I'll give him the name of our greatest English king. I'll call him Alfred."

In early autumn, following King William's death and the accession of Rufus, political tensions rise again. A group of men approach Barnikel seeking arms for a conspiracy, and the Dane agrees to supply them from the Tower cache. The operation is set for the night after Dorkes gives birth.

In the early afternoon, the child is born - a son, just as Osric predicted. Standing outside the lodgings, gazing over the Tower into the sky, the squat little fellow is unable to speak for several minutes, overcome with joy.

Later that day, in his daze of happiness, Osric forgets caution. Seeing Barnikel and Alfred together in the East Cheap, he rushes up to them exclaiming: "Oh sir, oh Alfred. I have such news. I have a son!" The two men, who have forgotten Osric's family concerns in their own crisis, gather round laughing and hug the little fellow.

Unknown to all three, they are being watched. Ralph's fiancée Gertha witnesses this meeting, and that evening innocently mentions it to Ralph, describing "the man who makes armour" and "a little man with a round head. No nose."

This casual remark gives Ralph the final piece of the puzzle: Barnikel the friend of Alfred, Alfred the friend of Osric, Osric found in the Tower cellars, Alfred who made the locks. Ralph suddenly understands: "The devils. I know what they've done. I know where the arms are hidden! The Tower itself!"

1087: The Night of Fire

That night, as a great fire spreads through London, Osric works alone at the Tower. Barnikel, being watched, has stayed in his house. Osric rows a boat to the drain entrance and crawls up the dark tunnel to the cellar, where he systematically empties the secret chamber and ferries the weapons down the passage to the boat.

The job is easier than expected, taking only two hours. But when he emerges from the tunnel, he receives a shock: blown by the wind, the fire has developed a huge and terrible life, creating a ring of flames around the Tower slopes. The roaring, crackling inferno lights the night sky.

Ignoring the flames, Osric dives back into the tunnel to complete the loading. More than half the weapons are stowed when he emerges again - only to find Ralph Silversleeves standing before him with a sword.

Ralph, having delayed at the fire to help organize bucket brigades, has finally reached the Tower. Finding no guard (the man has gone to watch the fire), he descended into the cellar, discovered the open drain, and followed the tunnel to the riverbank.

"You are arrested, Osric, in the name of the king," Ralph announces calmly, pointing his sword at Osric's midriff. "You thought you could deceive me with your friends, didn't you? But soon, perhaps in there," he jerks his head towards the Tower, "you are going to tell me all about it."

1087: Liberation

In desperation, Osric scrambles into the boat and snatches a spear. He faces Ralph with his weapon, but the Norman is not afraid. Ralph lets Osric advance while retreating up the bank, drawing him away from the boat full of arms. The flickering red firelight gives Ralph all the advantage - the spear gleams visibly while Osric blinks at the glare.

When Osric lunges, Ralph easily cuts off the spearhead with a single sword blow, leaving Osric with nothing but the shaft. "Well, little man," Ralph says softly, "are you going to kill me with that stick?"

Osric's large round face is woebegone, his eyes desperate and serious, an open pathetic smudge where his nose should have been. Uselessly, yet unable to give up, he takes another pace forward, jabbing with his broken weapon.

Ralph grins and raises his sword - but at that moment, Osric gasps in amazement. Behind Ralph, blocking out even the Tower, a huge figure arises surrounded by a great halo of fire: Barnikel the Dane, his great red beard and blazing eyes like some avenging Viking god. The Dane swings his mighty double-handed battle-axe through the flame-filled sky and smites down upon Ralph's head, smashing the skull and cleaving the Norman's torso down to the bottom of his ribcage.

1087: Aftermath and Departure

It is Osric's idea to bury Ralph's body in the secret chamber where the weapons had been stored. Dragging the corpse up the passageway wrapped in oiled cloths, they carry it to the chamber and place it inside. Osric carefully seals up the wall again, locks and refixes the grilles behind them, leaving no trace. It pleases him to think of the Norman locked in there for eternity.

Soon afterward, Osric guides the boat into the stream toward the place where other hands will disperse the weapons. It is his last recorded act in London.

1097: The Legacy

Ten years after the great fire, Hilda reflects on what has become of the participants in that fateful night. "Osric had gone," she notes, "though she sometimes saw his little son, who lived with Alfred and his family now."

Osric's son, named Alfred after the great English king and raised by Alfred the armourer and his family, represents the hope that Osric had cherished - that his child might have a better life than his own, perhaps even becoming the carpenter that circumstances denied the father.

Relationships

Ralph Silversleeves

Osric's relationship with Ralph is defined by brutal oppression and mutual hatred. Ralph's cruelty is not random but calculated - he identifies with Osric's position as an inferior brother and takes pleasure in denying the serf the same craft advancement that Ralph himself was denied. Ralph promises: "I'll never let you go," and declares: "In a way, Osric, I think I almost love you." This twisted affection is that of a torturer for his living object of cruelty.

Osric's revenge through the garderobe incident and his final confrontation with Ralph at the riverbank show both his cunning patience and his desperate courage. Though Osric lacks the power to defeat Ralph directly, he never surrenders his human dignity or his capacity for resistance.

Alfred the Armourer

Alfred becomes Osric's patron and protector, recognizing in the young labourer both genuine talent and a kindred spirit who suffered injustice. Alfred tries repeatedly to buy Osric's freedom from serfdom, though Ralph always contrives to stop him. Alfred and his wife effectively adopt Osric into their family, ensuring that Osric and later Dorkes never want for food, caring for Dorkes's mother, and ultimately raising Osric's son after his departure.

Their relationship is one of craftsman to apprentice, friend to friend, and conspirator to conspirator. Alfred trusts Osric with his life in the dangerous weapons-smuggling operations.

Barnikel

The old Danish merchant sees in Osric a valuable ally - someone with both the skill and the motivation to strike back at Norman rule. Barnikel values Osric's ingenuity and rewards him with inclusion in the resistance work that gives Osric's suffering some larger meaning. Their final collaboration - the removal of the arms and the killing of Ralph - cements a bond forged in shared opposition to conquest and oppression.

Dorkes

Osric's relationship with Dorkes develops slowly from mutual awareness at the washing place to a quiet domestic partnership. Though their union has no official sanction, it provides Osric with family life and hope for the future. Dorkes gives him three children (two daughters, one of whom is stillborn, and the longed-for son) and represents the possibility of continuity beyond his own difficult life. His joy at his son's birth is one of the purest moments in his story.

Historical Context

Osric's story unfolds during the consolidation of Norman power in England following the Conquest of 1066. His mutilation - the slitting of his nose - reflects the brutal punishments meted out to English resisters, though King William's policy of "clemency" (mutilation rather than execution) shows the Conqueror's attempt to manage his new kingdom with calculated severity rather than wholesale slaughter.

The construction of the Tower of London, where Osric labours, represents the physical manifestation of Norman domination. The massive stone keep, with its walls twenty-six feet thick at the base, is explicitly designed to overawe the Londoners with the message: "King William is your master." That much of the Tower is built by forced English labour, using English blood mixed into mortar, symbolizes how the English people are literally being incorporated into the structures of their own subjugation.

The underground resistance network that Osric joins reflects the continuing English hope, especially among the Anglo-Danish community, that Norman rule might be overthrown. The expected Danish invasion of 1085 represents the last realistic chance for this to occur. When King Canute's fleet fails to sail, the resistance's hope effectively dies, though small acts of defiance continue.

The fire of 1087 that destroys much of London, including the Saxon St Paul's cathedral, symbolically marks the end of one era and the beginning of another. The rebuilding in the Norman style - the massive stone cathedral that will dominate the skyline just as the Tower dominates the river - represents the permanence of the Conquest.

Themes and Significance

The Dignity of Labour

Despite his status as a serf and his mutilation, Osric possesses genuine skill and intelligence. His carpentry work is objectively excellent, recognized by both the craftsmen on site and by Ralph himself. The tragedy is not that Osric lacks talent, but that the social order prevents him from exercising it. His story illustrates how conquest and rigid social hierarchy waste human potential.

Ingenious Resistance

Osric's contributions to the resistance are not matters of military valor but of clever planning and patient execution. His design for the weapon-smuggling wagon and his creation of the secret chamber in the Tower walls show how the seemingly powerless can find ways to resist through intelligence and careful planning. His final ironic triumph - burying Ralph's body in the very chamber built to hide weapons from Norman eyes - represents the ultimate reversal of the power dynamic.

The Cost of Conquest

Osric's slit nose is a permanent mark of the Conquest's brutality, a disfigurement he must live with every day. Yet his story also shows the subtler costs - the lost opportunities, the wasted talent, the thwarted dreams. His hope that his son might become the carpenter he could not be represents the multi-generational nature of conquest's impact.

Small Victories

In a world where he cannot achieve freedom or advancement, Osric finds satisfaction in small acts of revenge and subversion. The garderobe incident, the successful smuggling operations, the creation of the secret chamber - these are the victories available to the powerless. They do not change the fundamental situation, but they preserve dignity and agency.

Hope and Continuity

Despite all he suffers, Osric maintains hope for the future, embodied in his children and especially his son. His decision to name the boy Alfred after "our greatest English king" shows his determination to pass on English identity and values even under Norman domination. That Alfred the armourer's family raises the boy after Osric's departure suggests that the networks of care and resistance will continue into the next generation.

The Tower as Symbol

Osric's entire life is entangled with the Tower - Ralph tells him "This Tower will be your life, Osric, and it will be your death." Yet Osric turns this prophecy on its head. The Tower becomes not just his prison but his workplace, his refuge (hiding in the fireplace), his resistance headquarters (the secret chamber), and ultimately Ralph's tomb. The massive Norman fortress meant to symbolize English subjugation becomes, through Osric's actions, a site of English resistance and Norman defeat.
