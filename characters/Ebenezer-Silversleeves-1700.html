Overview

Ebenezer Silversleeves is the hereditary keeper of the Clink, a small debtors' prison in Southwark. A cunning and opportunistic figure, he has perfected the art of systematically extracting money from imprisoned gentlemen who fall into his care. He serves as best man at the marriage of Jack Meredith and Lady St James, which takes place within the prison walls.

Physical Description

Ebenezer is introduced through his most remarkable feature: an enormous nose that precedes him through doorways. Behind this "huge protuberance" follow two mournful eyes, and a wig so dingy "it looked as if it had been used to clean the floor." He has a stooping posture and overall presents a shabby appearance befitting his station. Despite his shabby daily appearance, he owns a magnificent coat that once belonged to a deceased inmate, which he wears for special occasions such as weddings.

Character and Personality

Ebenezer embodies the calculating opportunist who has turned his hereditary position into a profitable enterprise. He is unfailingly polite and "serviceable" to his gentlemen prisoners, genuinely meaning it when he says he is "at your service" – because wealthy prisoners are his livelihood. His business philosophy is captured in his advice to his children: "Give me a gentleman whom his creditors have fleeced, and I'll show you how to skin him."

He operates with systematic efficiency, carefully gauging each prisoner's resources and then methodically extracting value through a predictable progression: first selling better accommodations, then personal effects like watches and rings, then sending confederates to remove items from the prisoner's home, then selling their fine clothes piece by piece, until finally the prisoner ends up back in a dark cell subsisting on bread and water.

Despite this predatory approach to his charges, he maintains a cordial and helpful demeanor throughout. He is also flexible and practical in his business dealings – for a fee, he is "always happy to allow his gentlemen a brief exeat, as long as they promised to return." This accommodation allows Jack Meredith to leave the prison temporarily to attend the fateful meeting at Vauxhall Gardens that leads to the duel with Lord St James.

Life and Events

1750-1751: The Meredith Affair

When Captain Jack Meredith is imprisoned for debt at the Clink, Ebenezer immediately assesses his new charge as "just the kind of prisoner he liked" – a gentleman with resources to be exploited. Despite Meredith's claims of having no money, Ebenezer quickly spots a theatre token for Covent Garden and takes it "in a trice," declaring he could get a few pounds for it.

For five guineas from Lady St James, Ebenezer grants Meredith a temporary leave from the prison, allowing the captain to attend Vauxhall Gardens and arrange the circumstances that lead to the duel with Lord St James. Meredith returns to the Clink as promised, where Lady St James later visits him in secret.

When Lady St James decides to marry Meredith to secure her position, Ebenezer plays a crucial role in facilitating the Fleet marriage within the prison walls. He serves as best man, wearing for the occasion the magnificent coat that had belonged to a former inmate "since deceased." An elderly clergyman from the Fleet performs the ceremony, making it legally valid despite the unusual venue.

Relationships

Jack Meredith

Ebenezer views Meredith as an ideal prisoner – a gentleman fallen on hard times with connections and resources to exploit. He provides Meredith with the Clink's best room (for a price) and essential services, including meals, wine, and reading material. He also facilitates Meredith's temporary release and ultimately serves as best man at his wedding, demonstrating both his flexibility as a businessman and his willingness to accommodate unusual requests from paying customers.

Lady St James

Though their interaction is limited, Ebenezer proves useful to Lady St James's schemes, accepting her five guineas to allow Meredith's temporary release and later participating in the prison wedding. He asks no questions about the unusual arrangements, maintaining the discretion expected of a man in his position.

Historical Context

Ebenezer represents the corrupt but accepted practices of Georgian England's prison system. In 1750, debtors' prisons like the Clink, Fleet, and Marshalsea operated on a system where basic bread and water were provided, but anything beyond that – better rooms, decent food, temporary release – came at a price paid to the keeper.

His family's history stretches back centuries in such roles. The text notes that "before Ebenezer, his father, and his father before him, had exercised their shabby authority over the little prison." Even earlier, when the family lived in Rochester, they had been "petty clerks or gaolers, ever since the days when Geoffrey Chaucer had encountered Silversleeves at the assizes four centuries before." This suggests a connection to the medieval Pentecost Silversleeves and the Silversleeves family line that has persisted through the centuries in similar positions of minor authority.

The Clink itself was an ancient institution, originally the medieval prison of the bishops of Winchester who ruled over the Liberty of the Clink and the brothels of Bankside. By Ebenezer's time, it had been reduced to a small debtors' prison used when the larger Fleet and Marshalsea were full.

The practice of Fleet marriages, which Ebenezer facilitates by hosting one in the Clink, was a peculiar feature of Georgian London. Clergymen imprisoned for debt in the Fleet prison performed legal marriages for fees, even setting up shops outside the prison to tout for customers. These marriages were legally valid, though they existed in a gray area of ecclesiastical authority, occurring "not half a mile from the Bishop of London's great cathedral" but "quite unhindered by the Church authorities."

Themes and Significance

Ebenezer represents the institutionalized exploitation of the vulnerable in Georgian society. While the debtor himself is helpless, imprisoned for inability to pay, the keeper profits systematically from their misfortune. His hereditary position and family history of similar roles suggest how such exploitation becomes embedded in social structures, passed down through generations as an accepted way of life.

His character also illustrates the complex moral ambiguities of the period. He is not portrayed as villainous but rather as a businessman operating within the accepted norms of his society. His politeness and genuine serviceability to his "gentlemen" exist alongside his systematic fleecing of them. This duality captures the Georgian era's acceptance of practices that would later be seen as unconscionable.

The Silversleeves family's long history in such positions – stretching back to the days of Chaucer – demonstrates the persistence of certain family patterns across the centuries, one of the novel's recurring themes. Where the medieval Silversleeves were ambitious climbers seeking power through positions near authority, their descendant Ebenezer has settled into a more modest but similarly parasitic niche in society's margins.
