Overview

Hilda is the daughter of Leofric, a prosperous Saxon merchant, whose life becomes entangled in the political and cultural upheaval of the Norman Conquest. Originally betrothed to Hrothgar Barnikel's son as part of a strategic family alliance, she is instead married off to Henri Silversleeves when her father's financial troubles and the conquest threaten the family estate. Her marriage becomes a loveless arrangement that achieves its practical objective of preserving her family's fortunes, while she finds solace in a chaste and later romantic relationship with Barnikel himself. Her involvement in a conspiracy against Norman rule reveals her quiet defiance and loyalty to those she loves.

Physical Description

Hilda is described as a young woman at age thirteen in 1066:

  • Broad, unworried forehead
  • Fair hair, somewhat thin
  • Pale blue eyes with a calm innocence
  • Breasts had just formed at thirteen
  • Well-shaped calves
  • Somewhat thick in the ankle
  • Simple woollen smock and leggings bound with leather thongs

Character and Personality

Hilda demonstrates remarkable resilience and pragmatism in navigating the difficult circumstances created by the Norman Conquest. She possesses a quiet strength that allows her to accept her fate without bitterness, understanding from the outset that her marriage to Henri serves a larger purpose for her family. She is dutiful and obedient to her father, offering to do "whatever you wish, Father, if you are in need of help" when he asks for her assistance.

Despite her compliance, Hilda is not passive. She shows genuine affection and loyalty to those she cares about, particularly Barnikel, whom she comes to love despite the familial rift. Her involvement in warning Barnikel about Ralph Silversleeves's plans reveals her capacity for defiance and her willingness to take risks for those she loves.

Over time, Hilda mellows from her disappointments, finding contentment in small pleasures like her walks with Barnikel and the security she has provided for her family. By the end of her story, she has achieved a kind of resigned peace with her circumstances, no longer even angry at her manipulative husband.

Life and Events

1066: Betrothal and Marriage

In January 1066, at age thirteen, Hilda is betrothed to Barnikel's eighteen-year-old son. The marriage is eagerly anticipated by both families—Barnikel is fond of the girl and looks forward to having her at his family table, while the match would strengthen the bond between the Saxon and Danish merchant families.

Her father Leofric, however, faces mounting financial troubles. He owes money to both Barnikel and Becket of Caen, and his business is on a knife's edge. When Silversleeves offers to take over all of Leofric's debts in exchange for Hilda's hand in marriage to his son Henri, with no dowry required, Leofric finds himself torn between his word to Barnikel and his family's financial survival.

The situation resolves when, after King Harold's defeat at Hastings, Barnikel publicly calls Leofric a "traitor" for supporting London's surrender to William the Conqueror. In his rage, Barnikel declares that his son will "marry no traitor's child." Leofric then accepts Silversleeves's offer, and Hilda's betrothal to Henri is announced. The marriage takes place in summer 1067.

1067-1078: Married Life

Hilda's marriage achieves its intended purpose. When William confiscates estates in Kent, including the family's ancestral Bocton estate, Silversleeves comes to the rescue. Her father is freed from his debts and able to build a solid fortune for her brother Edward.

Hilda finds that she has never loved her husband, which she considers fortunate since it saves her from disappointment. Henri is clever and hard-working, a master of swift business dealings, but "his heart was entirely cold." He despises his brother Ralph and is devoted only to the Silversleeves fortune, "like a fortress of which he was the constable, and which she knew he would never surrender."

Despite the coldness of her marriage, Hilda achieves a respectable position. She and Henri attend court at Westminster, where King William praises her as proof that Normans and English can live contentedly together. She speaks French prettily and is well-received at court.

A defining moment occurs when her father sends magnificent falcons as a gift for the king, giving them to Hilda to present. When the king asks where such fine hawks came from, Henri claims credit in front of her: "I searched far and wide, sire." This betrayal, particularly his smile afterward, causes "something to die" within her.

1078-1087: Relationship with Barnikel

After Barnikel's son marries someone else and Barnikel's wife dies, Hilda and the old Dane gradually develop a friendship. They had been "shy of each other at first, both regretting the family rift," but once Barnikel's son was married, they felt less awkward. Learning where she takes her evening walks, Barnikel begins to meet her regularly by St Bride's church, and together they walk westwards towards St Clement Danes.

For years, this remains a chaste relationship. Once, about 1082, when she appears tired and sad, Barnikel asks if Henri mistreats her and declares, "I would take you away from him." She gently discourages such declarations: "I may not see you if you say such things."

Their relationship deepens when Hilda learns from Ralph about his secret mission to intercept arms smuggling. Guessing that Barnikel must be involved in resistance activities, she warns him. When she encounters a cart on the road to Hatfield while Ralph and his men search it, it is revealed to be Hilda herself, carrying wine for Silversleeves's estate. Her ruse allows Barnikel's actual arms shipment to pass safely. This marks her active participation in anti-Norman resistance.

In autumn 1087, Hilda's life reaches a crisis point. Ralph's fiancée Gertha inadvertently reveals information that leads Ralph to deduce that arms are hidden in the Tower itself. In a moment of anger and frustration over the Bayeux Tapestry project (which reminds her of her lost family estate), Hilda encounters Ralph and learns his plans. She warns Barnikel again.

That evening, sitting with Barnikel in his hall, Hilda feels a wave of tenderness for the old warrior who refuses to give up despite his age and circumstances. She kisses him, saying "Do" when he warns what might follow. They make love—Hilda "experiencing for the first time the slow and delicate caresses of an older man and brought gently and lovingly to ardour, she found a warmth that was infinitely touching." They stay together until the early hours.

The Great Fire and Its Aftermath

The night after Hilda's encounter with Barnikel, as she runs to warn him that Ralph has gone to the Tower, she is grabbed by mysterious hands near the London Stone and held for two hours. She is released near the Walbrook only to find half of London burning. She never learns that it was Henri's men who seized her, or that Henri sent a message in her name to Barnikel, deliberately setting up a confrontation between Barnikel and Ralph.

A month after the great fire of 1087, Barnikel suffers a massive stroke at Billingsgate Wharf and dies. Hilda is "glad of that"—better that he died then than living to make "a fool of himself" in the failed rebellion of 1088.

1097: The Truth Revealed

Ten years later, Hilda lives quietly in the stone hall near St Paul's. Old Silversleeves has died, and she and Henri have inherited his considerable fortune. Their children are grown, and the couple has evolved "a habit of politely ignoring each other by which they could tolerate the other's presence quite comfortably."

One evening, irritated with Henri, she snaps at him. He calmly reveals that he has known for years about her relationship with Barnikel: "You were Barnikel's lover. And that you helped him commit treason." He had her followed for years because "you are my wife."

Henri then reveals his role in Ralph's death. When Hilda ran to warn Barnikel, Henri's men stopped her, but Henri then sent his own messenger in her name to tell Barnikel that Ralph was going to the Tower. "Either Ralph would arrest your lover or your lover would kill Ralph. Either way, a neat move." Ralph's death ensured that the family fortune would not be divided with Ralph's potential heirs.

Hilda accuses him of being "the devil," but Henri coolly points out that if Ralph had married and had heirs, "your own children's inheritance would have been cut in half." He adds that while he may have orchestrated events, "I committed no crime. Which is more, my dear, than I can say for you."

Hilda walks out to the old jetty at Aldwych where she used to meet Barnikel. As she sits and gazes at the Thames, thinking of her rich children and the passing years, she realizes to her astonishment "that she was not even angry any more." She concludes: "That, she now saw, was for her personally the meaning of the Norman Conquest."

Relationships

Leofric (Father)

Hilda has a dutiful relationship with her father. When he asks for her help in 1066, she responds with complete obedience: "I will do whatever you wish, Father, if you are in need of help." Though her father later confesses, "I'm sorry I made you marry Henri," she replies honestly, "No you're not," though she smiles as she says it. She understands his pragmatism and bears him no ill will. Her marriage achieves its purpose: securing her father's freedom from debt and allowing him to build a fortune for her brother.

Henri Silversleeves (Husband)

Hilda's marriage to Henri is loveless but functional. She sees him clearly from the beginning: clever, hard-working, devoted to the Silversleeves fortune, with "a heart entirely cold." A crucial moment occurs when Henri claims credit for her father's gift of falcons to the king, then smiles at her. "Perhaps, she considered afterwards, she might have forgiven him if he had not smiled at her."

Over the years, they develop a polite indifference. By 1097, they have "evolved a habit of politely ignoring each other by which they could tolerate the other's presence quite comfortably." When Henri finally reveals that he has known about her affair and her treasonous activities, and that he deliberately engineered Ralph's death, she initially calls him "the devil" but ultimately feels no anger. Their marriage represents the cold pragmatism of the Norman settlement.

Hrothgar Barnikel (Lover)

Hilda's relationship with Barnikel is the emotional center of her adult life. Initially, she was to marry his son, and Barnikel was fond of her as a prospective daughter-in-law. After the family rift and her marriage to Henri, they gradually develop a friendship marked by "chaste rendezvous by the banks of the Thames."

For years, Barnikel maintains the role of "an ardent suitor who, perhaps, was not quite without hope," which brings "its own particular kind of joy." Hilda, "knowing herself to be imperfectly loved at home," appreciates being valued "by an older, wiser man."

Her feelings deepen when she becomes involved in his resistance activities. In 1087, moved by his courage and warmth, she initiates their physical relationship. "I'd rather have married this older man, even as he is now, with his magnificent courage, and his big, warm heart," she thinks. Their night together represents the only true love and warmth in her life.

When Barnikel dies a month later, she feels relief that he was spared the humiliation of the failed rebellion. Years later, sitting at their old meeting place by the Aldwych, she thinks of him with affection and without regret.

Ralph Silversleeves (Brother-in-law)

Hilda feels a mixture of pity and revulsion toward Ralph. She is "sorry for Ralph" partly because Henri despises him and partly because he remains unmarried and unloved. On several occasions she suggests finding him a wife, but Henri discourages this to prevent Ralph from having heirs.

Ralph inadvertently provides Hilda with the information she needs to warn Barnikel, confiding in her because he "liked Hilda" and wanted to impress her with his "important mission." When she encounters him on the road to Hatfield, she calls him "idiot" and makes as if to strike him with a whip, publicly humiliating him.

Historical Context

Hilda's life perfectly encapsulates the experience of the Anglo-Saxon upper middle class during the Norman Conquest and its aftermath. Born just before the Conquest, she comes of age in a world where Saxon merchants must make accommodations with Norman power to survive.

The breaking of her betrothal and her marriage to Henri Silversleeves represents the disruption of traditional Anglo-Danish alliances and the formation of new Anglo-Norman arrangements. Her family loses its ancestral estate at Bocton (confiscated by the Normans), but through her marriage, they secure financial stability and even prosperity.

Her involvement in resistance activities reflects the ongoing resentment of Norman rule among the English population, while her ultimate resignation represents the gradual acceptance and assimilation that characterized the decades after 1066. The story of her participation in smuggling arms, her romantic relationship with a Danish resistance figure, and her husband's manipulation of these circumstances for his own advantage provides a personal view of the larger political and social transformation of England.

The Bayeux Tapestry, which she is forced to help create, serves as a powerful symbol of her subjugation—being made to embroider the Norman version of events that cost her family their estate and her people their freedom.

Themes and Significance

Adaptation and Survival

Hilda embodies the Saxon middle class's adaptation to Norman rule. Her marriage to Henri, though loveless, preserves her family's position. She learns to speak French, attends court, and presents an acceptable face to the conquerors while secretly harboring English sympathies and even engaging in resistance activities. Her ultimate acceptance of her circumstances—"she was not even angry any more"—represents the gradual Norman-English synthesis that would define medieval England.

Love and Duty

Hilda's life is defined by the tension between duty and desire. She dutifully accepts marriage to Henri to save her father from ruin, but finds love with Barnikel. This love remains chaste for years out of duty to her marriage and position, only becoming physical when she has matured enough to make that choice deliberately. Even her involvement in conspiracy is motivated more by personal loyalty to Barnikel than by abstract political conviction.

The Personal Cost of Conquest

Through Hilda, we see the intimate human costs of political upheaval. She loses her intended marriage, her family's ancestral estate, her country's independence, and the possibility of a loving marriage. Yet she also gains security, status, and eventually wealth. Her story illustrates how conquest transforms not just kingdoms but individual lives, relationships, and identities.

Manipulation and Agency

Despite the constraints placed upon her, Hilda exercises agency in subtle but important ways. She assists in arms smuggling, warns Barnikel of danger, and chooses when to consummate their relationship. However, she discovers that her husband has been manipulating her for years, even using her actions to eliminate his brother. This reveals the limited nature of women's agency in this period—even her acts of defiance serve her husband's interests.

Resignation and Peace

Hilda's final realization—that she is no longer angry—represents a hard-won peace. After decades of living with disappointment, manipulation, and loss, she has achieved a kind of philosophical acceptance. This is neither defeat nor triumph, but a realistic accommodation to circumstances beyond her control. In this sense, she personifies England itself under Norman rule: conquered, changed, but ultimately at peace with its new identity.
